Head of Office of Government Ethics resigns

By Christina Prignano Globe staff  July 06, 2017

The director of the federal Office of Government Ethics announced his resignation on Thursday, following months of clashing with the Trump administration over financial disclosure rules and other ethics issues.

Walter Shaub announced his resignation in a letter posted to Twitter on Thursday. He did not discuss the reason for his departure in the letter, which thanked his staff and other officials in the executive branch.

But in a statement annoucing he will join Campaign Legal Center as a senior director for ethics, Shaub appeared to criticize the Trump administration.

“In working with the current administration, it has become clear to me that we need improvements to the existing ethics program,” he said in the statement.

The center describes itself as the “home to the nation’s premier election law experts.”

Trump and Shaub’s office have clashed recently. Earlier this month, the president complained the ethics office was causing delays that were making it difficult to fill vacancies in his administration.

Shaub disputed Trump’s assertion.

Advertisement

Shaub was also embroiled in a recent controversy when the Trump administration granted an exemption that allowed White House strategist Stephen Bannon to communicate with editors at Breitbart News, where he was recently an executive. The exemption was granted retroactively, a fact that raised ethics questions.

“There is no such thing as a retroactive waiver,” Shaub said at the time.

Last month, the Trump administration tried to block an effort to dislcose any ethics waivers granted to lobbyists who now work for the federal government. Shaub had asked for copies of the waivers. The White House then asked him to withdraw that request.

In a statement, Senator Elizabeth Warren, a Massachusetts Democrat, called Shaub’s resignation “deeply unnerving.”

“Despite swimming for months in the toxic swamp of ethics problems at every level of the Trump Administration, Walter Shaub has done exactly what a nonpartisan ethics official is supposed to do - put the law first,” she said in the statement.

Danny McDonald contributed to this report. Information from The New York Times and the Associated Press was used in this report.
