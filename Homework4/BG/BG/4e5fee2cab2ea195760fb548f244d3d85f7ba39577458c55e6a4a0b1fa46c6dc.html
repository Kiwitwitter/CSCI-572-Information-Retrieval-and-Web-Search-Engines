Nurses fighting mandatory flu vaccinations sue Brigham and Women’s

By Priyanka Dayal McCluskey Globe Staff  September 26, 2017

The Massachusetts Nurses Association is again suing Brigham and Women’s Hospital to try to block a policy that requires employees to receive flu vaccines.

The Brigham notified 18,000 employees last week about the new policy, which mandates that all workers be vaccinated annually, with exceptions for medical or religious reasons. Employees who go without the flu shot must wear face masks when they’re around patients.

The union, which represents 3,400 nurses at the Brigham, says the rule unfairly and unlawfully punishes nurses who don’t want to get a flu shot for other reasons. The union filed suit in Suffolk Superior Court last week, asking a judge to stop the policy, which is set to begin Sunday.

David Schildmeier, a spokesman for the union, said nurses — not their employer — should have the right to decide what goes into their bodies. He said nurses could be fired for declining the flu shot under the new policy.

“This is a strong overreach,” he said. “It’s totally unnecessary.”

This debate has played out at the Brigham before. In 2014, the union sued after the hospital proposed a similar vaccination policy. The suit ultimately went nowhere, because the hospital never enforced the policy.

Hospital officials said 94 percent of the nurses were vaccinated last year, but stricter rules are necessary to stop the spread of flu, a contagious and sometimes deadly virus.

Advertisement

“The hospital is moving forward with implementing this policy because we believe that mandating the vaccine will create the safest-possible environment for our patients, visitors, and staff,” spokeswoman Lori Schroth said in an e-mail. “Influenza can carry significant health risks, especially for someone who has a compromised immune system.”

The Massachusetts Health & Hospital Association, which represents the Brigham and several other hospitals, supports policies that require all health care workers to be vaccinated. Mandatory vaccination policies already are in place at some hospitals,the association said.

On average, 92 percent of hospital workers statewide were vaccinated last flu season, although the figures vary across hospitals, according to the state Department of Public Health. The department “encourages” all health care workers to be vaccinated, unless there is a medical reason not to get the vaccine.

“The flu is a serious illness and vaccination is the best way to protect both the health care providers and their patients,” department spokeswoman Ann Scales said in an e-mail. “Getting a yearly flu vaccination is particularly important for those who work in health care settings because a person can spread the virus to others before they begin feeling sick themselves.”

The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention recommend that almost everyone older than 6 months get a flu vaccine every season. The effectiveness of the vaccine can vary, but the CDC says vaccination typically reduces the risk of illness by 40 to 60 percent.

Schildmeier, of the nurses union, said nurses shouldn’t be required to get a vaccine supported by “inconclusive” scientific evidence. Some Brigham nurses have had reactions to the vaccine and shouldn’t have to get it again, he said.

The union also argues that when thinking about the spread of infections, hospital officials should be more worried about the many patients and families coming through the doors every day than about nurses.

The union’s lawsuit claims the Brigham is violating a state rule that allows health care workers to decline vaccinations.

The hospital said employees have until Dec. 1 to receive their flu shots. Overall, 91 percent of the Brigham’s staff were vaccinated last year.

“This year,” hospital officials said in a memo to employees, “we know that we can do even better.”
