BC 28, CENTRAL MICHIGAN 8

BC runs to easy win over Central Michigan

Matthew J. Lee/Globe Staff

A.J. Dillon celebrates his 15-yard touchdown run in the first quarter.

By Julian Benbow Globe Staff  September 30, 2017

Boston College football exists in a strange place between two completely different worlds.

One week, the Eagles are in Death Valley in front of 81,000 fans holding on for dear life in a tug of war against the Atlantic Coast Conference powerhouse Clemson Tigers trying to keep things from inevitably going off the rails.

The next week, they’re at Alumni Stadium in front of 27,036 scattered fans, keeping their heads above water and their bowl hopes alive by beating up on a Mid-American Conference opponent.

“Those are pretty good sizable emotional roller coasters,” coach Steve Addazio said. “And what’s hidden in there is not only the emotional roller coasters, but there’s a physical toll taken in those games.”

Their 28-8 win over Central Michigan on Saturday helped them rejuvenate — at least for a week.

After taking a gut punch last week at Clemson in the form of a third straight 20-point loss, the Eagles went into their matchup against Central Michigan trying to hold things together while seven starters sat out with injuries. Nursing a right shoulder injury, it wasn’t clear until Thursday if redshirt freshman quarterback Anthony Brown would be able to take the field.

Freshman running back A.J. Dillon rushed for 120 yards on 25 carries with a 15-yard score. Junior running back Jon Hilliman scored a career-high three touchdowns (two rushing, one receiving) while running for 89 yards on 23 carries. Brown completed 14 of 21 passes for 85 yards, including a 3-yard touchdown pass to Hilliman.

“This was kind of like a must-win game for us,” said Hilliman said. “We kind of knew that coming in. So we just knew to keep the morale high and keep the confidence high for certain guys.”

The Eagles had to overcome a shaky start.

On their first drive of the game, after Brown came up a yard short on a keeper on third and 10, Addazio chose to go for it on fourth and 1. Dillon took the inside handoff but got stuffed at the line of scrimmage. The Eagles turned the ball over on downs at their 44.

The Eagles’ defense forced Central Michigan into a three-and-out, but Chippewas punter Jack Sheldon aired out a kick that bounced inside the 5, and gunner Gary Jones kept the ball from going into the end zone, pinning BC at the 1.

Lining up in their end zone, Addazio called a run by Hilliman to give them some breathing room, but Hilliman got taken down by Nate Brisson-Fast for a safety that put Central Michigan up, 2-0.

Eagles punt returner Michael Walker (128 yards on four returns) helped shift the momentum, setting up BC’s offense to work with multiple short fields.

One series after the safety, Walker broke loose for a 61-yard return that set up the Eagles at the 6 with 10:38 left in the first quarter. The very next play, Hilliman punched in a 6-yard touchdown that put the Eagles up, 7-2.

They never trailed again.

An Eagles defense playing without linebackers Connor Strachan and Max Richardson came up with three sacks (one from Harold Landry, one from Zach Allen, and one split between Ty Schwab and Wyatt Ray) and three interceptions (two by Lukas Denis, one by Allen). Short on bodies, Landry had to line up at linebacker for a handful of snaps.

“We’re trying to adapt based on some of the personnel hits that we’ve taken, to be honest with you,” Addazio said. “We’re taking a lot of hits right now.”

The Eagles pushed their record to 2-3. Both of their wins have come against MAC opponents. Their early losses have made their road to a bowl game that much narrower.

“We’re playing some high-level games,” Addazio said. “So at this stage of the game, you’re looking at a lot of teams in the country that are 4-0 and some of that reality is coming into focus again. We haven’t had that. We opened up with a MAC team. That MAC team beat Nebraska, OK, and we were on the road. To think that stuff doesn’t add up, it does. To think that positive confidence doesn’t matter, it does.

“We’re doing this the long hard way right now, which is front-ending some really tough teams. And we’ve still got many tough teams to go. That’s the nature of where we are in this conference and the division and the side that we play on.”

As the Eagles look ahead, they’ll find themselves right back in the pressure cooker of the ACC as they host No. 12 Virginia Tech next Saturday, then travel to No. 17 Louisville.

“We played Clemson toe-to-toe, dropped the rope a little bit,” Hilliman said. “Same thing with Notre Dame. But we know that if we come with that same energy, we can shock this team and no one’s going to be shocked in the locker room if we do it . . . It’s a very doable task. It’s a tall task, but a very doable task.”
