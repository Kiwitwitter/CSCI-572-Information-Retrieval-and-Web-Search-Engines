Here’s how the Senate health care vote went down overnight

Zach Gibson/Getty Images

Senator John McCain left the Senate chamber after voting.

By Christina Prignano Globe staff  July 28, 2017

It’s hard to imagine a more dramatic night unfolding over the fate of the Republican health care bill.

After seven years and countless starts and stops in the GOP’s quest to repeal and replace President Barack Obama’s signature health care legislation, the Senate found itself voting overnight on a measure some Republican members would only vote in favor of it if they received assurances that it would never become law.

Among them was Arizona Senator John McCain, who recently made a dramatic return to Washington to vote in favor of opening debate over the legislation.

McCain, along with Senators Lisa Murkowski of Alaska and Susan Collins of Maine, who opposed the bill from the beginning, ultimately dealt a major blow to not only the GOP’s attempts to repeal Obamacare but to President Trump’s agenda more broadly.

Here’s how it went down overnight:

McCain holds a press conference to denounce the ‘skinny repeal’

Early in the night, McCain took to a podium along with Senators Lindsey Graham and Ron Johnson to slam Senate Marjority Leader Mitch McConnell’s latest proposal: A “skinny” repeal bill that would remove the individual and employer mandates but leave in place much of the rest of the legislation.

McCain wanted assurances from House Speaker Paul Ryan that the “skinny repeal” would never become law, but would instead serve as a vehicle for the House and Senate to hash out a compromise in a conference committee.

McCain gives a thumbs-down

Advertisement

Several of McCain’s Republican colleagues attempted to sway him toward a “yes” vote, including Vice President Mike Pence. But McCain had apparently made up his mind. Asked by reporters around 11:30 whether he knew how he’d vote, McCain replied that he did and told reporters to “wait for the show,” according to the Washington Post.

The Arizona senator approached the floor of the Senate at around 1:30 a.m. and gave an understated thumbs down to signal his “no” vote. Some Democratic Senators broke into scattered applause.

McCain releases a statement on Twitter

About an hour later, McCain released a statement and tweeted about his decision. He said would only support a repeal bill that came with an adequate replacement plan and said the “skinny” repeal didn’t do so. He also signaled that he wasn’t confident in Ryan’s assurances about the bill’s fate in a conference committee.

McCain pleaded for a bipartisan process:

“We must now return to the correct way of legislating and send the bill back to committee, hold hearings, receive input from both sides of aisle, heed the recommendations of nation’s governors, and produce a bill that finally delivers affordable health care for the American people,” he said in the statement.

Trump tweets his displeasure with Republicans

Though he did not single anyone out by name as he has in the past, President Trump tweeted that “3 Republicans and 48 Democrats let the American people down.”

But Trump did not issue any statements or speak further on the issue Friday morning.

McConnell speaks after failed vote

In the early morning hours, McConnell took to the Senate floor after the vote to indicate the Senate would move on. He called the failure “a disapppointment.”

The majority leader thanked Republican members for trying to reach a consensus but suggested Democrats had stymied their efforts.

“So now, Mr. President, it’s time to move on,” McConnell said as he indicated to the Senate president that the body would move on to other matters with no further plans for a new health care vote.
