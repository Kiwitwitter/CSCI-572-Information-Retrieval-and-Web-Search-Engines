HIGH SCHOOL BOYS’ SOCCER

Two soccer powers, two diverging paths

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Tom Petrini Globe Correspondent  September 08, 2017

Much has changed since Masconomet defeated Beverly, 4-0, for last year’s Division 2 North boys’ soccer championship.

Beverly coach Kevin Leahy retired after 17 years at the helm, and third-year Masconomet coach Jared Scarpaci will see the return of just one of the four Cape Ann League all stars that led his team to its second-straight state championship game last year. Masconomet has its sights set on that still elusive state title, while Beverly hopes to build an identity under new coach Mark Devlin.

Born in Ireland, Devlin moved to England four years ago to coach high school soccer. His wife is from Beverly, and the two moved to her hometown late last year. Soon after, the coaching job there opened up, and Devlin took it.

He comes into the program a foreigner, both literally and figuratively, which presents a unique set of opportunities and challenges. He brings a wealth of knowledge from a global hotbed of soccer, but met his players for the first time a few weeks ago.

“The biggest challenge is getting to know what each player is like,” Devlin said about starting fresh.

Heading into his third year, Scarpaci knows most of his players well, especially senior captain Mike Budrewicz. Before either was involved with Masconomet’s varsity, Budrewicz was a freshman in Scarpaci’s history class. The two bonded over a mutual love of the game, and the relationship has only strengthened over the past two years.

“He’s my favorite coach I’ve ever had,” said Budrewicz. “If you believe in him and give him your full 100 percent trust, it’ll work.”

“He’s got a great attitude every day in training,’’ Scarpaci said of his only returning all star. “He leads on the field with the style of how he plays.”

Last year, Nauset outscored opponents, 108-1, over the course of its undefeated state championship run. Budrewicz holds the distinction of netting the only goal that juggernaut let up all year, in the title game yet. Still, Masconomet fell, 2-1.

“It was two really good soccer teams, and for us to be able to [score], we were pretty excited,” said Scarpaci. “Obviously the result wasn’t what we wanted, but we played a really good soccer game and they played a better soccer game, and that’s sports.”

Everyone returning for Masconomet wants to reach that mountaintop. Budrewicz sees it as his responsibility to motivate his younger teammates.

“Last year in the Cape Ann League we went 16-0, and that’s what we’d like to do this year,’’ he said. “But I have to get it through everyone’s head that winning is a mentality, and that we want to get back to that state championship game. I’ve lost in two state championships and that’s really the only motivation I need.”

He feels that lessons learned from those losses will stick.

“Losing is not a good feeling, but you take away that you can always get better,” he said. “Our coach still brings things up about Nauset, what they did against us. We still take things from that game that we can build on in practices this year.”

For Devlin in Beverly, the outlook for his team is a bit murkier. “It’s impossible for me to know,’’ he said, “given I don’t know too much about the competition.”

While Scarpaci has been lucky enough to have healthy teams in his first two seasons, Devlin is entering his first year with three players on crutches.

“I know that we’re probably not as strong as we were last year, basing that on a lot of seniors leaving,” said Devlin. “Our only returning captain moved to California last week, and that was a surprise.”

Devlin chose senior captains who have been with the team the longest, although the primary scoring punch might come from junior Bryce Visnick.

“He’s very quick off the mark and able to run at defenders, quick to his shots as well,” Devlin said of Visnick, who won a club national championship this summer with the New England Futball Club’s 2000 National Premier League team. “More than likely he will be our person that hits the most shots.”

The summer competition helped Visnick’s development, which fits with Devlin’s more European approach to training. He’s focusing less on drilling and more on game experience; a coaching style that has paid dividends for player development overseas.

“[Players] want to play scrimmages and games a lot more, so they’re engaged in that regard,” said Devlin.

His stated goal is to improve each player’s soccer standard, and rather than drilling, he strives to do that by using game situations to teach.

His players will obviously try to win and get back to that D2 North title game, but it’s a transition year for Beverly under Devlin. “My job is to make each of these players better,’’ he said. “That way hopefully anyone who’s returning next year as a senior or even a junior, we’ll be a better side next year.’’
