DAN SHAUGHNESSY

Using David Price in relief is letting him off the hook

jim davis/globe staff file

David Price will pitch in relief as he rejoins the Red Sox.

By Dan Shaughnessy Globe Staff  September 14, 2017

Picked-up pieces while wondering how things will end for the 2017 Indians and Dodgers . . .

■  So now the Red Sox are paying David Price $217 million to be Heath Hembree?

Advertisement

The Sox activated Price Thursday, and manager John Farrell announced that the sour southpaw will work out of the bullpen for the rest of this year. I hate this.

Perhaps it’s rational given Price’s recovery from elbow woes and his potential value as a lefthanded power arm out of the pen. But I still hate it because it’s letting Price off the hook too easily.

As much as it might be dangerous for the Sox, I wanted to see Price get the ball for a playoff start in 2017 (with double-barrel action in the Boston bullpen during the anthem). It’s my thirst for great sports theater.

Historically, Price is a postseason gag artist (nine starts, 0-8, 5.74). He coughed it up in his one playoff start with the Sox last year. This year, he managed to offend all of Red Sox Nation with his bad behavior, and he has never apologized.

I wanted to see him take the ball for a postseason start and either enjoy a John Lackey-like moment of redemption or establish once and for all that he cannot pitch in Boston. Now he gets to slink to the bullpen and get protection from the pressure.

Advertisement

Oh, and let me remind you that Price has pitched exactly once out of the bullpen in the last seven years (2015 playoffs with Toronto).

■  Take heart, Rick Porcello. A lot of good pitchers have registered 20-loss seasons. Luis Tiant was 9-20 for the woeful Indians in 1969.

The Red Sox have not had a 20-game loser since Milt Gaston (13-20) and Jack Russell (9-20) did it in the same year, 1930, when the Sox went 52-102. Bill Monbouquette and Dave Morehead both went 10-18 for the 100-loss Red Sox in 1965.

The amazing thing, of couse, is that Porcello (9-17) is doing this for a first-place team. But he won’t lose 20. The Sox will “rest” him if he gets to 19.

Detroit’s Mike Maroth (9-21 in 2003) is baseball’s only 20-game loser since 1980.

■  Kudos to the New York Post for placing an asterisk next to “Boston” in its daily American League standings. Below the standings, the asterisk denotes, “Caught stealing.’’

When the Sox got caught cheating, how come nobody made the connection to “Damn Yankees”? The name of the devil in the famed Faustian show/movie is “Applegate.’’

One of my favorite readers reminds us, “You can’t hold a whole baseball team responsible for the behavior of a few sick, perverted individuals. For if you do, then shouldn’t we blame the whole major league system . . . ?’’

■  Hate to rub it in, but the New York Football Giants will salute their 2007 Super Bowl champs when they play their home opener in the Meadowlands Monday night against the Lions. Eli Manning and Zak DeOssie are the only players left from that squad. David Tyree currently works for the Giants as director of player development.

■  When it comes to the Yankees, Judge Sonia Sotomayor makes Fanboy Richard Berman look neutral. The Supreme Court justice donned a robe and sat in the “Judge’s Chambers” in right field (named for Aaron Judge) when the Red Sox visited New York last month. She cheered madly for her Yankees.

Sotomayor has two other connections to baseball. Her ruling in 1995 ended the 232-day player strike that killed the 1994 World Series. And in February of 2013, her autobiography was No. 1 on the New York Times nonfiction bestseller list. This is significant to baseball only because the No. 2 book was “Francona,’’ a Fenway party starter co-authored by the former Sox manager and yours truly. Tito is still peeved that the judge denied him No. 1 bestseller status.

■  American League MVP? Don’t sleep on little-known Cleveland slugger Jose Ramirez or Tribe shortstop Francisco Lindor. Houston’s Jose Altuve is the front-runner. Angels star Mike Trout no doubt will be in the top three.

■  Quiz: Longtime NHL defenseman Noel Picard died in Montreal last week at the age of 78. Why is Picard forever part of Boston sports history? Answer below.

■  Primarily owing to bad run support, Chris Sale won zero of his five starts against the Yankees this year. The Red Sox beat the Yankees in only one of Sale’s starts.

Sale (16-7) has had an amazing season. So why was Price’s 17-9 season last year perceived as failure?

■  We lost one of the all-time greats last week when former boxer/state auditor Joe DeNucci died at the age of 78. The Newton legend fought more times and won more fights than any other boxer in the long, great history of the old Boston Garden. At his peak, DeNucci was the world’s fifth-ranked middleweight. He served Massachusetts as auditor for 24 years.

■  What’s up with “Pink Stripes” written on the whiteboard behind Ernie Adams during his fascinating interview on “Do Your Job, Part 2”?

■  There will be a Green Team push to tell us to “be patient” with the 2017-18 Celtics. You know the drill. Eleven new players, a lot of adjustment needed, blah, blah, blah. Not me. I want them to be great right out of the gate.

■  The late, great Steve Belichick clearly did not make the rules regarding media availability for the Navy football team. According to the Washington Post, “The Naval Academy — the rare college football program whose players might one day guard actual state secrets — doesn’t approach the sport as if it’s a fully classified endeavor. It doesn’t traffic in paranoia.’’

■  Maria Sharapova is unusually forthcoming regarding Serena Williams in her new book, “Unstoppable: My Life So Far.’’ Sharapova admits she is obsessed with Williams, whom she has beaten only twice in 21 matches. One of Sharapova’s rare victories over Serena was the 2004 Wimbledon final, of which Sharapova writes, “I think Serena hated me for being the skinny kid who beat her, against all odds, at Wimbledon.’’

■  Longtime “Monday Night Football” producer Don Ohlmeyer died last Sunday at 72. Richard Sandomir’s obituary in the New York Times included a story of Ohlmeyer telling Howard Cosell (through his earpiece) that the Patriots were about to call a pass play involving tight end Russ Francis. Ohlmeyer had discussed the play with Patriots coach Chuck Fairbanks before the game.

Cosell made the prediction, Francis scored on the play, and after the game Cosell said to Ohlmeyer, “How’d you like that call, kid?’’ Ohlmeyer snapped back at Cosell, “Do you think that plastic thing in your ear is a brain?’’

■  UMass’s commitment to FBS football continues to be one of the worst ideas of all time. The Minutemen are 0-3 going into Friday night’s game at Temple. UMass is 10-53 since jumping into big-time college football in 2012. The Minutemen paid Hawaii a guaranteed $400,000 to come to Amherst for a 38-35 win Aug. 26.

■  Twenty-one-year-old George Michael Steinbrenner IV runs Steinbrenner Racing and hopes there is an Indy 500 victory in his future.

■  Cooler Update: The Dodgers were 85-34 when Adrian Gonzalez returned to the team from the disabled list, then went 7-18 over their next 25 games, including an 11-game losing streak. The Cooler was at the center of two of the greatest collapses in baseball history — the 2010 Padres and the 2011 Red Sox. Now this.

■  Quiz answer: Picard was the St. Louis Blues defenseman who lifted his stick to trip Bobby Orr as Orr potted the winning goal in the 1970 Stanley Cup Finals. It was Picard’s trip that resulted in Orr flying through the air after the goal. Check out the statue in front of TD Garden.
