Paul Gray, 85, president who increased diversity at MIT

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Bryan Marquard Globe Staff  September 18, 2017

When Paul Gray spoke in 1980 at his inauguration as the Massachusetts Institute of Technology’s 14th president, he said the school “has a responsibility to itself and to the nation to be open — and to reach out — to the most talented and promising people, regardless of race or sex.”

That was no empty promise, as he showed by the time he stepped down a decade later. During his tenure, the number of female students more than doubled, to about 35 percent, and the number of African-American and Latino students tripled. Increasing diversity “may be the most important thing I did around here,” he would later say in an MIT oral history.

Dr. Gray, a transformative administrator who enrolled at MIT as an electrical engineering student in 1950 and retired in 1997 as chairman of the MIT Corporation, the institute’s governing body, died Monday in his Concord home. He was 85 and had been diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease.

“There may be no single person in modern history who has had such an impact on MIT as Paul Gray,” Jim Champy, an MIT Corporation life member and a graduate of the school, said in a statement. “So much of what we experience at MIT today was begun by Paul.”

The efforts to increase student diversity through recruitment, which Dr. Gray began in the late 1960s as an associate provost, brought lasting change to MIT, but they were far from his only legacy. During his tenure as president, plans were set in place to create the Whitehead Institute for Biomedical Research at MIT, and the five-year Campaign for the Future raised $710 million. While serving as chairman of the MIT Corporation, he was a leader in the seven-year Campaign for MIT, which raised $2.05 billion, far more than its goal of $1.5 billion.

Throughout his career as a professor and administrator, though, his focus remained on students. As associate provost, he championed efforts by Margaret MacVicar — who later became a dean — to found the Undergraduate Research Opportunities Program. That initiative encourages students and faculty to work together in research partnerships.

While Dr. Gray was president, MIT’s faculty reorganized much of the undergraduate program for arts and sciences and the humanities to move “away from a cafeteria approach to a more structured approach,” he told the Globe in 1989. “This means less choice from a student perspective and more commonality from the institution’s perspective. We have become more sure of what students know.”

He also wanted MIT to become more aware of how students felt. “I came in convinced that for many of our students the pressure and pace MIT generated was too great, and that it would be better if there was more time for reflection, contemplation, and informal learning,” he said in the same interview. “I have been pressing for 10 years and I’ve made a few converts, but whether I’ll have any lasting impact is uncertain. . . . This is a place dedicated to achievement.”

With his wife, Priscilla, Dr. Gray cofounded what is now the Priscilla King Gray Public Service Center, which was an outgrowth of her interests in that area, and her desire to enhance MIT’s sense of community.

“Paul Gray led MIT with the clear-eyed pragmatism and uncommon steadiness of a born engineer, and the humility, warmth, and wisdom of an exceptional human being,” L. Rafael Reif, MIT’s president, said in a statement. “He was an indispensable advisor to two MIT presidents who preceded him and all three who have followed him.”

Former MIT president Susan Hockfield recalled in a statement that Dr. Gray was her “first and most essential guide to MIT. With the wisdom gained from a lifetime devoted to the institute, he showed me MIT’s ethos and history.” She added that “his love of the place, of the people, and of our mission shone brightly in all he said and did. A part of me has always and will always see MIT through his eyes.”

An only child, Paul Edward Gray was born in Newark, the son of Kenneth Gray and the former Florence Gilleo. Dr. Gray would later recall that his father, a public utility technician who hadn’t finished high school, was brilliant and inspired his interest in science, electricity, and engineering.

While in elementary school, Dr. Gray began repairing radios and built his own, becoming a ham radio operator. His mother approved of that intellectual direction. “My grandmother decided that chemistry experiments smelled, but the electronic experiments were quiet,” said Dr. Gray’s older daughter, the Rev. Virginia Army of Vernon, Conn.

At MIT, he graduated in 1954 with a bachelor’s degree, in 1955 with a master’s, and in 1960 with a doctorate, all in electrical engineering. Through an MIT friend, he met Priscilla King on a blind date and they married in 1955. Dr. Gray was in ROTC at MIT and served in the Army for two years, but otherwise never left MIT after arriving as a freshman.

Priscilla “was the love of his life,” their daughter said. The Grays opened their home to MIT colleagues and students for decades, and the couple often spent time on campus conversing with students, staff, professors, and administrators. “From the time we were little, Thanksgiving dinner was populated by various students who couldn’t go home because it was so far away,” Virginia said.

“My father modeled for us a kind of inclusivity and embrace of each person you met,” she added, and Dr. Gray was a constant presence for his four children, 12 grandchildren, and a great-granddaughter born last year. “He hiked and biked and swam and sailed with us. He played with us, and he did our homework with us when we needed help. He was an encourager and a supporter: He believed in his kids and his grandchildren.”

Dr. Gray began his teaching career as an instructor and over the years has been the MIT Class of 1922 professor of electrical engineering, associate dean for student affairs, associate provost, dean of the School of Engineering, and chancellor.

“To me, he is the iconic president of MIT because he was made out of pure institute clay, as an undergraduate, graduate, professor, and academic leader,” John Deutch, an institute professor emeritus who formerly was provost of MIT, said in a statement.

In addition to his wife, daughter, grandchildren, and great-granddaughter, Dr. Gray leaves his three other children, Amy Sluyter of Kennebunk, Maine, Andrew of Danbury, Conn., and Louise Huyck of Amherst, N.H.

A memorial service will be held at 1 p.m. Oct. 1 in Hancock United Church of Christ in Lexington. An MIT memorial service will be held at 3 p.m. Nov. 30 in Kresge Auditorium.

Speaking at his 1980 inauguration, Dr. Gray mused that most of his adult life “has centered on the surroundings of this courtyard. I stand here today as no stranger to you, and the institute is a central part of my life.” Nonetheless, daunting challenges lay ahead, and there were changes to be made to improve his professional and intellectual home.

“I feel a little bit like the human cannonball at the circus,” he quipped. “I’m now in ballistic free flight, nearing apogee, but I have faith and confidence that out there somewhere are people with a big net.”
