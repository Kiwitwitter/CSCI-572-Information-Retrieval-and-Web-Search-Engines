AVALANCHE 4, BRUINS 0

Bruins had nothing going in loss to Avalanche

Matthew J. Lee/Globe Staff

Bruins defenseman Charlie McAvoy (73) battled with Avalanche center Colin Wilson in the first period.

By Kevin Paul Dupont Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

The power play sputtered. Tuukka Rask was less than elite. Their boatload of kids, who were a bundle of energy and endless promise on opening night, found out the NHL learning curve can be a funhouse full of frightful twists and turns.

Add it all up, and the Bruins on Monday afternoon dealt themselves a painfully dull 4-0 loss to the Colorado Avalanche at the Garden. They fell behind early, couldn’t generate traction despite repeated power-play chances (now 1 for 9 through two games), and thus extended a winless streak vs. Colorado on home ice that now spans nearly 20 years.

Some holiday. Had Christopher Columbus stumbled across such a mess some 500 years ago, he likely would have cross the Hub of Hockey off his list of “Top 10 Finds.”

“I don’t think we were good in any area early,” said coach Bruce Cassidy, his club falling into an early 2-0 hole, the Avs connecting on two of their first three shots vs. Rask. “It wasn’t like a barrage of chances, either — we gave up some good ones.”

In turn, the Bruins, now 1-1-0 on the 82-game manifest, never mounted much sustained pressure at the other end, where Avs netminder Semyon Varlamov stifled all 29 Boston chances.

Varlamov’s best stop came at 1:08 of the second period when he thwarted David Pastrnak’s stuff attempt at the right post. Had Pastrnak nudged it across, the lead would have been cut in half (2-1), but replay clearly showed the puck never crossed the goal line, no matter how loud the boos and groans of a sellout crowd of 17,565 who interpreted it differently from the in-arena replay.

“I saw it didn’t go in,” said Pastrnak, who attempted six shots for the day, half of them making it to the net. “I thought I got it on [a second attempt] . . . I tried to poke it in, but someone lifted my stick.”

Varlamov faced 16 more shots over the remaining 39 minutes, but was never in much danger of losing his lead.

The knockout punch came early in the third when Rask, roaming far from his net when a free-flying Nail Yakupov broke into the zone, was caught in no-man’s land. The streaking Yakupov took possession and was left wide open, both he and Rask some 25-30 feet from the cage.

“It’s a split-second decision,” explained Rask. “You see you have a chance to get the puck and you go for it — try to make a play and give the puck to your own team. I didn’t do that and that’s all you can really say about it.”

The Avs now have gone 12 straight games on Causeway Street (10-0-1-1) without a loss, dating back to March 30, 1998.

“From the first shift, everything went the wrong way,” said Pastrnak. “But those games happen. The season is long. Obviously, we have to be better . . . but we can’t let this bring us down.”

The Avs squeezed off only five shots in the first 20 minutes. Most games, that would be a very encouraging sign for the Bruins. This wasn’t most games. Two of those shots eluded Rask, leaving the Black and Gold with a 2-0 deficit at the first intermission.

Sven Andrighetto, the former Canadien, sniped in the 1-0 lead at 4:41 on the Avs’ first shot of the afternoon. Afforded an open look in the slot, the Swiss-born winger snapped off a 15-footer that Rask initially blocked with his [left] glove hand, only to have the puck roll over the top of his glove and fall over the goal line.

Ex-Michigan standout J.T. Compher, acquired in the Ryan O’Reilly trade with Buffalo, then finished off a 2-on-1 break with Carl Soderberg, the ex-Bruin, for the 2-0 lead at 13:22. Rookie defenseman Charlie McAvoy, the lone Bruin fronting the play, couldn’t close off the pass and Compher snapped it over Rask’s glove on the short side. A marksman’s shot, but stoppable.

“The second one was more my bad,” said Rask, not pleased with either of the first two goals, but more upset at the second. “I was square to the puck. It ricocheted off my shoulder . . . it was one I should have saved.”

Following the loss, Cassidy noted that he hadn’t been pleased with the club’s two practice days over the weekend. Following Friday’s day off, and the emotional win over Nashville in the opener, tempo and execution were lacking in the workouts.

“It just wasn’t a good effort, from young, old, in between,” said Cassidy, reflecting on the loss. “Clearly I put myself in that category. We’re supposed to be ready to play at home, especially after a couple days off. Things aren’t going to go your way some nights, you’ll fight the puck . . . but not to have the energy to sustain it and get yourself back in the game, it’s disappointing.”
