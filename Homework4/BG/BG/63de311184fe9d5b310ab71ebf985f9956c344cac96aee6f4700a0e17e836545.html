‘Foodie Crawl’ brings taste of the world to East Somerville

By Kathy Shiels Tully Globe Correspondent  September 29, 2017

EAT, DRINK, EAT Loosen your belt. It’s time for Somerville’s annual “Foodie Crawl,” from 6 to 11 p.m. on Tuesday, Sept. 26 (raindate: Sept. 27) in the city’s East Broadway neighborhood, aka the “Gastronomic District.” Last year, over 700 made the edible pilgrimage.

While following a trail to over 20 restaurants, you can taste the cultures from around the world: Ethiopia, Mexico, Haiti, Brazil, Italy, El Salvador, and more. Quaff craft suds at three pop-up beer gardens featuring local Aeronaut Brewery and newcomers Rincon Mexicano and Maya Sol Mexican Grill. Vegetarian? Don’t worry. There’s samplings at each venue. Desserts will be served at select restaurants.

All proceeds benefit East Somerville Main Streets, a volunteer-driven nonprofit committed to developing a safe, vibrant, multicultural community and business district.

Tickets are $25 online, $30 at the door (if still available). Crawlers can add two beer tickets for $12 vs. $7 each bought individually.

SLAVERY’S LEGACY Where better to learn intricacies about the history of slavery than on the estate of the largest slaveholding family in Colonial Massachusetts?

Annette Gordon-Reed, a Harvard Law School professor and Pulitzer Prize-winning historian and author, is the featured speaker at the annual “Giving Voice” benefit in Medford for the Royall House and Slave Quarters, 15 George St.

Advertisement

“History is to a degree an imaginative enterprise; when writing it or reading it, we try to see the subjects in their time and space,” Gordon-Reed writes. “Imagining requires some starting point of connection.”

“The Hemingses of Monticello: An American Family” — her book about four generations of slaves on Thomas Jefferson’s plantation in Charlottesville, Va. — won the Pulitzer Prize for history in 2009. Gordon-Reed recently published “Most Blessed of the Patriarchs: Thomas Jefferson and the Empire of the Imagination,” cowritten with historian Peter S. Onuf.

The benefit takes place from 2:30 to 4:30 p.m. on Sunday, Oct. 1. It will include refreshments and tours.

Tickets are $40 for Royall House members, $50 for nonmembers. Advance tickets can be purchased for $125 by those wishing to participate in a small group discussion with Gordon-Reed from 2 to 3 p.m. before the larger event. For information and tickets, go to: royallhouse.org.

RUN FOR BREAD Lace up to help feed neighbors in need during the Bread of Life’s 25th annual Walk for Bread & 5K Run.

The event takes place from 12 to 3 p.m. at Pine Banks Park in Malden and Melrose on Sunday, Oct. 1. Registration is at 12 and the walk starts 1 p.m. All participants get a free T-shirt, snacks, and drinks. DJ Jack Oliva will provide tunes to run and walk by.

The faith-based ministry serves the communities north of Boston. In 2016, the nonprofit distributed over 1 million free meals, an increase of 2.5 percent over 2015.

Registration costs $25 ($30 day of the event). Runners and walkers also collect pledges. To register, pledge, or become an event sponsor, visit breadoflifemalden.org or call 781-397-0404.

DUAL FIREWORKS Listen. It’s the Cape Ann Symphony kicking off its 66th concert season with not one, but two performances of “Russian Fireworks.”

Music director Yoichi Udagawa will conduct the fiery Tchaikovsky-Stravinsky-Rachmainioff program that also will feature the brilliant pianist, Ya-Fei Chuang.

“Simply extraordinary!” is how Udagawa described the soloist. “She will stun audiences with her talent!”

Pick your venue: Saturday, Sept. 23 at 8 p.m. at the Manchester Essex Regional High School auditorium, 36 Lincoln St., Manchester-by-the-Sea, and Sunday, Sept. 24 at 2 p.m. at Ipswich High School, 134 High St..

Tickets are $43 for adults, $38 for seniors, $15 for students, and $5 for youth. To purchase, go to capeannsymphony.org.

BOO AT THE ZOO Add some creepy crafts to a zoo, plus ghoulish games, costume contests, a haunted maze, and a not-to-miss trick-or-treat trail among the animals, and what do you have?

It’s Stone Zoo’s annual “Boo at the Zoo.” The haunted Halloween event, located at 149 Pond St. in Stoneham, is being held from 11 a.m. to 3 p.m. on Oct. 21 and 22.

Admission to the special event is free with admission: $16.95 for adults, $14.95 for seniors over 62, $11.95 for children 2 to 12. Children under 2 and members are free. Visit zoonewengland.org.
