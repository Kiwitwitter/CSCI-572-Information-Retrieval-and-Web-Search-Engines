This year, a new vision for indie fest Fuzzstival

Peach Ring is among 31 diverse bands slated to perform during Fuzzstival.

By Zoë Madonna Globe Staff  September 27, 2017

CAMBRIDGE — What’s fuzzy about Fuzzstival? It used to be the bands. For its first four years, the festival focused on psychedelia and garage rock, genres that often include layers of guitar distortion. This year, in the festival’s fifth iteration, the fuzziest thing is the boundaries between artists and their styles, with the unifying factor being connections to the Boston area or the Northeast.

Sounds you might hear at the festival, which runs Thursday through Saturday, include the gauzy lo-fi pop of Somerville-based Mini Dresses, the explosive geek-girl rap of Sammus, and the politically prescient punk rock of Kominas. And that’s only on one day of the three-day jamboree, which takes place at various venues in Somerville and Cambridge.

“I think this is the first year that I’ve seen the Fuzzstival lineup be incredibly diverse,” says Ariana Ratner, 24, the high-octane lead singer of noisy punkish threesome Peach Ring. “But I definitely think the Fuzz ethos still holds true. A day of loud, exciting acts.”

The inaugural Fuzzstival was also the first event that organizer Jason Trefts booked as Illegally Blind, which has to date presented and promoted hundreds of shows in the Boston area, usually at smaller venues like Lilypad in Inman Square and ONCE Ballroom in Somerville. On his lunch break from his full-time job as a case manager, Trefts explains that his goal was to reestablish what Fuzzstival was. “It’s less a specific sound and more a reflection of the kind of shows I book all the time,” he says. He estimated that about 26 of the weekend’s 31 bands are new to the festival.

Peach Ring, which formed last year, is one such band. “I’d say that the people that show up to Illegally Blind events are very supportive, they’re very enthusiastic, they’re willing to hear new things,” says Adrienne Arditti, 26, a trained opera singer who plays drums and bass in Peach Ring. “It’s a nice sense of community without feeling like a clique.”

This year’s Fuzzstival is exclusively all-ages. The bands and artists are mostly young, and many of them are unsigned to labels. Most of them center their online presences on Facebook and the artist-driven music-sharing website Bandcamp, which takes a smaller cut of album proceeds than iTunes; such a difference can be vital for musicians paying Boston rents.

According to Lira Mondal, 28, the lead singer of Fuzzstival veterans Mini Dresses, Trefts’s outspokenness about paying artists fairly has made a noticeable difference in the local music community. “He’s been an incredible support for us, ally, mentor,” says Mondal at a Somerville cafe. “He tries to get everyone in the scene no matter what genre they participate in, in equal focus and measure.” Mondal, who identifies as South Asian-American, also expressed her appreciation for the “range of ethnicities and genders and styles” on the bill.

After four years running Illegally Blind as well as stints booking at the Middle East Upstairs and Church of Boston, Trefts’s name is highly regarded among local musicians. He’s been nominated in the best promoter category at the Boston Music Awards for the past three years, and his reputation preceded him when he reached out to Mint Green, another young band that just received a BMA nomination for new artist of the year.

“Illegally Blind is a booking company that I’ve always wanted to play a show for. So when they hit us up for it, it was really cool,” says Mint Green lead singer and songwriter Ronnica, 21, who prefers to use only her first name. She describes the band’s confessional, propulsive music as “indie rock, so they aren’t scared if I say punk.”

Though Trefts’s mission is to showcase local and regional music, he’s not optimistic about its future in Boston in light of the rising cost of living and the shortage of practice and performance spaces. “I think we’re all just gonna move,” he says. “It’s inevitable that in a year or two, most of this [expletive] is done.”

“I feel like my band compatriots in [Boston] work a lot and try to squeeze music in, and it just seems to be very difficult,” says Mondal. “It’s increasingly becoming hard for artists to live here. Everyone’s kind of moving to the outskirts of the city or even out of the state.”

Illegally Blind and all its associated events remain a labor of love for Trefts, who has no ambition to grow the venture. His primary goal, he professes, is to bring people together. “I’m risking thousands of dollars for no benefit to myself other than to put together something I believe in,” he says. “I don’t think it’s super logical to do that, but I don’t worry about it a lot. Maybe at a later day I’ll think it’s crazy.”

FUZZSTIVAL

At various venues in Cambridge and Somerville, Sept. 28-30. Tickets $15 single-day, $30 three-day passes, www.illegallyblindpresents.com
