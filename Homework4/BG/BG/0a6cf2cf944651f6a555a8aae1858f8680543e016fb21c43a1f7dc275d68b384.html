Civil War reenactors are in the line of fire

Craig F. Walker/Globe Staff

Mikal Madyun, 19, of Providence played the part of a wounded Union soldier during a battle during the Civil War Living History Reenactment at Historic Fort Adams in Newport, R.I.

By Hiawatha Bray Globe Staff  September 26, 2017

MARSHFIELD — Paul Brundige is a man of shifting loyalties. During his recent visit to the Marshfield Fair, he suited up in Union blue. But when circumstances demand, he becomes a Reb — part of the 35th Virginia Cavalry.

For 30 years, Brundige, who manages a livestock and pet care store in Norwell, has spent many of his weekends refighting the American Civil War, a pastime that by necessity can entail commemorating the bravery of Confederate soldiers.

Now he and his fellow reenactors are concerned that this commitment to authenticity, at a time when memorials to the Confederacy are being reexamined, could trigger a backlash against their gray uniforms and pose another threat to a hobby that has seen diminished numbers in recent years.

“The people that come here, they’re America enthusiasts,” Brundige said. “They’re not Confederate enthusiasts, they’re not Union enthusiasts . . . they love America. They love their country and they want to know where it came from.”

These days Brundige and his comrades are facing new and unfamiliar foes. Age, for one thing. The baby boomers who revived historical reenactments as a major hobby are aging out and moving on, and there are not enough younger enthusiasts to replace them.

Then there is the growing worry that Civil War reenactments will become the next battleground in the nation’s cultural war. You can’t reenact the Civil War without recreating the Confederate army, an army that fought on behalf of an evil cause — the preservation of slavery. Many Americans are outraged by positive depictions of the Confederacy. Yet for decades, hardly anyone objected when reenactors donned Confederate gray and unfurled the infamous battle flag.

That’s starting to change, said Brundige. “I think people are a lot less tolerant now,” he said. “They can’t say, ‘OK, I don’t like this, I’ll turn the channel, I’ll go to a different place.’ Instead, if I don’t like it, ‘well, you can’t do it.’ I don’t feel that’s the American way.”

These days, there’s a powerful campaign to remove public monuments to Confederate leaders and a resurgent white nationalist movement vowing to preserve them. The two sides met in Charlottesville, Va. in August. Blows were exchanged, blood was spilled, and an antiracism protester was run over and killed, allegedly by a neo-Nazi.

Craig F. Walker/Globe Staff

A Confederate battle flag flew over camp.

Within days of the atrocity, reenactors felt the backlash. In Newton, N.C., reenactors dressed as Confederate soldiers were pepper-sprayed during a Soldiers Reunion parade. Organizers canceled a two-day reenactment at the site of major Civil War battles in Manassas, Va., and reenactment events in Chantilly and Fauquier Va., and York County, S.C., were also cancelled.

“I’ve been involved in reenacting for about 26 years now, and I think I’m more concerned about what’s going on than ever,” said Benny White, who plays a company commander in the 54th Massachusetts Regiment, the first black unit to fight in the Union army.

White said, “I wonder, are we going to be putting ourselves in harm’s way by going to the re-enactments.”

As the seat of the American Revolution, New England is more familiar with war reenactments that feature Redcoats instead of Rebs. But while there were no Civil War battles fought on Massachusetts soil, there were at least a dozen reenactments of the conflict scheduled in the state this year. The staging of those mock battles dates back as far as the Civil War era itself, but it did not become a popular hobby until the 1960s during commemorations of the 100th anniversary of the conflict. Time Magazine estimated that in the mid-1980s as many as 50,000 Americans had become reenactors. It’s a highly decentralized activity conducted by dozens of groups, and there’s no central organization that keeps track of them.

At two recent events, at Marshfield Fair and at Fort Adams Park in Newport, R.I., the talk among reenactors is relentlessly apolitical — at least, when it comes to 21st-century politics. They discuss everything from military tactics and logistics to the correct way to build a bonfire. The politicians most likely to come up in conversation are Abraham Lincoln and Jefferson Davis. They are reluctant to talk about the violence in Charlottesville, except to express revulsion.

Craig F. Walker/Globe Staff

Pete Northrup (center) helped a young reenactor with his cap.

If anyone at the reenactments harbored racist attitudes, they kept it strictly to themselves. That’s not always the case, according to White, who is 73 years old and lives in Hyde Park. “On a couple of occasions I was subjected to the racism and bigotry of a few southern reenactors who still believe in the ‘South will rise again’ attitude,” he said, acknowledging that in the main such incidents were “more the exception rather than the rule.”

White himself occasionally goes to war on the Confederate side. He’s part Cherokee Indian, and thousands of the tribe fought on both sides of that conflict. So White fits right in as a member of the Confederacy’s First Cherokee Mounted Rifle. Still, he has his misgivings about play-acting a rebel and only does it when there aren’t enough volunteers.

“I don’t like to do too much of that,” White said. “I don’t like to be on the Confederate side.”

There are women here, offering medical care, cooking meals, and carrying arms too. Michelle Brady, 49, of Winchendon, arrived at Marshfield Fair in the uniform of a Confederate cavalryman, leading her Tennessee walking horse. Brady got into it a decade ago when one of her sons was bitten by the reenactment bug. The family even owns a working cannon, a “mountain howitzer” as Brady calls it, for when they play roles in a Confederate artillery force.

Craig F. Walker/Globe Staff

Amanda Morgante (left) and her sister, Jennifer, of Woodbury, Conn., prepared for the reenactment.

Michelle Brady’s mother grew up in Germany during World War II, where she was at the mercy first of the Nazis and then of the occupying Americans. Because of her mother’s experiences, Brady has sympathy for the rank-and-file Confederate soldiers who did most of the fighting and dying for the South.

“Most of those kids didn’t know what they were fighting for,” Brady said. “They went off to war because it’s what you did.”

Brady’s husband and son are missing in action, because of football practice. But she brought along her 15-year-old daughter Elyse, portraying a Confederate belle in a splendid blue dress. Other youngsters suited up for the two events. At Marshfield Fair, Daniel Dupre, 18, from Rhode Island, wore the uniform of the US Signal Corps and brandished a sword and single-action Colt revolver. And among the Union “dead” at the Fort Adams Park reenactment lay a smiling Mikal Madyun, a 19-year-old African-American from Providence who’s been at it since he was 14.

“I love history, I love reenacting, so I figured, ‘why not give it a try?’” Madyun said.

But longtime reenactors say there aren’t enough newcomers to replace those retiring from the field. At the Fort Adams Park reenactment, Robert Beebe, a member of the Artillery Company of Newport, fired off blank rounds from a replica Civil War cannon. Beebe said it takes months to learn how to fire the big gun safely, but it’s hard to find young people who want to learn.

Craig F. Walker/Globe Staff

Stephen Feid (left), playing the part of a company commander for the Confederacy, readied his troops for battle.

“There are so many other things you can do to entertain yourself, in this digital age,” Beebe said. “Who wants to go out and relive the past?”

Joseph Pereira, former head of the Liberty Greys, a group of New England Confederate re-enactors, agrees that time and age are taking their toll. “A lot of guys are burned out,” he said.

But Pereira, who is 61 and lives in Fall River, worried that the ongoing culture wars will accelerate the decline of reenactments, with more communities quietly concluding that such events are no longer welcome.

“I think communities and organizations that have in the past embraced the reenactments are going to start pulling back,” he said. “There’s a snowball there. And as that snowball rolls down the hill, sooner or later a lot of people who are innocent in the whole thing are going to be run over.”
