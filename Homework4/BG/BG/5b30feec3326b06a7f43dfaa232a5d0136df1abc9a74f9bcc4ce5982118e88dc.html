Flipping the script on some macho men who made history

Nile Hawver/Nile Scott Shots

A rehearsal of the SpeakEasy Stage production of “Men on Boats,’’ which chronicles an 1869 expedition to chart rivers in the American West, but with a diverse cast in traditionally white male roles.

By Chris Wallenberg Globe correspondent  September 06, 2017

One of the famous refrains from “Hamilton” — “Who lives, who dies, who tells your story” — is a theme that certainly applies to the spirited history saga “Men on Boats,” about real-life 19th-century explorer John Wesley Powell and his hardened group of macho-men adventurers. Indeed, they’d probably be shocked to see who’s telling their tale almost 150 years later.

The play, which begins a month-long run at SpeakEasy Stage Company on Friday, chronicles the trials and tribulations of Powell’s 1869 expedition to chart the Green and Colorado rivers from the Wyoming Territory into the unknown dangers of what came to be called the Grand Canyon. But it flips the script on your standard action-adventure tale thanks to a daring mandate from its playwright, Jaclyn Backhaus. While “Hamilton” cast people of color as the country’s Founding Fathers, Backhaus instructs directors and producers to employ racially diverse actors who are female, trans, or gender-fluid in the roles of Powell and his white male cohorts.

“It’s an incredible, true story of courage, bravery, and the American West. But the play gets turned on its head because it’s not the usual cast of characters playing those roles,” says Mal Malme, who portrays Powell’s taciturn brother, Old Shady. “White cisgendered men in the 1800s could say and do whatever they really wanted. Everybody cast in this play is not that. So how does that change the story? I think it gives it a different lens, a whole different richness. It allows people to think about history differently.”

The gender subversion isn’t all that distinguishes “Men on Boats.” The play prompts director and cast to use inventive modes of stagecraft and theatrical magic to bring to life its dramatic action sequences — from boats barreling down rapids, tumbling over waterfalls, and crashing onto river banks, to characters clambering up canyon walls, facing harrowing encounters with snakes, and narrowly escaping roiling waters. It’s also an antic comedy that employs contemporary vernacular full of self-aware in-jokes and witty colloquial anachronisms.

“The way the characters speak has that Joss Whedon quality — really modern, really fresh, and really cutting,” says the show’s director, Dawn M. Simmons. “If you’re thinking about ‘Buffy’ or ‘Angel,’ there’s something about being in these hyper-kinetic situations in which everything is larger than life and the stakes are really huge, but the language in some of those moments can be incredibly casual and contemporary and accessible.”

Inside a rehearsal hall at the Calderwood Pavilion, Ally Dawson and Alice Kabia are working with Simmons to perfect a choreographed handshake, booty-bump, and exclamation of “Friendship!” between two characters — Hawkins, the crew’s cook and Hall, the mapmaker. They’re working through the sass- and brass-filled moments in which their characters gossip and banter about other members of the expedition, speculate about a crew member who may be filching tobacco, and decide that they “won the lottery” by being paired with each other on what they call the “party boat!”

“We’re pushing the adventure and the physicality,” Simmons says. “We’re hopefully bringing back that sense of play and that sense of imagination you had when you were younger, when you were much freer to go out and turn a stick into a snake, turn it into a sword, turn it into a light saber.”

Among the 10 crew members traveling in four boats, there’s the serious, authoritative geologist and academic Powell, who lost an arm in the Civil War; his dour, war-scarred brother Old Shady; fellow war vet and experienced adventurer Sumner; hunter, trapper, and wannabe expedition leader Dunn; the manic young gun Bradley; and the arch yet excitable Brit, Goodman.

In working with the actors, Simmons says she’s asked them “to play the truth of their bodies and the truth of their gender as they inhabit it. What is paramount is that they’re playing an explorer. What does that mean, and what does that look like?”

Over coffee at a West Village cafe, Backhaus says that she recalls watching actor Mark Rylance and an all-male company of performers transform into women in the Broadway production of Shakespeare’s “Twelfth Night.” “They all looked like they were having so much fun,” she says.

Backhaus, who lives in Queens with her husband and son, knew Powell’s story since her childhood growing up in Phoenix. Her father had lots of books about explorers in their house, including Powell’s published journals.

As she was writing the play, she realized it was compelling to her because the part of a one-armed male explorer from the 1800s “isn’t a role I would normally get to inhabit in any way,” so writing it allowed her to experience vicariously the thrill of Powell and company’s life-and-death endeavor.

Several exploratory readings with friends, mostly women, convinced her she was on the right track. “Those initial workshops helped me kind of unpack and uncover themes about Manifest Destiny, and about who the American Dream usually served, and who suffered at the expense of this conquest, and in what moments does this conquest ideology become completely farcical?”

The conclusion of her experiment? “This is probably more dynamic in the end than if I had cast it with like 10 Hugh Jackmans,” Backhaus says with a laugh. “The humor and the heart really came out of the process. It didn’t become just a lampooning of gender. It became more about examining the nature of the explorer and who is allowed to inhabit what space on stages.”

Still, with cast members spouting dialogue rife with bluster and bravado, the play skewers the swaggering, macho pose of explorers and their quest for immortality. It also contains a prickly critique of Manifest Destiny, the entitlement of European settlers, and the displacement and destruction they unleashed on the native peoples.

“I think there is love in the play for this expedition and these people. There’s great reverence for their story and their journey,” Simmons says. “But there are moments of indictment, too. It’s not a send-up of white men exploring, but it examines how people, before and after these guys, caused a systemic wiping out of history. Those are the places where you find the most biting commentary.”
