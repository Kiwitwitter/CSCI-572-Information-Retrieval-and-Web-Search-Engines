Sometimes, at least with cruise ships, size does matter

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Christopher Muther Globe Staff  September 27, 2016

The numbers are staggering.

Royal Caribbean’s new Harmony of the Seas ship is taller than Mount Rushmore. It has more guest rooms than at the Planet Hollywood Casino in Las Vegas. It’s got more neighborhoods than New York City has boroughs. It’s twice the size of the Washington Monument. It has a slide that’s taller than Buckingham Palace.

It sounds overwhelming and over-the-top, and it absolutely is. With room for 6,700 passengers, plus 2,300 crew members, the ship has a larger population than the town where I grew up. It also has many more culinary options. It’s officially the world’s largest passenger ship.

“Just like the Titanic?” a macabre friend asked.

Let’s ignore that for a moment. I wasn’t worried about hitting an iceberg when I sailed Harmony of the Seas in the Western Mediterranean this summer. I was more worried about finding my cabin or perhaps contracting a Titanic-sized case of norovirus. I found my cabin and never contracted super norovirus. Thank you for your concern.

For those who have already had an opportunity to sail on a massive ship, I’m sure all of this will elicit a shoulder shrug and a stifled yawn. Royal Caribbean already has two other ships in what it calls its Oasis class. In Royal Caribbean speak, Oasis class means “weighs more than 17,000 elephants.” Harmony of the Seas begins sailing out of Ft. Lauderdale in November.

Before I burden you with more facts and figures, let me give you a quick, subjective opinion of the ship: It’s a beast of a resort that happens to be at sea. It’s the kind of 18-deck ship that could allow a multi-generational family to travel together, yet get through a day and never run into each other. You know, the perfect family vacation.

There are kid and teen zones, 23 pools, waterslides, hot tubs, and other aquatic distractions. Explore further and you’ll find a sizeable casino, a shopping mall-like promenade, a boardwalk complete with a carousel and arcade, a park, a fitness center, giant spa, and enough decks to find a chair, slather yourself in sun block and hide away.

I have many fussbudget friends who clutched their metaphorical pearls and gave me an “I would never do that” look when I told them about the trip. I had similar thoughts as I pondered what to pack. I’m still a cruise newbie and find that sometimes cruising can get a bit dull. I once took a cruise where the sole entertainment was a husband and wife singing duo. The highlight of their shtick was when the wife donned a brunette wig and sang Carpenter’s songs all night.

Harmony of the Seas was sensory overload. I didn’t hear a single Carpenter’s song, but I sat in a 1,400-seat theater and watched a slick production of “Grease.” Another night I saw divers perform an intricate show in a high-tech aqua theater, which was like Cirque du Soleil-meets-extreme synchronized swimming.

A word of advice: If they’re showing “Batman v Superman: Dawn of Justice,” on the giant al fresco screens, you’re better off skipping it and watching the robotic bartender mix, shake, and muddle a drink you order from an iPad. It’s far more entertaining — and you can get thoroughly hammered!

Better yet, skip the movie and spend your time people watching. When you have 6,700 passengers on a ship, you can bet your bikini that’s where the real show takes place. I practiced this art form one night when I met a pair of chippies (their word, not mine) who were cackling up a storm at the tango bar.

“Buy us a drink, sweetheart!” commanded Molly, a retired British teacher who was sailing with her friend Patrice (names changed to protect their dignity). One was wearing a T-shirt with a picture of a melting ice cream cone that read “Let’s Get Sticky.” The other was wearing a low-cut sundress that displayed her sun-damaged décolletage.

I knew immediately it was going to be a long night.

The next morning Molly and Patrice knocked at my cabin door with mimosas in hand. Why had I told them my room number? It was like I was living in a cruise ship episode of “Absolutely Fabulous” with these two around.

“We want to see your room,” they said as they pushed passed me. “Look! He’s got trees and flowers!”

My room overlooked the neighborhood called Central Park. At times I didn’t feel as if I was on a ship because all I could see was greenery and no ocean. The room felt as if it belonged in a hotel rather than a ship. I had plenty of storage and even the bathroom was a decent size.

Patrice and Molly had made it clear the night before that I would be spending the day with them at the pool. They were experts at never asking questions, they just told me what to do. After settling in our chaises, they began their venomous assessments of fellow passengers.

“Oh, look at those two,” Patrice said while glancing over her sunglasses. “Newlyweds. A shame it won’t last.”

They looked happy to me.

“Look at the way he’s checking out other women,” Patrice said. “He’s got a case of the wandering eye. My ex-husband suffered the same affliction.”

This is how it went for the next hour. No one escaped their sharp gazes.

“Darling quick, look up,” Molly said. “Do you see those? They’re fake.”

When they tired of the game, it was time to torture me.

“Honey, let’s go on that surfing thing,” Patrice said.

She was referring to contraption called the flowrider. It’s a pool that lets you simulate surfing, surfboard and all.

“I’ll do it if you do it,” she said.

I’ll mention here that Patrice was in her late 50s (that’s a generous guess) with a voice as smooth as a box of gravel and a pair of dimpled thighs that indicated her last workout buddy was Jane Fonda on a Betamax tape.

Seeing her on a surfboard would be more entertaining than “Grease” and the robotic bartender combined.

While in line I saw others struggle to stay upright on the surfboard (technically a flowboard) as sheets of water at speeds of 20 to 30 m.p.h. rushed beneath them. Most of them wobbled a few seconds before they were swept up in the current and whipped backward.

I hesitantly stepped forward as a kindly attendant tried to explain to me how it all worked. I cautiously got on the surf board, a foot at a time. He held both of my hands while I tried to balance.

In my head I repeated “Don’t let go, don’t let go, don’t let go.”

He let go.

I think I stayed upright for about 10 seconds before my legs gave out and I shot backward. It didn’t hurt, but my bathing trunks slipped down revealing parts that should not be revealed publicly.

I heard some familiar cackling. Patrice had stepped out of line and joined Molly in the spectator’s area. She and her dimpled thighs had no intention of getting on the flowrider. They just wanted to see my epic fail.

“I almost saw your block and tackle!” she screeched.

“Hey buddy,” the attendant said, handing me the surfboard. “Give it another try.”

I politely insisted that I didn’t need another turn. Perhaps he was unaware that this was an activity for athletic sadists only. I don’t fall into either category.

With enough coaxing, I got back on the surfboard, and this time I last more than five seconds. I lasted 20 seconds and kept my John Thomas in my trunks.

Later I raised a glass to celebrate this semi-success with my new British friends. We tried out the infamous 10-story slide called the Ultimate Abyss and were sufficiently terrified. We tried rock climbing — at least I tried it. Patrice said she didn’t want her thrupney bits showing. I was too afraid to ask what that meant.

By late afternoon my two friends were ready for tea, which meant Long Island Iced Tea. I told them I would meet up with them, and then quickly slipped off to find a remote deck chair where I could hide and not seek further adventures.

Once tucked away I opened the Royal Caribbean app on my phone and bought an excursion to go into Rome the following day. I may have forgotten to tell my buddies that I bought the excursion. That was the advantage of traveling on a massive resort at sea. I could choose from endless options and socializing with new friends, but at that moment my favorite option was free. It was nothing more than looking at the sunshine reflecting off the ocean, and the serene and blissful silence.

Seven night Western Caribbean cruises departing from Fort Lauderdale on Harmony of the Seas start at $750.
