Terror suspect was overweight, bored, and not serious about ISIS, defense says

By Maria Cramer Globe Staff  September 20, 2017

In 2015, David Wright was an Islamic State disciple only in his mind — an obese, deeply insecure man so desperate for attention that he began to indulge in fantasies that he was a combatant for the Islamic State, his defense attorney told a jury on Wednesday.

“He was self-consumed,” his lawyer, Jessica Hedges, told jurors during her opening statement in Wright’s terrorism trial at the John Joseph Moakley Courthouse in Boston. “Mostly, back then, he was an incredibly lost young man with incredibly poor judgment.”

Prosecutors have described Wright, 28, as an ardent follower of the Islamic State who was prepared to carry out violence against anyone the terrorist group deemed its enemy. Wright is charged with conspiring to support a terrorist organization, conspiring to commit acts of terrorism, obstruction of justice, and aiding and abetting.

In her opening statement, Assistant United States Attorney B. Stephanie Siegmann told jurors that Wright recruited his uncle, 26-year-old Usaamah Abdullah Rahim, and another man, Nicholas Rovinski, of Warwick, R.I., to pledge their loyalty to the Islamic State and commit violence in its name.

She said the evidence would show that Wright wanted to hurt people on a scale surpassing the 2013 Boston Marathon bombing, which killed three spectators and wounded more than 260 others.

“He wanted to harm the United States more than the Boston Marathon bombers because in his words that was not sufficient,’’ she said.

Wright was arrested in Everett on June 2, 2015, the same day Rahim was fatally shot by police after they said he attacked them with a knife in a Roslindale parking lot.

Rahim had called Wright that morning to tell him he planned to target “boys in blue,” a conversation heard and recorded by the FBI, which had placed Rahim under surveillance.

“The defendant encouraged him to kill police officers,” Siegmann said. “He urged Mr. Rahim to pursue martyrdom.”

But Hedges said Wright never imagined his uncle would go so far. Wright, who weighed 530 pounds, had fooled himself into believing he and his uncle were playing make-believe, like when they were younger and pretended they were ninjas or rap stars, Hedges said.

By that spring, he was so despondent about his life that he retreated into an online world where he played video games at all hours and chatted online with members of the Islamic State, who made him feel like a warrior, Hedges said.

“He wanted to escape the life he had,” she said.

At one point, Hedges asked Wright to stand up. The towering man, who wore a blue button-down shirt and navy sweater vest, carefully rose to his feet and faced the jury.

“David looks and is different today,” Hedges said. “When you look closely, you will see that David felt very, very fat, very failed, and was living in a world of fantastical ideas.”

Hedges acknowledged that Wright’s online screeds, which called for killing anyone the Islamic State considered an enemy, were horrifying, but said they qualified as protected speech under the First Amendment.

“We can be incredibly angry at what someone reads, at what someone says, and not convict them as a criminal,” she said.

To convict Wright, prosecutors must prove that he intended to engage in a conspiracy to help terrorists; that he conspired to destroy evidence that implicated him and the other men involved in the plot; or that he aided and abetted Rahim’s efforts.

One of the first witnesses called Wednesday was Rovinski, 26, who pleaded guilty in 2016 to conspiracy charges of plotting to carry out Islamic State-inspired attacks. He is expected to be sentenced to 15 years in prison.

Rovinski said he and Wright began communicating on Facebook in the fall of 2014 and bonded over their shared admiration for the Islamic State.

They met the following May and drove to a secluded beach in Rhode Island near Rovinski’s home, where Wright talked with excitement about killing Pamela Geller, an anti-Islam activist.

“He said that she deserved to be beheaded for the fact that she insulted the Prophet Mohammed,” Rovinski said.

He convinced his uncle to join the plot, Siegmann told jurors. Rahim eventually bought three knives for the men on Amazon, but by June 2015 he grew impatient and instead chose to pursue police.

John Ellement of the Globe Staff contributed to this report.
