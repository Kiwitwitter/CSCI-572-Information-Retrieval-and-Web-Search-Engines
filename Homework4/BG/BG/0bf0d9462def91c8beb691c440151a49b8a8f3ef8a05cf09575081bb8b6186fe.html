Today is National Lobster Day and this US senator really (really) wants a lobster emoji

By Steve Annear Globe Staff  September 25, 2017

In 2016, the lobster haul in Maine was worth more than $500 million. Now, an elected official from the coastal state is trying to get the ocean dwellers the recognition he thinks they deserve.

US Senator Angus King has appealed to the organization that oversees which emojis make their way into people’s smartphones to include the crustaceans pulled by the thousands from the New England seas.

Advertisement

In a letter sent last week to the Unicode Technical Committee, which is part of the Unicode Consortium, a nonprofit that holds the power to accept or deny emoji proposals (they do lots of more pressing work), King tried to butter up the organization.

“We can expect frequent usage of a lobster emoji,” King wrote in his two-page letter, which took a light-hearted tone. “Not only is the lobster of substantial cultural relevance . .. but statistics from leading online platforms indicate a public interest in lobster equal to that of the most closely-related emoji, the crab.”

King told the committee that adding a lobster would complete a set of emoji representing crustaceans commonly consumed as food items.

For those unfamiliar, the Unicode Consortium maintains the Unicode Standard, the character coding system that allows electronic devices around the world to “share or exchange text that is written in any language or with symbols,” including emojis.

Anyone can submit a proposal for an emoji character, according to the consortium’s website, but a strict set of guidelines must be followed, and there’s often a lot of competition.

Advertisement

The lobster is currently one of 67 emoji characters that are “draft candidates” for inclusion in Unicode 11.0, which is scheduled for release in 2018. The candidates include emojis for a “frowning pile of poo,” a skateboard, and a lab coat to name a few.

Finalists from that list will be revealed in October, at the organization’s next quarterly meeting.

Despite the odds, King, who also happens to be the force behind Monday’s designation as National Lobster Day, seems determined in his quest.

He even tried to advance his cause to slot the lobster in the emoji lineup — perhaps somewhere between the shrimp emoji and the leg of chicken — by posting a message on Instagram last Friday. The post featured a picture of a bucket of lobsters and a hefty serving of puns.

“OK, the Maine lobster industry is on a roll. It . . . supports families up and down the coast, and is part of what induces millions of visitors to claw their way through southern New England traffic to get up here every summer,” he said. “In fact, lobsters are so important to Maine, it makes me downright [crab emoji] that there’s #nolobsteremoji.”

In August, Luke’s Lobster, whose owner is from Maine, launched a Change.org petition in favor of a lobster emoji. The petition has garnered more than 4,000 digital signatures as of Monday.

If the reactions to the petition and King’s Instagram post are any indication, the emoji would be a popular one — especially in the summer, when lobster rolls dominate menus and social media feeds fill up with artistic shots of tasty lobster dishes.

“Now that is a wicked idea! I would use it all the time,” one person wrote.

Another replied, “I was steamed when I realized there was no lobster emoji.”

Lucky for them, something is cooking.
