Vibrant red blends from Chile worth a second taste

Ellen Bhang for The Boston Globe

By Ellen Bhang Globe Correspondent  September 26, 2017

If your impression of Chilean wine was formed in the ’90s, when the country’s wine industry was just making inroads into international markets, you might think of these bottles as offering solid value, but little else. The news flash is that the South American powerhouse is home to plenty of producers crafting vibrant, head-turning pours. Three red blends provide a glimpse at the diversity and quality on offer.

In the Maule Valley, Chile’s largest production zone, French-Basque winegrower David Marcel and his wife, Loreto Garau, are reviving an old style of table wine, called pipeño, a fresh, low-tannin red that’s light in body. On Viña Maitia, their 25-acre estate, the couple blends a heritage varietal called país — listán prieto in its native Spain, and the mission grape in California — with carignan, which gently plumps up the mid-palate. The duo uses minimal sulfur, and conducts fermentations with indigenous yeast in concrete. Both artisanal practices garner attention of natural wine enthusiasts who often associate Chile with mass-produced pours.

Also situated in the Maule Valley is producer J. Bouchon. The family’s patriarch, viticulturist Emile Bouchon, left Bordeaux for Chile in the late 19th century. Today, Julio Bouchon and his children vinify a trio of grapes into a vivid pour called “Canto Sur,” an aged-in-concrete red. Carmenère, which forms the backbone of this wine, was long misclassified as Chilean merlot. In the mid-’90s, it was rediscovered as the southwestern French varietal. The Bouchon clan loves what the vigorous grape can do, along with carignan and país, cultivated from hundred-year-old vines.

Travel north to Santiago, then continue 300 miles more to the southern edge of the Atacama Desert. The Elqui Valley, one of the driest spots on earth, is also one of the best places to observe the stars. Mauro Olivier, son of Italian-born Aldo Olivier, a well-known pisco producer and founder of Viña Falernia, heads up the wine brand Mayu. Giorgio Flessati, Aldo Olivier’s cousin, oversees winemaking. This project was launched a dozen years ago, when the area was still developing as a cool-climate hinterland for wine. Carmenère and syrah, both of which are partially aged in oak, make up the blend. This bottle shows that balanced richness can be coaxed out of an extreme environment.

The motto of Mayu, which is the Incan word for the Milky Way, is “drink the stars.” Good advice when it comes to enjoying each of these stellar pours.

Aupa “Pipeño” 2016 Raspberry scents combine with notes of smoke and graphite in this light-hued, salt-accented blend. 12.9 percent alcohol by volume (ABV). Around $14. At The Cheese Shop of Salem, Salem, 978-498-4820; The Wine Bottega, North End, 617-227-6607.

J. Bouchon “Canto Sur” 2015 Ripe berries, violets, and anise on the nose, leading to a ripe palate with vibrant fruit and undeniable swagger. 13.3 percent ABV. Around $15. At Brookline Liquor Mart, Chestnut Hill, 617-734-7702; Needham Center Fine Wines, Needham, 781-400-1769.

Mayu Carmenère-Syrah 2014 Rich with dark fruit and a whiff of ferrous soil, this concentrated blend offers generous but balanced heft. 14.5 ABV. Around $15. At Brookline Liquor Mart, Chestnut Hill; Pairings Wine and Food, Winchester, 781-721-9463.
