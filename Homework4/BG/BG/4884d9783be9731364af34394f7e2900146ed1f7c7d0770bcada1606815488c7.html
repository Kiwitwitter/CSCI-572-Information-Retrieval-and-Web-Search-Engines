Boots on the ground

By Kevin Lewis Globe Correspondent  September 02, 2016

An earthquake struck Northern Pakistan on Oct. 8, 2005, killing tens of thousands and leaving millions homeless. For the next several months, foreign aid and aid workers poured in. Surveys conducted in affected villages four years later suggest that we may be too pessimistic about the intransigence of Muslim attitudes toward the West. In these villages, levels of trust toward Westerners increased “from those typically found in Pakistan (one of the lowest in the world) to those found in Sweden (one of the highest in the world).” In fact, villagers trusted foreigners (including Westerners) more than they trusted their own neighbors. This was specifically attributable to the presence of aid workers and was not explained by pre-earthquake village demographics or post-earthquake mortality and migration.

Andrabi, T. & Das, J., “In Aid We Trust: Hearts and Minds and the Pakistan Earthquake of 2005,” Review of Economics and Statistics (forthcoming).

Tough times for health insurance

Your preexisting conditions no longer prevent you from buying health insurance, but your early-existing conditions may change your mind. In a new study, when asked to think pessimistically about the economy, people who grew up poor became significantly less interested in having insurance coverage than people who grew up rich, even controlling for current financial and health status, age, and gender. However, when people were explicitly given information about the probability of getting sick, the effect reversed, such that people who grew up poor and were asked to think pessimistically about the economy became more likely to think they could get sick and more interested in having insurance coverage.

Mittal, C. & Griskevicius, V., “Silver Spoons and Platinum Plans: How Childhood Environment Affects Adult Healthcare Decisions,” Journal of Consumer Research (forthcoming).

Betting on women

Advertisement

Some research has found that women are less inclined to take risks and that this may partly explain the challenges women face in the workplace. However, research on two indigenous island cultures suggests that it’s more nurture than nature. The Palawan of the Philippines are patrilineal (i.e., social status passes down the male line, as has been the tradition in the West), while the Teop of Papua New Guinea are matrilineal, such that “women’s prerogative over land includes defining land boundaries, giving permission to hunt or to harvest timber, and the exclusive right to veto decisions on land-related matters.” The researchers asked villagers to choose from several different gambles and to predict how a generic man and woman would choose. For one’s own gamble, there was no gender difference in either culture, but when predicting a woman’s gamble, men in the patrilineal culture underestimated her willingness to take a risk, while men in the matrilineal culture overestimated her willingness to take a risk.

Pondorfer, A. et al., “Gender Differences in Stereotypes of Risk Preferences: Experimental Evidence from a Matrilineal and a Patrilineal Society,” Management Science (forthcoming).

A silly troll makes a good choice

If you’re selling something — a product, a service, a charity, etc. — you’re probably a bit paranoid about any negative reviews being published. But according to new research, you can relax, at least about the lame ones. In a series of experiments, researchers found that adding a negative but irrelevant review — for example, a one-star review of an espresso machine that only states “there is a considerable difference in taste when mineral or filtered water are used rather than tap water” — to a set of good reviews actually boosted consumer evaluations compared to only having good reviews, or adding a positive but irrelevant review. This was true even though people thought the irrelevant review should indeed be irrelevant to the evaluation. The effect was strong enough to make a set of reviews by strangers seem as reliable as a set of reviews by friends. And the effect even carried over to post-choice satisfaction: People who chose to watch a video that had a negative but irrelevant review were more satisfied with the video than people who chose to watch the same video when it had a positive but irrelevant review.

Shoham, M. et al., “Irrelevant Negative Information Enhances Positive Impressions,” Journal of Consumer Psychology (forthcoming).

Previous research has found that people are less moved when there are multiple victims, compared to a single, identified victim. A new study extends this research to the reactions of victims themselves. People expected a victim of negligence to experience more harm if there were no other victims. Likewise, participants in an experiment who were deceived to think they had gotten a low test score “judged the deception as less immoral and less harmful to them when they were told that other participants had also been deceived.”

Konis, D. et al., “A Sorrow Shared Is a Sorrow Halved: Moral Judgments of Harm to Single versus Multiple Victims,” Frontiers in Psychology (August 2016).
