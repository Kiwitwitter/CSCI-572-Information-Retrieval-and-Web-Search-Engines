Mayoral candidates sound off on Framingham’s future

By Jennifer Fenn Lefferts Globe Correspondent  October 05, 2017

Framingham residents will head to the polls on Nov. 7 to vote for the city’s first mayor. Yvonne Spicer and John Stefanini were the top two vote-getters in last month’s preliminary election, setting the stage for this historic election.

This is the first in a series of questions and answers about the candidates and their visions for the future of the city of Framingham.

Yvonne Spicer, 55

Occupation: Vice president of Advocacy & Educational Partnerships for the National Center for Technological Literacy division at the Museum of Science

Experience: Town Meeting member, member of Massachusetts STEM Advisory Council, the Economic Empowerment Trust Fund, and the Massachusetts Business Roundtable

Why do you want to be mayor?

Advertisement

I feel that Framingham is a great community to live in. As we embark on this transition, Framingham deserves leadership that is tried, tested, and true. When we made the decision to become a city, I thought I’ve learned a lot that poised me to be the first mayor of Framingham.

You didn’t support the idea of becoming a city. As mayor, how will you lead this transition and bring the community together?

If we are going to be a city, let’s be the best city we can be. We need to engage people on both sides of the issue — those who wanted to be a city and those who didn’t. At the end of the day all the people want the same thing — quality education, quality living, maintaining open space. One of the things I’ve made very clear is that my administration will be very open, honest, and transparent. Everyone will see what we are doing, how we spend money, and our strategic plans. That goes a long way on building credibility.

What are the three biggest challenges facing Framingham?

Economic development, particularly in the downtown area. Education – Framingham does have a couple of schools labeled as Level 3. And thinking about our open spaces and sustainable environment.

What do you see as the role of a mayor?

I see the mayor as the chief cheerleader in so many ways — the public face of Framingham. This is my city and I’m going to the mat for it, having a seat at the state level and really being that champion and the visionary. Having a vision to guide this city into the 21st century and beyond. And having measurable accountability along the way so we can measure our progress of where we are and where we are going.

John Stefanini, 53

Occupation: Attorney

Advertisement

Experience: Served 10 years as a state representative; six years as a member of the Board of Selectmen. Has served on several boards including trustee for MetroWest Medical Center and the Framingham Heart Study. Was chair of the Cushing Hospital Task Force.

Why do you want to be mayor?

Framingham is a great community but it has the resources and ability and people to be so much more. The opportunity to put my 30 years of experience teaching, studying, serving, and working in state and municipal government to work for the community I know and love is an incredible opportunity.

You supported moving to a city form of government. As mayor, how will you lead this transition and bring the community together?

I’m proud of my support and work to reform our community to bring accountability, transparency, integrity, and efficiency to our decision making. This is a fundamental difference between my opponent and I.

First and foremost, we need to follow the directives of the charter to create a strategic master plan through an open, participatory, and collaborative process that speaks to our values, reaches for our dreams, and meets our dreams. Then we need to roll up our sleeves to make our strategic plan a reality. We do this by listening to each other and working together in a collaborative manner.

What are the three biggest challenges facing Framingham?

The biggest changes facing Framingham are filling and revitalizing our vacant shopping centers, improving our four underperforming schools, and getting our finances under control.

What do you see as the role of a mayor?

Our full-time mayor needs to be prepared to wear many hats — chief negotiator, collaborator, advocate, spokesperson, and cheerleader. I’d like us to be a city that embraces its diversity, that involves all its citizens in the decision-making process, that treats taxpayers’ dollars with respect and that is always accountable to its residents.
