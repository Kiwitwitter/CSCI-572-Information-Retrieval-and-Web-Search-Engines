WOMEN’S COLLEGE HOCKEY 2017-18

BC’s Kenzie Kent finds lacrosse a nice complement to hockey

By Barbara Matson Globe Correspondent  September 27, 2017

Vote now for The Globies sports awards

Hockey was Kenzie’s idea. Everybody in the Kent family of Norwell was introduced to lacrosse by Kenzie’s mother, Jennifer, a three-sport athlete at Colby College and a lacrosse coach. Lacrosse sticks were everywhere in the house, dozens of them jammed into a decorative vase inside the front door so the six children could grab and go. Kenzie’s father Jeff had been a football player and a wrestler.

But one day in grade school, Kenzie Kent decided she wanted to try out for hockey.

Jennifer was being pulled in several directions by job and kids, so it was Kenzie’s grandfather who took her to the hockey tryout. But nobody in the family really knew anything about hockey, and they went to the wrong rink.

The Kents knew about Pilgrim Arena in nearby Hingham, but they had never heard of Hobomock in Pembroke. That drafty dinosaur was where Kenzie’s hockey career started, with the South Shore Seahawks.

“The second tryout — which was her first tryout — she was the first in line,” said Jennifer. “And she really didn’t know how to skate. It was one of those, ‘Oh my gosh, what are we doing?’

“But it was all her. We had no hockey in our family.’’

Kenzie Kent, now captain of the fourth-ranked Boston College women’s team, which begins its season next Thursday against Minnesota-Duluth, soon made herself into a hockey player. From the Squirt B travel team to Thayer Academy to the Assabet Valley club (three Tier 1 national championships) to prep power Nobles, she just got better and better. So good that she was twice named to the US Under-18 team for the World Championships.

Advertisement

She also never stopped playing lacrosse, and she is now one of the most impressive two-sport athletes in collegiate sports. This will be her fourth year playing back-to-back seasons of hockey and lacrosse, which overlap by several months. She joins the lacrosse team when hockey is finished, which for the last three years has been after a tough Frozen Four loss.

As a forward for the hockey team, Kent has point totals of 38, 43, and 26 in three years; those 107 points place her 14th on the Eagles’ career scoring chart, and she has 76 career assists, tied for 10th. The 21-year-old senior is a star on the lacrosse team, and she was last year’s Atlantic Coast Conference Female Athlete of the Year. She has played in four Final Fours (three in hockey, one in lacrosse) in three seasons, and she is determined to snag a trophy.

“It’s got to happen,” she said. “I can’t leave here without one, I really can’t.”

Kent’s run through the NCAA lacrosse tournament in May was extraordinary: She tied the championship-game record with 10 points (5 goals, 5 assists) against No. 1 Maryland and smashed the tournament scoring record, tallying 37 points in five games — topping the previous mark of 26. She was named MVP of the tournament despite not playing on the winning team.

“She was unbelievable,’’ said her hockey coach, Katie Crowley. “Two years ago, we had just lost in the championship game and five days later, she’s starting against UNC.

“I went to the game and was just in awe. I don’t know lacrosse that well, but I’m thinking ‘Wow, she’s, like, the best one on the field.’ Maybe she isn’t, but I think she is, especially coming from being on the ice and not running.”

When Kent joins the lacrosse team, she is essentially bumping someone else from a spot. But it has never caused a problem with her teammates.

“She’s a humble, nice person, and that has a lot to do with it,’’ Crowley said. “She’s able to earn that respect from people.”

Kent always figured lacrosse would be her college sport.

“I got way more attention from lacrosse schools,” she said.

It turns out, the BC hockey coaches had their eye on her from her days at Thayer.

“We really thought she had something, that there was something unique about her,’’ said Crowley, a multisport athlete herself (hockey and softball) who said Kent’s crossover in the sports helps her read the game.

“She sees things that other people don’t necessarily see, and that, to me, is what is so unique about her. She’s not going to stand out as the fastest or the strongest or having the most goals or anything like that.

“She’s going to be one of those players who sets people up, who makes her teammates better because she’s on their line.

“And she has really good hands on the ice; you can see that comes from lacrosse. She does similar things out there, I think, just with the puck on the ice instead of a ball up in the air.”

For Kent, a typical day at BC means an early class, a lift, a practice, and another class. Oh, and time for hanging out, probably in BC’s sumptuous new locker room.

“I was there from 12 o’clock to 8 o’clock last night,” she said with a giggle. “We were just watching movies, and we left to get food, of course, and then came back.”

On the ice, Kent is learning her new role as a team leader. She sets a quiet example.

“My favorite part of hockey is it’s a game of mistakes, so you can make as many mistakes, and as long as you make up for the mistakes, it’s OK,’’ she said. “It’s really fast, so I like how if you make a mistake, you have to go on to your next play, and it’s not dwelled upon.”

Kent has no time to dwell on mistakes. Once one season ends, she transitions to another and she’s off and running.

Here’s a look at the Division 1 women’s teams in Boston.

BOSTON COLLEGE

Coach: Katie Crowley, 248-80-44, 11th year.

Last year: 28-6-5; 17-4-3 Hockey East.

Postseason: Won Hockey East; lost NCAA semifinal to Wisconsin.

Outlook: Cayla Barnes, captain of the gold-medal US Under-18 team and the only player to win three consecutive golds at the U18 World Championships, is likely to step right in to bolster the defense. And the Eagles will need her after losing blue liners Megan Keller and Kali Flanagan for the year to Olympic training. Senior Toni Ann Miano provides the experience on defense. Up front, where Andie Anastos and Kristyn Capizzano were lost, three freshmen have been added: Daryl Watts and Maegan Beres bring international U18 experience from Canada, while Willow Corson has played in the U18 Canadian national championships. Feisty forward Makenna Newkirk, the leading returning scorer (15-24—39), drives the offense, along with senior Kenzie Kent (9-17—26), and sophomores Caitrin Lonergan (15-18—33) and Delaney Belinskas (16-17—33). Goalie Katie Burt (1.537 GAA), already the Eagles’ career leader in wins, shutouts, save percentage, and goals-against average, enters her senior season tied for sixth in NCAA history in wins (91) and fifth in shutouts (31). Expect BC, ranked No. 4 in the initial USCHO.com poll, to remain the team to beat in Hockey East, as the Eagles aim for their fifth consecutive title.

BOSTON UNIVERSITY

Coach: Brian Durocher, 250-141-49, 12th year.

Last year: 19-12-6; 12-8-4 Hockey East.

Postseason: Lost to Northeastern in Hockey East semifinal.

Outlook: The Terriers lost forwards Mary Parker (24 goals) and Maddie Elia (12-24—36), but found four new forwards, three defensemen, and one goaltender. Two of the newcomers have Division 1 experience: transfers D/F Reagan Rust from RIT and D Abbey Stanley from the defunct North Dakota program. The freshmen are F/D Rachel Allen, F Jesse Compher, F Nara Elia, F Lauren Klein, G Corinne Schroeder, and F Kristina Schuler. Senior F Victoria Bach, who nabbed the Hockey East scoring title last season with 22 goals and 49 points and was 11th nationally in scoring, will be counted on to lead the attack. Bach has some experienced players on hand to chip in on offense, including seniors Rebecca Leslie (16-18—34) and Nina Rodgers (11-20—31). Sophomore D Abby Cook was an All-Rookie team choice last season, and led all Hockey East freshmen at plus-19. In net, BU has senior Erin O’Neil, 30-15-5 career, 4-3-2 last season.

NORTHEASTERN

Coach: Dave Flint, 156-103-28, ninth year.

Last year: 22-12-3; 14-8-2 Hockey East.

Postseason: Lost to BC in Hockey East final.

Outlook: NU opens its season with a pair of games against defending national champion Clarkson, so it must get ready in a hurry. The offense will be paced by last year’s leading scorer, Denisa Krizova (18-27—45, tied for 15th in the nation), and fourth-year forward McKenna Brand, who scored a team-high 23 goals and finished second on the team with 38 points. The Huskies added BC transfer F Tori Sullivan (55 points in two years) and should have more offensive depth. A top recruit is Brooke Hobson, a Canadian U18 defenseman who is a great skater and can move the puck. In goal, Brittany Bugalski (21-10-3, 1.98 GAA, .928 save percentage) returns for her third season as the starter.

HARVARD

Coach: Katey Stone, 451-208-46, 23rd year.

Last year: 5-19-5; 5-13-4 ECAC.

Postseason: DNQ.

Outlook: The Crimson lost six seniors, including goalie Brianna Laing, Natasha Rachlin, Briana Mastel, Abbey Frazer, Molly Tissenbaum and second-leading scorer Sydney Daniels (11-5—16). Nine freshmen have been added to reinforce the Crimson, who struggled mightily last year just two years removed from their last trip to the Frozen Four. The newcomers include Becca Gilmore, a six-year varsity forward from Noble & Greenough and the top US forward at the U18 world championships. Among the returnees trying to rebuild the Crimson is last season’s top scorer, Lexie Laing (8-13—21). Beth Larcom and Emily Yue will compete with freshman Becky Dutton (Kent School) for time in net.

BARBARA MATSON
