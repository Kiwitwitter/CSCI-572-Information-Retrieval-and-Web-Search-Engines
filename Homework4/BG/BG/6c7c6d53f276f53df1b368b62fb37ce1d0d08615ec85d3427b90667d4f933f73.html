Time for the Boston Film Festival

Boston Film Festival

Olivia Thirlby and Jonathan Rhys Meyers star in “Damascus Cover.”

By Loren King Globe Correspondent  September 15, 2017

The Boston Film Festival celebrates its 33rd year Sept. 21-24 with a modest but eclectic lineup of scripted features, documentaries and shorts. Actor Jonathan Rhys Meyers (“Mission: Impossible III,” “The Tudors”) and director Daniel Zelik Berk, a Brandeis graduate, will attend the Sept. 23 world premiere of their thriller, “Damascus Cover,” at AMC Boston Common.

Set in 1989 after the fall of the Berlin Wall, it’s about a veteran spy who’s sent undercover to smuggle a chemical weapons scientist and his family out of Syria and who runs into life-threatening dangers and complications. Olivia Thirlby and the late John Hurt (in his final performance) costar.

There are only three other scripted features in the festival but they offer something for everyone. Director and co-writer Ash Avildsen’s thriller “American Satan” (Sept. 22) is about a struggling rock band headed by the aptly-named Kat Faust (Denise Richards). The bandmates drop out of college, pile into a van, and head for Los Angeles in pursuit of stardom. An enigmatic stranger helps them achieve their dream but the bargain comes at a price. John Bradley and Malcolm MacDowell costar.

Massachusetts native Bryan Buckley wrote and directed “Dabka” (Sept. 23), with New Hampshire native Matt Lefebvre producing. Based on a true story, it’s about a rookie reporter (Jay Bahadur) who impulsively decides in 2008 to embed himself among Somali pirates. His experience results in a vivid, first-hand account of the lives of these men and the forces that drive them. The cast includes Al Pacino, Melanie Griffith, and Barkhad Abdi (“Captain Phillips”).

Kavin Trent directs “Crash Pad” (Sept.24) from a script by Maine native Jeremy Catalino. It’s a battle-of-the-sexes comedy about romantic slacker Stensland (Domhnall Gleeson) who thinks he’s found love after meeting an older woman, Morgan (Christina Applegate). But he gets snared in a love triangle when he discovers their one-night-stand was a just an act of revenge against her boorish husband, Grady (Thomas Haden Church). To get back at Morgan, Grady moves into Stensland’s apartment and takes over his life. The young misfit must get the couple back together to regain control.

All screenings take place at the AMC Boston Common.

www.bostonfilmfestival.org

Big movies, big screen

The Boston Society of Film Critics last year awarded a commendation to the Somerville Theatre for its first-ever 70mm and Widescreen Film Festival. The festival was such a success with audiences that Somerville manager Ian Judge and projectionist David Kornfeld have put together an even more ambitious lineup, with 15 titles from the 1950s through the 1980s, all shot in the prestigious widescreen format. It starts Sept. 20, with the Charlton Heston epic “The Agony and the Ecstasy” (1965). That’s followed on Sept. 21 by David Lean’s masterpiece “Lawrence of Arabia” (1962), starring Peter O’Toole, with an additional showing on Sept. 23. A pair of films from the 1980s, “The Dark Crystal” and “Howard the Duck,” share a bill on Sept. 22.
