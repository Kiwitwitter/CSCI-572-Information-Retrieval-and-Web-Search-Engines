A guide to sauces, plus six Chinese baked treats you must try

Get to know these flavors from spicy to sweet.

WHAT’S THAT SAUCE?

A salty, sweet, spicy array of condiments makes Chinatown dining interactive. Here are a few favorites.

> Duck sauce — This gelatinous sweet sauce is a hit with kids and pairs well with snacky fried dishes like spring rolls. Most mainstream versions are pinkish, usually incorporating apricots; some are made with applesauce.

> Hot chili oil or paste — This amber puddle of fire made with red chilies often arrives in a shallow saucer. The paste clings nicely to the skins of fried or steamed dumplings, while the oil is perfect splashed atop Sichuan noodle dishes.

> Hot mustard — Sharper than American versions, this pale, smooth, pure mustard is often made simply with hot mustard powder, white vinegar, water, and sometimes horseradish. Just a dab will do, adding spicy complexity to a won ton or dim sum.

> Hoisin sauce — Usually slathered on moo shu dishes and Peking duck, this savory sauce with a sweet tang is strangely named: It means “seafood” sauce, but it’s not served with seafood (besides moo shu, you might see it on pan-seared turnip cakes at dim sum). It’s often made using black bean paste, brown sugar, and Chinese five-spice powder.

> Black rice vinegar and shredded ginger — Use this to dunk soup dumplings, often seen on menus as xiao long bao or mini juicy buns. The smoky, woodsy flavor plays well with these thick, purse-like pockets native to Shanghai, frequently stuffed with pork and crab.

> XO sauce — This sauce originated in Hong Kong; it often incorporates fermented bean paste, dried seafood, and soy sauce. A few drops add rich depth to stir-fried vegetables and roasted meats. Head to a Cantonese seafood spot for your fix.

SIX TASTY BITES TO SAMPLE AT CHINESE BAKERIES

Egg tart (pictured above) — Also served at dim sum restaurants, dan tat is essentially a little pie filled with egg custard. Great Taste Bakery and sister store Top Bread have great versions with a flaky crust.

Joanne Rathe/Globe Staff

Pineapple bun — Buyer, beware: This pastry gets its name from its appearance, not because it contains pineapple. A simple bun with a crispy and slightly sweet coating, it’s popular in Hong Kong. Some versions are plain; this one from Great Taste is filled with red bean.

Joanne Rathe/Globe Staff

Swiss roll — Sponge cake with cream inside, this Western-influenced dessert comes in a variety of flavors. You can buy individual slices or a whole cake for a party. Eldo Cake House has served its moist vanilla version for decades.  

Lane Turner/Globe Staff

Cocktail bun — Stuffed with a sweet coconut filling, this bun has no alcohol content: Its Chinese name literally means chicken tail. Try it at Great Taste, Crown Royal, or May’s Cake House.

Joanne Rathe/ globe staff

Steamed big bun — A fluffy, filling bun the size of a large fist, it’s stuffed with pork, Chinese sausage, hard-boiled egg, mushroom, and chive. Try one at Eldo Cake House or fresh from the oven early in the morning at Great Taste Bakery — they sell out by afternoon.

Joanne Rathe/ globe staff

Portuguese tart — Similar to the Portuguese pastel de nata, this rich, creamy treat is popular in Macao, formerly a Portuguese colony. Great Taste and Top Bread serve delicious ones.

THE HOT SPOTS

> Crown Royal Bakery, 23 Edinboro Street, 617-338-8889

> Eldo Cake House, 36 Harrison Avenue, 617-350-7977

> Great Taste Bakery, 61-63 Beach Street, 617-426-8899, greattasteboston.com

> May’s Cake House, 223A Harrison Avenue, 617-350-0210

> Top Bread, 77 Harrison Avenue, 617-338-1689
