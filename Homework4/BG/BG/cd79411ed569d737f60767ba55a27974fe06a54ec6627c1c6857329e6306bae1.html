The dubious theory behind President Trump’s tax plan

Tom Brenner/New York Times

President Trump delivered an address about tax reform in Indianapolis on Wednesday.

By Evan Horowitz Globe Staff  September 28, 2017

President Trump picked Indiana as the site to unveil his latest proposal to slash the corporate tax rate while reducing a variety of individual taxes, calling the state “a tremendous example of the prosperity that is unleashed when we cut taxes and set free the dreams of our citizens.”

Except there’s not much evidence for this. Indiana did cut taxes pretty dramatically under then-Governor Mike Pence in 2013, eliminating the estate tax while lowering rates for businesses and individuals.

But in the few years since, its economy has grown more slowly than the US as a whole, expanding roughly 1.4 percent per year compared to 2.3 percent nationwide, and household incomes haven’t kept up either. If anything the gap between Indiana’s economy and the rest of the country only widened after the tax cuts.

So where should Trump have gone, if he wanted a state that better exemplified the economy-boosting power of reduced tax rates?

It’s really not clear. Despite the president’s confident rhetoric, there isn’t much real-world evidence to support the idea that corporate tax cuts unleash economic growth by encouraging companies to invest, hire workers, and ultimately boost wages for average Americans. That theory — called supply-side or sometimes trickle-down economics — has been tried before, with limited success.

Which is not to say that tax cuts are always a bad idea. They can be a vital tool to help the economy through difficult times.

Trump cited an earlier example in his Wednesday speech: Ronald Reagan, who passed a massive tax cut package in 1981 that helped the US weather a recession and emerge with new strength. More recently, President Obama cut payroll taxes after the financial collapse of 2007-2009, bolstering the recovery by putting more spending money in the pockets of everyday workers.

But when it comes to making tax cuts work, so much depends on timing and detail.

The best economic case for a supply-side approach like Trump’s is that it will increase investment in the United States. Lowering the corporate rate could make it more attractive for companies to set up shop here, as opposed to seeking out lower-tax countries.

More important, tax cuts would give corporations money to invest in new machines and updated facilities. Firms get more efficient, which frees up money to hire more workers and pay better wages.

This only works, however, if businesses are eager to invest but struggling to raise money. In that case, tax cuts would be a real boon.

But that's not the current situation. Businesses have ready access to funds, both because after-tax profits are high and borrowing rates remain relatively low. Yet, despite these conditions, investment remains surprisingly limited.

Separately, you can’t estimate the economic impact of tax cuts without knowing how they’ll be paid for.

Republicans are still planning to offset most — if not all — of their proposed cuts by closing loopholes in the tax codes. But think about what that means for businesses. They may pay lower rates, sure, but they’ll also lose valuable tax breaks. So the influx of new money isn’t quite what it seems. And the more loopholes that get closed, the weaker the argument tax cuts will generate investment.

It seems unlikely — maybe mathematically impossible — that the entirety of Trump’s tax cuts will be made up by eliminating loopholes and deductions. Which leaves two possibilities.

Republicans could cut spending to match the lost revenue. In which case, the overall economic effect would depend on what spending we choose to cut. Less government funding for scientific research, child health, or military readiness could actually damage the long-term health of the US economy — or the country as a whole.

And while it’s popular to say we should just cut waste and fraud, that’s no free lunch. Stopping fraud costs money in the form of increased oversight, better tracking technologies, and investigations.

Alternatively, we could take the path most travelled and decide not to cover the cost of tax cuts at all. Ever since Reagan, the favored approach among supply-side politicians has been to cut taxes, leave spending more or less alone, and watch the deficits pile up.

But that has its own problems. Deficits can put upward pressure on interest rates. More fundamentally, deficit-financed tax cuts aren’t really cuts at all; they’re more like tax deferral. The government borrows $5 from the bond market and hands it to US businesses today, but at some point it will have to repay bondholders with new tax dollars, plus and interest.

Piece it together and you can see that tax cuts work best under a set of pretty narrow conditions. They have to be targeted to parts of the economy that are genuinely struggling, using money that the government doesn’t need for its own vital investments, and paid for in a way that won’t undermine their intended purpose.

Trump’s current proposals don’t seem to fit these criteria. So unless the economy suddenly sours, or Congress ends up taking a wildly different approach, this package may ultimately be remembered not alongside Reagan, but rather George W. Bush, whose own tax-cutting plans ended up having a much bigger effect on the deficit than they did on the economy.
