Mookie Betts reels in catch of the day

Stan Grossfeld/globe staff

Red Sox right fielder Mookie Betts snags a fly ball from Astros Josh Reddick in the second inning to save a home run.

By Julian Benbow Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

As soon as the ball jumped off Josh Reddick’s bat, the Red Sox could see their season parasailing to the right-field corner.

They were already in a three-run hole, and with two on and two out in the second inning, the shot that Reddick sent screaming for the short fence threatened to make the hole twice as deep.

When Mookie Betts first started tracking the ball, he didn’t think it had a chance to leave the park.

“But it just kept going and going,” he said.

From center field, all Jackie Bradley Jr. could do was watch. He had manned the outfield with Betts by his side long enough to trust that Betts could make a play.

“Anytime that a ball has enough air under it, the majority of the time, it’s going to give us a chance to do something,” Bradley said.

First, Betts had to cover the ground.

Advertisement

He ran a 112-foot route, according to MLBAM’s Statcast, just to get to the short fence.

Then, he had to count on the fans in the front row to leave the catch for him.

“It was just one of those things where Fenway Park, everyone kind of understands baseball,” Betts said. “I expected some arms coming up. But usually, when the arms go up, nobody makes contact with the ball. They get scared when it starts to come down. So I figured I’d go ahead and catch it.”

Betts made his long run and over-the-shoulder grab look easy. Reddick, making his way to second, stopped in his tracks and slammed his helmet in disbelief.

They both went back to the dugout knowing how critical the catch was.

Instead of the Astros throwing the Red Sox in a 6-0 ditch early, the Sox stayed within striking distance, climbed out of the hole, and ultimately ran away with a 10-3 win to stay alive in the American League Division Series.

“It’s a completely different game,” Andrew Benintendi said. “I’m pretty sure it was going to be a home run. Big momentum swing.”

The Sox offense exploded, but Betts’s catch was the catalyst.

“That’s definitely a game-saver,” Bradley said. “Obviously, we don’t know how the game’s going to unfold, but it’s a big momentum-changer. Three-run homer? We would’ve been down, 6-0? You start adding numbers up, the percentages of coming back from a 6-0 game is a lot less than a 3-0 game.”

Red Sox right fielder Mookie Betts snagged a fly ball from Astros third baseman Josh Reddick in the second inning to save a home run.

Backs to the wall, facing the potential of being swept for the second straight postseason, every play mattered, Betts said.

“We understand it’s kind of a do-or-die, go-home situation,” he said. “But I think every game we should play with a sense of urgency.”

The resemblance to the catch Dwight Evans made in Game 6 of the 1975 World Series was obvious. Betts had to travel a few steps farther into the corner and closer to the short fence.

The Sox have become accustomed to seeing the Gold Glover make the kind of grabs that make hitters slam their helmets.

“Usually if it’s in the park, Mookie’s going to catch it,” Dustin Pedroia said.

If it’s not?

“Then it’s a home run,” Pedroia said.

In the seventh, with the Sox clutching tightly to a 4-3 lead, Betts came up with another play that only he could make.

With one on and one out, Carlos Correa sent a shallow liner to right. Betts, who typically plays more shallow than most right fielders, raced in to pluck it before it bounced in for a single. A batter later, David Price struck out Marwin Gonzalez to end the inning and cut the cord on the Astros’ last true threat.

“He had a big day defensively in right field,” Sox manager John Farrell said. “We have talked so much about the range of our outfielders. He has it.”

As he gathered his things and headed to the scrum of media huddled in the center of the clubhouse, Betts grabbed a trucker hat, then read the logo stitched on the front.

“Flashing Leather.”

He chuckled and said “How ironic.”
