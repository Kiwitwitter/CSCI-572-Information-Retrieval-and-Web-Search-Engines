Who will win the Emmys — and who should

By Matthew Gilbert Globe Staff  September 15, 2017

Remember that extra-long “Game of Thrones” break between seasons six and seven, those dull months when you started biting your nails and took up fidget-spinning?

Well that break has put the HBO epic out of the running at this year’s Emmy Awards, which air Sunday night on CBS with host Stephen Colbert. As a result, there are plenty of newcomers in the drama categories, most notably “The Handmaid’s Tale,” “Westworld,” “Stranger Things,” and their lead and supporting actors. And, as loyal Emmy viewers know, newcomers can make those protracted Emmy nights far more intriguing and bearable. When a first-time nominee seemingly comes out of nowhere to win, as Rami Malek of “Mr. Robot” and Louie Anderson of “Baskets” did last year, it can cast a spell over the night.

This year may turn out to be a politically-tinged one for the Emmys, not in terms of acceptance speeches calling out the president — although we can probably expect some of those — but in terms of what wins. “Saturday Night Live” got a striking 22 nominations this year, thanks to a creative resurgence grounded in memorable political satire by Alec Baldwin as Donald Trump, Kate McKinnon as Hillary Clinton, and Melissa McCarthy as Sean Spicer. Surely voters are eager to read Trump’s tetchy Twitter response if Baldwin gets the gold.

This year may also signal a fundamental shift in the show’s emphasis, as the once bland limited-series categories have become just as competitive and exciting as the drama categories. It’s not only that big names and Oscar winners are up against one another, including Nicole Kidman, Robert De Niro, and Jessica Lange; it’s that all of the contenders are quite deserving. Hey, how’s that for a twist?

“House of Cards” Netflix

“Stranger Things” Netflix

“This Is Us” NBC

“Westworld” HBO

Will win: Because “Game of Thrones” isn’t eligible this year, the field is wide open. And I’m thinking that will help push “This Is Us” into the winner’s circle. In this moment of social and political unrest, voters may be drawn to a show about healing, and one with an inventive narrative structure at that. Plus, tons of feels. “Stranger Things” is the underdog, a streaming series that became last summer’s sensation.

Should win: Hands down, “The Handmaid’s Tale.” The timing is just right, sadly, as Margaret Atwood’s dystopian vision of misogyny didn’t seem alien enough. But more than that, the show was constructed beautifully, and the acting was superb all around. Oh, and by the way, phooey on voters for skipping the powerful “13 Reasons Why,” whose controversial portrayal of teen suicide and rape might have scared them off.

LEAD ACTRESS IN A DRAMA

Viola Davis “How to Get Away With Murder,” ABC

Claire Foy “The Crown,” Netflix

Elisabeth Moss “The Handmaid’s Tale,” Hulu

Keri Russell “The Americans,” FX

Evan Rachel Wood “Westworld,” HBO

Robin Wright “House of Cards,” Netflix

Will win: After six previous nominations for her deserving work in “Mad Men” and “Top of the Lake,” Moss will finally go home with the statue.

Should win: Moss brought heart and soul to the story, showing us her character’s hidden thoughts with small eye movements and cold stares. She was a tower of resistance. Foy brought inner life to the inscrutable Queen Elizabeth, but Moss gets my vote.

LEAD ACTOR IN A DRAMA

Sterling K. Brown “This Is Us,” NBC

Anthony Hopkins “Westworld,” HBO

Bob Odenkirk “Better Call Saul,” AMC

Matthew Rhys “The Americans,” FX

Liev Schreiber “Ray Donovan,”

Showtime

Kevin Spacey “House of Cards,” Netflix

Milo Ventimiglia “This Is Us,” NBC

Will win: Often multiple nominees from the same cast will cancel one another out. But this year, that may not happen. In this case, Brown, who won a supporting actor Emmy last year for “The People v. O.J. Simpson,” makes his character, Randall, transparent, so that we can see into his big heart.

Should win: Rhys deserves notice for his turn as an undercover action anti-hero (or is he a hero?) and dad. But a Brown win wouldn’t be a tragedy.

COMEDY SERIES

“Atlanta” FX

“Black-ish” ABC

“Master of None” Netflix

“Modern Family” ABC

“Silicon Valley” HBO

“Veep” HBO

“Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt”

Netflix

Will win: Emmy voters like to affirm their previous winners with more wins; just ask “Modern Family.” That’s why two-time winner “Veep” will take the gold, after a season showing Selina out of office. If there’s an upset, it might be by “Black-ish,” for some on-point episodes including one about the election of Donald Trump (who tweeted about the show in 2014, “racist at the highest level?”).

Should win: I was thoroughly charmed by season two of “Master of None,” and I’d be happy to see it win. But “Atlanta” was the year’s most impressive comedy, with touches of surrealism blended with astute character drama and cultural humor. Sadly, voters failed to note Issa Rae’s “Insecure,” Pamela Adlon’s “Better Things,” or Phoebe Waller-Bridge’s “Fleabag,” a trio of fresh and insightful comedies about women.

LEAD ACTRESS IN A COMEDY

Pamela Adlon “Better Things,” FX

Jane Fonda “Grace and Frankie,” Netflix

Allison Janney “Mom,” CBS

Ellie Kemper “Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt,” Netflix

Julia Louis-Dreyfus “Veep,” HBO

Tracee Ellis Ross “Black-ish,” ABC

Lily Tomlin “Grace and Frankie,”

Netflix

Will win: Is it possible that Louis-Dreyfus won’t win? No. And it’s hard to argue. Ross does wonderful work, and she could sneak in from behind if the voters are feeling open-minded.

Should win: Pamela Adlon is a force of nature as a single mother of three. And yet she’s remarkably subtle in the role.

LEAD ACTOR IN A COMEDY

Anthony Anderson “Black-ish,” ABC

Aziz Ansari “Master of None,” Netflix

Zach Galifianakis “Baskets,” FX

Donald Glover “Atlanta,” FX

William H. Macy “Shameless,”

Showtime

Jeffrey Tambor “Transparent,”

Amazon

Will win: It’s close, between two-time winner Tambor for his transcendent turn and Glover for his low-key, generous work. I’m thinking voters will go off-script here and anoint Glover, who won a Golden Globe for the role earlier this year.

Should win: I feel like a sap for singing the same song over and over again, because it will never happen. But, like most of the cast of “Shameless,” Macy is indelible in this role.

LIMITED SERIES

“Big Little Lies” HBO

“Fargo” FX

“Feud: Bette and Joan” FX

“Genius” National Geographic

“The Night Of” HBO

Will win: This is the most quality-stuffed category of the year. But the prize will go to David E. Kelley’s “Big Little Lies,” a dishy, dark, imaginatively directed, and socially aware story focusing on the power of women working together.

Should win: And “Big Little Lies” ought to win, although “The Night Of” was remarkable, “Feud” was endless Old Hollywood fun, and “Fargo” was solid, if not as superb as season two.

LEAD ACTRESS IN A LIMITED SERIES OR MOVIE

Carrie Coon “Fargo,” FX

Felicity Huffman “American Crime,” ABC

Nicole Kidman “Big Little Lies,” HBO

Jessica Lange “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Susan Sarandon “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Reese Witherspoon “Big Little Lies,” HBO

Will win: Kidman reminded us of her enormous power here, with a raw and riveting turn as an abused wife. She will win.

Should win: And Kidman deserves to win, for drawing our sympathy but not our pity. Lange blew my mind as Joan Crawford, a seamless performance. But Kidman broke my heart.

LEAD ACTOR IN A LIMITED SERIES

OR MOVIE

Riz Ahmed “The Night Of,” HBO

Benedict Cumberbatch “Sherlock: The Lying Detective,” PBS

Robert De Niro “The Wizard of Lies,” HBO

Ewan McGregor “Fargo,” FX

Geoffrey Rush “Genius,” National Geographic

John Turturro “The Night Of,” HBO

Will win: De Niro, with his star power and his commitment to doing an upcoming Amazon series, will win this strong category. And De Niro did do a good job of projecting the shadings of Bernie Madoff’s slippery personality.

Should win: Ahmed was a revelation as Naz, toying with our perceptions of a murder suspect who gets corrupted by the system. He’s my choice, although Turturro, too, blew the stereotypes out of the water as his lawyer (a role originally intended for James Gandolfini).

SUPPORTING ACTRESS IN A DRAMA

Uzo Aduba “Orange Is the New Black,” Netflix

Millie Bobby Brown “Stranger Things,” Netflix

Ann Dowd “The Handmaid’s Tale,”

Hulu

Chrissy Metz “This Is Us,” NBC

Thandie Newton “Westworld,” HBO

Samira Wiley “The Handmaid’s Tale,” Hulu

Will win: Newton will win, as the sole vote of confidence for the complex, culty “Westworld.” Possible upset: The charming Brown, whose Eleven was the heart of ”Stranger Things.”

Should win: Dowd, no question. If you’ve seen “Handmaid’s Tale,” and you hear the words “Aunt Lydia,” you shudder.


SUPPORTING ACTOR IN A DRAMA

Jonathan Banks “Better Call Saul,” AMC

Ron Cephas Jones “This Is Us,” NBC

David Harbour “Stranger Things,”

Netflix

Michael Kelly “House of Cards,”

Netflix

John Lithgow “The Crown,” Netflix

Mandy Patinkin “Homeland,”

Showtime

Jeffrey Wright “Westworld,” HBO

Will win: Lithgow will take this one. The Emmys love him — he has won five across his career — and this may be the only way for voters to express their praise for the excellent “The Crown.”

Should win: I’ve been surprisingly moved by Jones, who does sentiment better than anyone else on the sappy “This Is Us.” His death episode was pitch perfect. But Lithgow deserves this one, for his definitive, entertaining, and pathos-filled turn as Winston Churchill.

SUPPORTING ACTRESS IN A COMEDY

Vanessa Bayer “Saturday Night Live,” NBC

Anna Chlumsky “Veep,” HBO

Kathryn Hahn “Transparent,” Amazon

Leslie Jones “Saturday Night Live,” NBC

Judith Light “Transparent,” Amazon

Kate McKinnon “Saturday Night Live,” NBC

Will win: Gee, I think the voters liked “SNL” this year, don’t you? The show got 22 nominations, and Dave Chappelle and Melissa McCarthy won best guest actors last weekend. McKinnon will win again, primarily for her Hillary Clinton, an impersonation that spoke to the absurdities of the campaign and the final outcome.

Should win: Light had a memorable season, as her Shelly came out of her role as ex-wife, mother, and girlfriend and into her own. Added plus: Her killer version of “Hand in My Pocket.”

SUPPORTING ACTOR IN A COMEDY

Louie Anderson “Baskets,” FX

Alec Baldwin “Saturday Night Live,” NBC

Tituss Burgess “Unbreakable Kimmy Schmidt,” Netflix

Ty Burrell “Modern Family,” ABC

Tony Hale “Veep,” HBO

Matt Walsh “Veep,” HBO

Will win: Again, it’s “SNL” night. Also, imagine President Trump’s response if an ugly impersonation of him, one he has said “stinks,” took home the prize. Yup, Baldwin’s the one. Possible upset: scene stealer Tituss Burgess.

Should win: Baldwin, for putting a distinct, and distinctly grotesque, spin on a man who has been over-impersonated.

SUPPORTING ACTRESS IN A LIMITED SERIES OR MOVIE

Judy Davis “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Laura Dern “Big Little Lies,” HBO

Jackie Hoffman “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Regina King “American Crime,” ABC

Michelle Pfeiffer “The Wizard of Lies,” HBO

Shailene Woodley “Big Little Lies,” HBO


Will win: Dern is funny as the mean-girl mom, but ultimately poignant. She’ll prevail.

Should win: Since Jeannie Berlin wasn’t nominated for her fierce performance in “The Night Of,” I’m going with Dern.

SUPPORTING ACTOR IN A LIMITED

SERIES OR MOVIE

Bill Camp “The Night Of,” HBO

Alfred Molina “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Alexander Skarsgard “Big Little Lies,” HBO

David Thewlis “Fargo,” FX

Stanley Tucci “Feud: Bette and Joan,” FX

Michael K. Williams “The Night Of,” HBO

Will win: Skarsgard was deeply disturbing as the hot husband who turns out to be an abusive nightmare. I hate using the “brave performance” cliché, but he was profoundly unlikable. Without his committed work, Kidman might not have achieved such heights.

Should win: Thewlis was masterful as the epitome of ugliness and evil, but still, Skarsgard gets my vote.

VARIETY TALK SERIES

“Full Frontal With Samantha Bee” TBS

“Jimmy Kimmel Live” ABC

“Last Week Tonight With John Oliver” HBO

“The Late Late Show With James Corden” CBS

“The Late Show With Stephen Colbert” CBS

“Real Time With Bill Maher” HBO

Will win: It’s a close call between Bee and Oliver, but I think the balance will tip toward Oliver, who won last year.

Should win: Bee is sharp, and Colbert, too. They make life funnier in these troubled times. But Oliver goes deeper than either of them, as he forges his potent union of comedy, reporting, and outrage.
