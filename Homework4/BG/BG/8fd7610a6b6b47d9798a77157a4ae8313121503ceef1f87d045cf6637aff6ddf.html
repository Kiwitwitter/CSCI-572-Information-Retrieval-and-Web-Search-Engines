DAN SHAUGHNESSY

Superdome again scene of a Tom Brady success story

JIM DAVIS/GLOBE STAFF

Tom Brady completed 30 of 39 passes for a whopping 447 yards on Sunday.

By Dan Shaughnessy Globe Columnist  September 17, 2017

NEW ORLEANS — Any place that Saints call home has to be sacred space.

The Superdome qualifies. So many things have happened here since the building opened as the world’s largest fixed-dome structure in 1975.

This is where Pistol Pete Maravich played home games and where Muhammad Ali registered his last victory in the ring. It’s where North Carolina freshman Michael Jordan canned a jumper to win the NCAA championship and where George Herbert Walker Bush was nominated for president. It’s where Rick Pitino took Providence to the Final Four and where Beyonce caused a power surge that blew out the lights during a halftime show. It’s where thousands of hurricane victims lived after Katrina ravaged the city of New Orleans in 2006.

Oh, and some football happened here, too. Seven Super Bowls have been played in the 76,468-seat space-ship theater they now call the “Mercedes-Benz Superdome.’’ This includes three ultimate games that involved your New England Patriots.

This is where the Fridge scored a touchdown and the Bears crushed the Patriots in New England’s only Kraft-less Super Bowl. This is where Desmond Howard kept returning kicks before the Packers beat the Drew Bledsoe Patriots in Super Bowl XXXI. And Bill Parcells didn’t fly home with the team.

The Superdome morphed into a New England football holy land in February of 2002 when 24-year-old Tom Brady orchestrated one of the greatest upsets in Super Bowl history, directing the winning drive while John Madden was telling the world that QB12 needed to take a knee and let the clock run out.

Accompanied by thousands of pilgrimaging Patriots fans, Brady returned to the scene of his first finest hours Sunday and completed 30 of 39 passes for a whopping 447 yards and three touchdowns in a 36-20 rout of the defenseless Saints. It was the third-most passing yards of Brady’s career and gave him the top passing day in the history of NFL 40-year-old quarterbacks (Brett Favre once threw for 446).

“It was four quarters of great competition,’’ said Brady. “We got off to a great start. It was a good win.’’

Brady no doubt heard the whispers after his poor start in New England’s 42-27 Opening Night defeat in Foxborough. Playing his first game as a 40-year-old, he completed only 16 of 36 passes for 267 yards and zero touchdowns. This triggered the usual hot-take debate around the country that Brady and the Patriots were finally showing signs of decline. You know the drill. Father Time is undefeated. There’s a reason people don’t keep playing at a high level into their 40s. Avocado ice cream can’t make up for being born in 1977. We all underestimated the loss of Julian Edelman.

Brady never got down, but he contributed to some of the doubt with a postgame news conference in which he questioned the “attitude” and “competitiveness” of his team.

The performance and those buzzwords guaranteed that we’d watch closely when Brady and the Patriots’ offense went back to work in Week 2. The announcement that Danny Amendola (head injury) wouldn’t be playing contributed to the negative narrative that the midnight hour might be nearing for Tom and the Patriots.

And then, just 3 miles from Ponce de Leon Street in New Orleans, we saw a performance suggesting that Brady really has found the fountain of youth, and its name is Alex Guerrero. Just two days before the official release of “The TB12 Method: How To Achieve a Lifetime of Sustained Peak Performance’’ (I already have mine on the nightstand), Brady went out and shredded the Saints with a 30-point first half in which he completed 19 of 25 passes for 302 yards, three touchdowns, and a quarterback rating of 155.3. He threw three touchdown passes in the first quarter for the first time in his career.

Asked about his questioning of the team’s attitude and competitiveness after the Game 1 loss, Brady acknowledged, “I think all of the veterans had a chance to say the things they wanted to say to their different groups. I think everyone was really . . . the NFL is tough, man. You can’t take anything for granted. In order to win you’ve got to go out there and compete. We did a good job on that today.’’

Prior to Sunday’s easy win (it came at some cost as Rob Gronkowski apparently injured his groin in the second half), Brady’s last two appearances vs. the Saints had been memorable. In 2013, just hours before David Ortiz’s season-changing ALCS grand slam vs. the Tigers, Brady led a dramatic comeback in Foxborough against the Saints. Back in 2009, the Patriots got waxed, 38-17, in their first Superdome appearance since beating the Rams in the Super Bowl in 2002.

He’s played only three games in this place. One in his 20s, one in his 30s, and now one in his 40s. He says he’s now smarter, more pliable, and yes, even faster than he was when he first shocked the pro football world in this same building.

Just as he did in 2002, Brady sent thousands of thirsty New Englanders back out to the French Quarter with extra bounce in their steps.

“I hope they’re all out on Bourbon Street,’’ Brady said. “It’s a good road game to come to.’’
