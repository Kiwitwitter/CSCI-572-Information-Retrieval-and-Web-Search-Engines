FALL TV PREVIEW

Sexy, savvy, and full of laughs: 8 new TV shows not to miss

HBO via AP

James Franco plays twin brothers in “The Deuce.”

By Matthew Gilbert Globe Staff  September 21, 2017

Once upon a time, fall was the season when prime-time TV delivered its harvest. Once upon a time, too, there were phone booths and tube TVs.

In the new world, new TV shows premiere year-round, on the networks, the cable channels, and the streaming services. We live in the land of Peak TV, where, just when you thought you’d caught up on everything, a season of some new It Show appears on your to-do list.

Advertisement

But still, the networks continue to throw a big bunch of series at the wall each fall, to see if any of them stick. It suits their business model, and it gets them tons of free fall-preview promotion. Last year, a few network shows did indeed stick, including CBS’s “Bull,” ABC’s “Speechless,” and NBC’s “This Is Us,” the hit that is trying to break your heart. At the same time, cable, with HBO’s “Westworld,” and streaming, with Netflix’s “The Crown,” released some successful titles.

This year’s list of fall shows includes a few possible hits and a few good shows, too. Netflix’s “Alias Grace” is based on a Margaret Atwood novel, and it’s as dark as it is memorable. Showtime’s “White Famous” is a comedy about race and Hollywood that allows “Saturday Night Live” alum Jay Pharoah to show some of his range. And ABC’s “Ten Days in the Valley” is a payday for the Kyra Sedgwick fan club, of which I am a member.

Here’s a selected sampling of what’s coming your way.

“Ten Days in the Valley”

(Sunday, Oct. 1, ABC)

There’s nothing particularly original about this mystery-thriller, in which a little girl disappears in the middle of the night and the list of suspects includes the mother, the estranged father, and a few others. It’s on my to-watch list, though, for a few reasons. One is Kyra Sedgwick as the stressed mother with secrets, Jane Sadler; she always engages me. Another is the supporting cast, including Adewale Akinnuoye-Agbaje as the suspicious detective. And the third is Jane’s professional life. She plays the overburdened writer on a TV crime drama, which gives everything a nice meta-tweak.

Jan Thijs/Netflix

“Alias Grace”

Advertisement

(Friday, Nov. 3, Netflix)

Yes, another haunting Margaret Atwood adaptation. While the Emmy-winning “The Handmaid’s Tale” looks into a darkly imagined future, this limited series goes back to 1866 to take on the true story of a young Toronto woman jailed for double-murder. The first episode is riveting, as Grace (the exceptional Sarah Gadon) recounts her Dickensian past to an American psychiatrist and — as is wont to happen in therapy — the truth gets muddier and muddier before it gets clearer. Written by Sarah Polley (“Away From Her”) and directed by Mary Harron (“I Shot Andy Warhol”), this is beautifully acted and carefully structured.

“The Deuce”

(Sundays at 9 p.m., HBO)

This show premiered earlier this month, but I’m including it because it’s the best new drama of the season. From David Simon and George Pelecanos, who worked together on “The Wire” and “Treme,” “The Deuce” is a deep dive into the sex industry in New York’s Times Square in 1971. James Franco is the show’s solid anchor as an ambitious bar manager and his gambler twin brother, and Maggie Gyllenhaal is captivating as a hooker who see business potential in the world of porn. The frankly portrayed subjects, including the physical, psychological, and financial exploitation of women, are difficult, but the storytelling is transporting. HBO has already ordered a second season.

Brandon Hickman/Hulu

“Future Man”

(Tuesday, Nov. 14, Hulu)

Honestly, sometimes I can laugh just looking at Eliza Coupe’s face. The former star of “Happy Endings” seems to have wryness built into her physiognomy. She’s fun to watch as an apocalyptic warrior in this lighthearted sci-fi comedy series from Seth Rogen and Evan Goldberg. The story follows Josh Hutcherson’s Josh Futterman, a maintenance man and video game addict who finds himself in a race to save humanity with two time-traveling characters from his favorite game. The farce can be excessive, but the charm of the cast, including Ed Begley Jr. and the late Glenne Headly, makes it all easy to watch.

Craig Sjodin/ABC

“The Mayor”

(Tuesday, Oct. 3, ABC)

The premise is simultaneously silly and, at a time when a reality TV star is president, totally not silly. An aspiring rap artist played by Brandon Micheal Hall runs for mayor in his California hometown as a promotional gimmick. But yeah, he gets elected and realizes that governing and rapping may have things in common. I liked the cast a lot, including Hall, Lea Michele, and Yvette Nicole Brown from “Community” as his mother, and the pace is spirited. As with all of these shows, I can only speak to the pilot, which in this case is thoroughly likable. Where the comedy goes from there is anyone’s guess.

“Mindhunter”

(Friday, Oct. 13, Netflix)

Unfortunately, Netflix did not have any episodes available for preview, but I am quite eager to see this one. The based-in-fact serial killer drama is from director and “House of Cards” producer David Fincher, and it’s set in 1979. The story follows two FBI agents, played by Jonathan Groff and Holt McCallany, as they study imprisoned serial killers — a phrase one of the agents invents — in order to learn how they think. Based on Mark Olshaker and John Douglas’s 1995 book “Mind Hunter: Inside FBI’s Elite Serial Crime Unit,” the show has already been renewed for a second season.

Michael Desmond/SHOWTIME

“White Famous”

(Sunday, Oct. 15, Showtime)

From the creator of “Californication,” Tom Kapinos, this comedy gives Jay Pharoah a chance to show his range after his years on “Saturday Night Live.” He plays stand-up comic Floyd Mooney, who is being pushed out of all-black-audience entertainment and into mainstream fare. “The Goal has always been to be white famous,” his agent, played by Utkarsh Ambudkar, reminds him. Like “Entourage” and “Californication,” the show satirizes Hollywood types and features a few comically excruciating scenes of white discomfort with black people. But it also has a tender side, as Mooney moons over his ex-girlfriend and their young son. It’s based on the experiences of executive producer Jamie Foxx, who appears as a twisted version (I think) of himself.

Chris Haston/NBC

“Will & Grace”

(Thursday, Sept. 28, NBC)

NBC has not previewed episodes for critics, so I can’t say whether or not this reboot will work. But I’m eager to try it. I loved “Will & Grace” for most of its run — loved it, despite the flaws and the fact that it stayed on the air too long. It successfully mixed up then-contemporary ideas about gender and sexual orientation with an almost vaudeville-like sitcom tone. And it helped to open up viewers’ minds along the way. No, I’m not a fan of TV rehash, which is very popular these days with “Fuller House” and the returns of “Gilmore Girls” and “Arrested Development.” And I can’t imagine what the show will take on as its big themes. But I’m all eyes and ears.
