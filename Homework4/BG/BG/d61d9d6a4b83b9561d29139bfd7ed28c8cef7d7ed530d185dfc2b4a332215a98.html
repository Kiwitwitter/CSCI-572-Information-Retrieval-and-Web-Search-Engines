Big Boi brings Stankonia Studios’ sound to Cambridge

    Share via e-mail

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Julian Benbow Globe Staff  December 09, 2015

The journey seven light years below sea level to the center of the earth to the place from which all things come started with a bus ride.

Antwan Patton wasn’t the slick, streetwise rhymer in the gator belts and furs with the flow that bounced off syllables like pinballs and padded walls. He wasn’t yet Big Boi. Andre Benjamin wasn’t the mind-bending experimentalist Andre 3000, who shape-shifted personas and rhyme patterns as he pleased. Together they weren’t one of hip-hop’s most influential forces. They weren’t Outkast. They were two teenage friends from Tri-Cities High School trying to get someone to hear their raps.

Before they stepped into their spaceship, they took the MARTA bus to Atlanta’s studio row.

Atlanta was an emerging hotbed in the early ’90s, the Motown of the South, with labels like LaFace and studios like Doppler, Tree Sound, and Curtis Mayfield’s home studio Curtom, dotting the city and churning out hits. At the time, Bobby Brown had a studio of his own, Bosstown Recordings, tucked on the northside of the city.

“Me and Dre used to catch the bus up there just hoping that we’d see Bobby Brown or somebody could hear us rap,” Big Boi said. “We would never see anybody.”

Eventually, they worked their way into the building. They were discovered by the production team Organized Noize — Rico Wade, Ray Murray, and Sleepy Brown — that had been working with some of LaFace’s artists, including TLC. That was how the door to the building ultimately opened.

“It was the first studio we ever recorded in,” said Big Boi, who comes to the Sinclair in Cambridge for a free Converse Rubber Tracks show on Dec. 15. (Tickets are no longer available.) “When Organized Noize was working on the ‘What About Your Friends’ remix with TLC, we were like the young guys on the squad, and they wanted to put some raps on the remix. So that was the first commercial release we ever did.”

The studio would become the laboratory for nearly all of Outkast’s work, and years later the group had an opportunity to make it officially its own.

On the wave of the critical acclaim that came with its third album, “Aquemini,” Outkast was in the market for its own studio when the duo bumped into Brown at a concert in North Carolina. It came up in conversation that Bosstown was for sale.

“He was like, ‘Hey, man! Ya’ll can have it!,’ ” Big Boi recalled.

They couldn’t believe it and ran it by their manager.

“Our manager was like, ‘Man, get out of here, man. Bobby just [messing] with ya’ll,’ ” Big Boi said.

But they took Brown at his word.

“We were like, ‘Nah, nah. He said we could have it.’ ”

When they got back to Atlanta, they looked into the building and learned it was in foreclosure. They put the money up to buy it, and from there the door to a new universe — where the grass at Atlanta’s notorious Bowen Homes projects was purple and the pavement was green — opened. They had their own language to describe the grooves they would come up with — it was something beyond funky, something “stank.”

“We made it into Stankonia,” Big said.

Stankonia Studios gave birth to Outkast’s fourth album, “Stankonia,” a meteor shower of sounds that burned through hip-hop conventions and put Outkast into the center of pop culture. The album spawned the frantic lead single “B.O.B.,” its infectious follow-up “Ms. Jackson,” and the slippery final single, “So Fresh, So Clean.”

Big Boi knew they were working with bottled lightning. They used instruments and musicians like beakers and flasks. One day, Big Boi came into the studio and heard Andre 3000 working on intergalactic synths and a breakneck drum pattern that sounded like they were pulled from drum-and-bass pioneer Roni Size. It was the beginnings of “B.O.B.”

“I walked into the room, and it’s like the room was glowing,” Big Boi said. “It was crazy.”

Andre 3000 had already laid a spitfire verse. Big Boi jumped into the booth immediately. He took the song home to play for his kids.

“They were going crazy,” he said. “So I called Dre and I said, I think we got one.”

Later they were tweaking what would eventually become “Ms. Jackson,” but wanted an added element. They brought in bassist Aaron Mills.

“It was storming raining,” Big Boi said. “And he got to thumping that bass, man. The room started glowing again. After that moment, I knew that this was the place where I was going to always record at.”

The studio became a symbol of Atlanta, a place where talent, ideas, and hit records were constantly percolating.

“Everybody that you know from T.I. to Ludacris to Lil’ John to TLC to Whitney Houston to Tony Braxton, you name it,” Big Boi said. “Everybody’s worked out of Stankonia.”

Stankonia’s imprint is what attracted Converse Rubber Tracks to it earlier this year, when it launched a global project that would open up 12 of the most famous studios in eight countries to emerging artists.

“Each of the participating studios within the program have played an integral role in shaping music history, with the artists who have recorded there redefining their respective genres,” said Converse global music marketing director Jed Lewis. “With Stankonia, it was no question. The extraordinary talent and music that have come from that studio spans decades, so it was a natural fit.”

The partnership placed the studio, which Big Boi and Dre rode the bus to as kids, in the same company as such music landmarks as the Beatles’ Abbey Road in London and Sunset Sound in Los Angeles, where Prince recorded “Purple Rain.”

“It’s definitely one of the wonders of the world,” Big Boi said. “When they first came with the idea, we were absolutely honored to be considered amongst those studios. For them to come through and consider Stankonia one of those pivotal points was just real cool.”

Each studio, including the Converse Rubber Tracks studio that opened in Lovejoy Wharf in July, opened its doors in September, offering producers, engineers, and expertise to 84 artists from around the world over a two-week span. Big Boi says he learned as much as he taught. He sat in on a session with Chilean rap fireball Planta Carnivora. Even if they were speaking different languages, Big Boi said he could catch her vibe.

“It was dope, man,” he said. “She had some real revolutionary music. I didn’t know what she was saying, but the way she was rapping and singing it was just hardcore. Then they were explaining to me what she was saying, I was like, ‘Damn.’ It was really pro-people and anti-establishment. And I was like, this goes on all over the world.”

Last year, Outkast toured the world in a run of festivals timed to the 20th anniversary of the group’s first album, “Southernplayalisticadillacmuzik.” Once it was done, Big Boi bought Andre 3000’s half of the studio, giving him full ownership. At any given moment, he can still walk around Stankonia, take a peek into one of the four production rooms, and catch the same glow.

“A lot of good music came out of there,” he said. “A lot of good music is still coming out of there. It’s our home, and it’s the place where all the funky things come from.”
