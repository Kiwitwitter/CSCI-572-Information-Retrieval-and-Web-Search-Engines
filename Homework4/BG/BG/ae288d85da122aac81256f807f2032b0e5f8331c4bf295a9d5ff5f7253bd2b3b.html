Turkey summons another US consulate employee as visa crisis deepens

ADEM ALTAN/AFP/Getty Images

A man walked in front of the US Embassy in Ankara, Turkey, on Monday as the United States and Turkey mutually suspended visa services.

By Kareem Fahim Washington Post  October 09, 2017

ISTANBUL — Turkish prosecutors said Monday that they had summoned an employee of the United States consulate in Istanbul to testify in a criminal matter, only days after the government’s arrest of another US consulate employee set off a diplomatic feud between the United States and Turkey.

Turkish authorities provided few details about the latest warrant, and a spokesman for the US Embassy in Ankara declined to comment on the case. But with its timing, the summons amounted to another provocation in an accelerating crisis that has raised concerns about the future of their alliance.

The latest episode started on Sunday, when the US Embassy announced that it was immediately suspending the issuing of nonimmigrant visas at its missions in Turkey.

The embassy statement cited security concerns, but the surprise move was widely seen as a response to Turkey’s arrest last week of Metin Topuz, a Turkish employee of the US consulate in Istanbul, on espionage charges.

Turkey’s embassy in Washington responded hours later with identical restrictions — suddenly upending the plans of countless Turkish and American tourists, students, businesspeople, and others who did not already possess the necessary visas for travel.

The spiraling argument has exposed deep divides between the government of President Recep Tayyip Erdogan and the Trump administration and undermined months of effort by both sides to paper over their differences with public expressions of solidarity.

The arguments — about the war in Syria and more recently over the detention of Turkish and American citizens — have burst into public view with a litany of official recriminations and increasingly provocative actions.

The US-imposed travel restrictions only affected new visas issued in Turkey, and did not prevent Turkish citizens for applying for visas at US missions in other countries, or restrict travel to the United States by Turks already holding visas, officials said.

Even so, the news sent the Turkish currency tumbling and prompted Turkish officials and a leading business association on Monday to urge that the restrictions be lifted.

‘‘The economic, social, cultural, and academic relations, as well as citizens of the two countries that are in conflict — who are not the ones responsible — will suffer,’’ said TUSAID, a business association.

An official at Turkey’s foreign ministry said that beginning Monday evening, American citizens would be prevented from obtaining visas on arrival at Turkey’s airports. An online system for obtaining visas was shut down Sunday night.

The restrictions would also prevent the issuance of new visas for longer term stays typically sought by academics, journalists, and scientists, the official said.

Turkish officials have linked their legal pursuit of the US consulate employees to their ongoing investigation into Fethullah Gulen, a Turkish cleric living in exile in Pennsylvania.

Turkey has accused Gulen of coordinating an attempted coup against the government in July 2016 and demanded his extradition from the United States. Tens of thousands of people have been arrested by the Turkish authorities in a post-coup crackdown, including Gulen’s followers as well as ordinary critics of the government.

Turkish prosecutors have accused Topuz of having links to Gulen. He was formally arrested on charges of espionage, trying to overthrow the government, and acting against the constitution.

A prosecutor’s statement carried by local news outlets on Monday said the second US consulate employee received the summons on suspicion of belonging to Gulen’s movement.

It did not say whether the employee, who was referred to by the initials N.M.C., was facing arrest but added that he did not enjoy diplomatic immunity.

The US Embassy condemned the charges against the Americans as baseless.

As relations between the NATO allies worsen, their longstanding military alliance is of particular concern. That pact has been vital to a US-led effort to expunge the Islamic State from neighboring Syria.

Some American drones and warplanes targeting Islamic State militants launch from Incirlik Air Base, not far from the Syrian border.

The base has also been used for resupply missions in Iraq and Afghanistan in the last decade, and could play a key role as more troops enter Afghanistan under the Pentagon’s new strategy to become more involved in operations there.

Eric Pahon, a Pentagon spokesman, said on Monday that the dispute has not affected military operations. Senior US officials, including Defense Secretary Jim Mattis and Secretary of State Rex Tillerson, have spent the last several months working to preserve the arrangement.

‘‘In fact,’’ Pahon said, ‘‘Secretary Mattis traveled to Turkey in August to meet with key government leadership and reaffirm our bilateral defense relationship.’’

An employee at the US consulate in Adana, Turkey, was arrested in March over similar accusations, but his case has not yet come to trial. The arrests have raised questions about the safety of all local employees of US diplomatic missions in Turkey.

Turkey has also detained dozens of foreign nationals on similar charges, including several Americans.

It has become clear that they are seen as potential bargaining chips in Ankara’s efforts to force the extradition of Gulen.

Erdogan has also expressed anger over the charges brought against 15 of his bodyguards over their use of violence against protesters in Washington in May, and over court cases against a former Cabinet minister and three others in a case of conspiracy to violate sanctions against Iran.

President Trump has praised Erdogan as a stalwart ally in the fight against terrorism. And he said last month after a meeting on the sidelines of the United Nations General Assembly that ties between the two countries were “as close as we’ve ever been.”
