In rapidly changing city, a close race for council

By Jill Terreri Ramos Globe Correspondent  September 01, 2017

There’s no race for mayor of Quincy this fall, but local voters inclined to skip the election will miss out on one of the most competitive City Council contests in years.

Seven candidates, five of whom are well-known political names in Quincy, are vying for three councilor at large seats. A preliminary election Sept. 12 will knock one off the ballot, and the other six will move on to the general election on Nov. 7. All registered voters in the city -- nearly 60,000 people out of some 93,700 residents -- can cast ballots for at large council seats.

“I think it will be very close -- particularly among the top four or five,” said state Senator John F. Keenan, a former Quincy councilor at large.

Though City Council races may not draw as many voters as a keen mayoral contest, Keenan said it’s important for people to pay attention.

“Local government has the most impact on people, and taxes as well,” he said.

Two incumbents, Nina Liang and Noel DiBona, both of whom have served single two-year terms as at large councilors, are running for reelection, while longtime incumbent Joe Finn has decided not to run again.

Voters are probably also familiar with three of their challengers: City Councilor Margaret Laforest, who represents Ward 1, which includes Merrymount and Hough’s Neck; former Quincy public works commissioner Daniel Raymondi, who has served as Ward 2 councilor and on the School Committee; and Anne Mahoney, a member of the School Committee who ran unsuccessfully for mayor in 2011 and 2015.

The other two candidates are Stephen Tougas, who opposed discussion about adopting sanctuary city status in Quincy, and Stanley Dong Fang Xiao, a Chinese immigrant who says he is interested in beautifying public spaces.

The crowded race comes as Quincy is experiencing explosive growth in its downtown and in some neighborhoods and as high-density housing built near local MBTA stations continues to draw commuters priced out of Boston and other more expensive markets. In the last few years, Quincy Center has attracted $400 million in infrastructure improvements and $220 million in private development projects, according to an MBTA bid document from mid-August and city data.

The pace of growth has fatigued some residents, who are concerned about traffic, rising taxes, and escalating real estate prices. The City Council can act as a backstop to question or check the mayoral administration on its plans, and to vote on major projects.

Merrymount resident Angie Walker, who has lived in Quincy for almost 20 years, said she is frustrated so many major developments appear to be a “done deal” before they are presented to the public. Interviewed following a recent candidates forum hosted by the Merrymount Association, Walker said that she believes there’s too much development in the city and that “traffic is horrendous.”

Similar concerns were reflected in questions to the seven candidates during the event on Merrymount Beach, which drew approximately 100 people.

In their remarks, the candidates tried to distinguish themselves from one another, highlighting their experience, their independence, or their ties to the community. Several agreed the city needs more commercial developments to offset the rising property taxes affecting homeowners.

Laforest pointed to her efforts to keep in touch with constituents as Ward 1 councilor, saying she would continue to do that as an at large councilor. She reminded her seaside neighbors that she worked to reduce the flood zone in Quincy, requiring fewer residents to buy flood insurance, and also spoke of her work to establish the city’s first dog park.

Raymondi, whose campaign literature features a laudatory quote from Mayor Thomas Koch, said though he accomplished much as commissioner of public works, he would like to be involved in setting policy. “We need to spend more money on our roads,” he said.

He spoke of his experience as a lawyer, his role in establishing community policing in Quincy, and how he fought for legislation to use local hotel and motel tax revenues to enhance parks and open space.

Mahoney said she doesn’t work in Quincy, and isn’t beholden to anyone in the city for her livelihood. Her background in business -- she is a marketing manager who has worked in business development -- and her willingness to question the administration would be tools to scrutinize city budgets and proposed tax rates, she said.

DiBona emphasized his support for public schools and his experience on the School Committee, and spoke about his plan to have a community dialogue on opioid abuse and the need to mitigate traffic problems.

Liang was asked about her failed resolution earlier this year calling for the City Council to explore whether it would be beneficial for Quincy to adopt sanctuary city status or something similar. She said that she felt it was her responsibility to raise the issue given the nationwide discussions about the treatment of immigrants, and that the council, which holds open meetings easily accessible to the public, would be the best forum for those conversations.

Liang also stressed her commitment to making government more transparent and accessible.

Tougas talked about his involvement with various causes at the state level, such as his opposition to a carbon tax and Common Core educational standards, and his support of an effort to repeal a law allowing transgender people to use public restrooms that correspond with their gender identity.

Xiao, a first-time candidate for public office, said he is focused on enhancing public spaces with landscaping and flowers to encourage more people to gather outside.

He said there isn’t enough for Quincy’s seniors to do, and said he would like to stimulate job creation and volunteer opportunities.

This is the first local election in Quincy in which there is no race for mayor, whose term was stretched from two to four years in 2015.

In addition to the three at large seats, the City Council also includes a seat for each of the six city wards. Preliminary elections will be held in wards 1, 5, and 6.

In Ward 1, where Laforest is not seeking reelection, four candidates are competing: Michael Falcione, Joseph Murphy, David McCarthy, and Daniel Davis.

In Ward 5, which includes part of Quincy Center and Wollaston, City Council President Kirsten Hughes is facing two challengers, John Rodophele and Michael Hurley.

In Ward 6, which includes North Quincy and Squantum, incumbent William Harris is facing two challengers, Herbert “Alie” Shaughnessy III and Steven Striffler. Harris was appointed to the seat in April 2016 following the death of Councilor Brian McNamee.
