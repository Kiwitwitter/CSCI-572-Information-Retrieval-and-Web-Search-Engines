8 ways to celebrate Frugal Fun Day

By Diane Bair and Pamela Wright Globe Correspondents  September 27, 2017

For some the idea of inexpensive or free fun is as unimaginable as a huge Trumpian chocolate cake with no calories — or head-shaking. But it is possible. In honor of International Frugal Fun Day on Sunday we set out to find some thrifty things to see, do, and explore in the region.

Timing is everything

Pay big bucks to get into Boston’s major museums? No, thank you. Boston’s Children’s Museum (308 Congress St., Boston, 617-426-6500, bostonchildrensmuseum.org) has $1 admission on Friday, 5-9 p.m. . . . The Institute for Contemporary Art Boston (25 Harbor Shore Drive, Boston, 617-478-3100, icaboston.org) is free on Thursday, 5-9 p.m. The trio of Harvard Art Museums, including Fogg Museum, Busch-Reisinger Museum, and Arthur M. Sackler Museum (32 Quincy St., Cambridge, 617-495-9400, harvardartmuseums.org) is free to Massachusetts residents on Saturdays, 10 a.m.-noon. It’s free at all times to Cambridge residents, kids under age 18, and some others. Check the website. . . . Harvard Museum of Natural History (26 Oxford St., Cambridge, 617-495-3045, hmnh.harvard.edu) is free to Massachusetts residents on Sundays, 9 a.m.-noon (year-round) and Wednesday, 3-5 p.m. (September-May). Also, check with your local library as many have discounted passes. . . . On Wednesday, 4-10 p.m., admission to the Museum of Fine Arts (465 Huntington Ave., Boston, 617-267-9300, mfa.org) is by voluntary contribution. Admission is free for folks age 17 and younger on weekdays after 3 p.m., weekends, and Boston public school holidays. There are also special free days, including the museum’s upcoming fall Open House, Oct. 9. Website has details.

Discover hidden gems

Advertisement

You’ll find rare documents, artifacts, and interactive exhibits tracing the history of Massachusetts at the not-to-be-missed Commonwealth Museum in the State Archives Building at Columbia Point (220 Morrissey Blvd., Boston, 617-727-9268, sec.state.ma.us/mus) This free-for-all treasure trove has a one-of-a-kind copper plate engraved by Paul Revere, the 1629 Charter that King Charles I issued to John Winthrop when he sailed from England, the Bill of Rights and the Constitution of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts written by John Adams, and the Massachusetts copy of the Declaration of Independence. . . . The always-free Museum Of Bad Art (781-444-6757, museumofbadart.org) is a hoot, showcasing pieces you won’t find in other museums, along with funny commentary. The collection includes some 600 works; see them online or view 20-30 displayed in the basement gallery of the Somerville Theater (55 Davis Square, Somerville, free with purchase of a theater ticket or request a pass from info@museumofbadart.org). There’s also a gallery at the New England Wildlife Center (500 Columbian St., South Weymouth), and in the lobby of the Brookline Interactive Group (46 Tappan St., Brookline).

Picnic on an island

Pack a lunch and head to Castle Island in South Boston (2010 William J. Day Blvd., Boston, 617-727-5290, mass.gov/locations/castle-island-pleasure-bay-m-street-beach-and-carson-beach). The 22-acre site boasts startling water and cityscape views, a historic fort, walking trails, a seaside boardwalk, a playground, picnic areas, and nearby beaches. Free guided tours of Fort Independence are offered Sunday, noon-3:30 p.m. through Columbus Day.

Star watch

Be dazzled by the night skies and learn about astronomy at Coit Observatory on the Boston University campus (725 Commonwealth Ave., Boston, 617-353-2630, bu.edu/astronomy/events/public-open-night-at-the-observatory/). Free, one-hour sessions are held most Wednesday evenings, 7:30 p.m., weather permitting. Guides are on hand to answer questions as you look for the Milky Way, planets, comets, and more, through powerful telescopes and binoculars.

Go for the cache

Who doesn’t enjoy a treasure hunt? Sign up for free (geocaching.com) or download the geocaching app to see a list of geocaches hidden in your neighborhood and beyond. Choose the one you want to find (they’re rated by degree of difficulty), get out your phone or GPS, and start hunting. It’s a fun way to add a little mystery and challenge to your next walk.

Take a tour

On a Free Tours by Foot tour of Boston (42 Robinwood Ave., Boston, 617-299-0764, freetoursbyfoot.com) you decide what to pay when the two-hour, 1-mile guided walking tour concludes. Themes include a Beacon Hill Crime Tour (murder and mayhem; best for ages 16 and up), the Freedom Trail, the North End, and Boston Strong: Fire, Flood and Famine. . . . Hop aboard the USS Constitution, the world’s oldest commissioned warship afloat (Charlestown Navy Yard, Charlestown, 617-426-1812, ussconstitutionmuseum.org), for a free tour, and then pop into the adjacent museum. It’s free with most library passes, or pay what you can; suggested donation is $20-$25 per family, or $5-$10 per adult and $3-$5 per child. . . . You’ve witnessed the remarkable $78 million transformation of the Boston Public Library’s central library (700 Boylston St., Boston, 617-536-5400, bpl.org). Now go behind the scenes on a free Art and Architecture Tour. Guides lead tours of (primarily) the McKim Building and point out major artworks housed within. Tour times vary, but include weekends. No reservation required for parties of fewer than eight people. . . . Enjoy fabulous views of the city, on the cheap, from the observation deck on the 26th floor of the US Custom House (3 McKinley Square, Boston, 617-310-6300, marriott.com). Set on what was once Boston’s waterfront, this iconic building is now a Marriott property, but there’s public access daily (except Friday) at 2 and 6 p.m. ($7 and $10, respectively; evening price includes a soda or, for $17, an adult beverage.) The 360-degree vistas are way worth it. . . . The Massachusetts state insect is a ladybug — who knew? And what’s the deal with that “sacred” cod? Get a 30- to 45-minute overview of the history and architecture of that glorious gold-domed structure on a free guided tour of the Massachusetts State House (24 Beacon St., Boston, 617-727-3676, www.sec.state.ma.us/trs/trsgen/genidx.htm). Tours are offered weekdays, year-round, from 10 a.m. to 3:30 p.m.; advance reservations are requested.

Down on the farm

Advertisement

One of the best things about fall (besides pumpkin-spiced everything) is navigating a corn maze. These can get a little pricey if you’re bringing the gang, but we found one that delivers the goods — two mazes, farm animals, ice cream — for just $6 per person. (Kids age 3 and under are free.) It’s Beech Hill Farm and Ice Cream Barn, open through October (107 Beech Hill Road, Hopkinton, N.H., 603-223-0828, beechhillfarm.com). . . . Rabbits, sheep, pygmy goats, chickens, and a 900-pound pig named Big Darryl — these are among the critters you’ll encounter at the barnyard at Russell Orchards (143 Argilla Road, Ipswich, 978-356-5366, russellorchards.com). This beloved pick-your-own farm draws families galore on weekends, waiting for the biofueled hay wagon that goes out to the apple-picking fields. There’s also a pumpkin patch and a farm store-slash-winery with tasty cider doughnuts. On weekdays, the charge is $3 per person plus $1.95 per pound of apples; on weekends and holiday Mondays, pay $20 per 10-pound bag of PYO apples, hayride included. Seems pretty fair for a hit of wholesome country fun.

Jacked up

If you’re looking for a good excuse for a family road trip how’s this: On Oct. 21, from noon to 5 p.m., the Camp Sunshine Pumpkin Festival at L.L. Bean (95 Main St., Freeport, Maine, campsunshinepumpkinfestivals.org) lights up the town with jack-o’-lanterns. There’s pumpkin carving, games, music, and a costume parade, concluding with the lighting of the jack-o’-lanterns at 5 p.m. Admission is free, but you’ll want to adopt a pumpkin and spring for pumpkin-y treats, with proceeds benefiting Camp Sunshine, which provides retreats for children with life-threatening illnesses and their families.
