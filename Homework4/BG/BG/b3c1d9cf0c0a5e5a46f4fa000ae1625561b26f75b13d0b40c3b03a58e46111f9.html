Replay played a pivotal role in lopsided Revolution loss

miguel martinez/AP

Referee Baldomero Toledo (left), after consulting with the video official, red-carded Xavier Kouassi (center).

By Frank Dell’Apa Globe Correspondent  September 14, 2017

The Revolution have been touting the advantages of MLS implementing video review last month, calling for replays of potential “match-changing” incidents. But refereeing decisions influenced by the video assistant referee were costly to the Revolution in a 7-0 loss to Atlanta United Wednesday night.

The game got off to a rough start for the Revolution, who surrendered a second-minute goal to Josef Martinez. They appeared to be recovering before losing captain Xavier Kouassi to a 16th-minute red card, following a review.

The Revolution will play their next game, at Kansas City Saturday, without Kouassi and defender Antonio Delamea, who was ejected after a 38th-minute review. Both are suspended.

The result left the Revolution (10-13-5, 35 points) 6 points behind the New York Red Bulls for the sixth and final Eastern Conference playoff spot, with six matches remaining. But general manager Michael Burns remains optimistic, though he questioned the Kouassi ejection.

“We all went into this knowing there would be speed bumps with VAR,” Burns said Thursday. “You look at Tony’s denial of a goal-scoring opportunity, it’s difficult to make an argument there. With Kouassi, there’s a lot more gray area.

“The issue for me is, if that is always, going forward, going to be reviewed and issued a red card, OK. No two plays are identical, but there is no guarantee a similar incident will be reviewed. It was identified, reviewed, and a decision was made. And if that happens [again], maybe the decision is not to review it, not issue a red card.

“There’s a lot of subjectivity there — who’s on the monitor, who the referee is. If it’s a penalty in the box, that’s black and white.”

In the 14th minute, Kouassi clashed with Atlanta’s Yamil Asad, referee Baldomero Toledo awarding a free kick. Toledo was then summoned by the VAR staff, led by Ted Unkel, and two minutes later returned to the field, issuing Kouassi a red card as several Revolution players protested the decision.

“We’re still so new at this and, long term, it’s going to be far more beneficial than not,” Burns said. “Last night, some would say it was 100 percent a red card, some would say it was a foul, some a yellow. At the end of the day, it’s up to the ref. If in the next game a very similar situation is reviewed and it’s a red card, fine. But is it always going to be treated the same?”

Martinez scored on a 31st-minute penalty kick, and in the 36th minute, Toledo awarded another penalty kick. Toledo again left the playing field to view the replay, returning to red-card Delamea, leaving the Revolution with nine players. Martinez converted a 38th-minute penalty kick for a 3-0 advantage.

According to MLS, “match-changing incidents” involve goals, penalty kicks, straight red cards, and mistaken identity.

The video review can be called by the referee in the booth, and “the on-field decision will only be overturned if the video indicates a clear and obvious error in one of four match-changing incidents.”

Some have questioned the timing of instituting the VAR late in the season, instead of at the beginning. Several decisions have required long delays, interrupting the flow of matches.

“Arguably, this is one of the biggest initiatives in the history of the league,” Burns said. “Most places, any league in the world, it would commence at the beginning of season. We still have a learning curve and, for us, it’s in the middle of the season.

“I’m optimistic long-term. Timing is important, but it’s more important that the correct decision is made.”

The Revolution had closed within a point of the final playoff spot after a 1-0 win over the Montreal Impact Saturday. But Atlanta (12-8-6, 42 points), an expansion team competing in its first season, extended its edge with a 3-0 win over Dallas in the opening game at Mercedes-Benz Stadium on Sunday.

Atlanta’s seven-goal margin of victory tied the MLS record for a regular-season game. The Revolution, who became the first team to fail to take a shot in an MLS match, have sustained two 6-0 defeats, against Real Salt Lake on April 25, 2009, and in a playoff match against Chicago on Sept. 22, 2000.

“That was as close to a must-win as we’ve faced all year,” Burns said. “I can’t remember a game that as much went against us, and some of it was our own doing, obviously, in the first half, conceding an early goal, two penalty kicks, two red cards.

“Everything that could have gone wrong did go wrong. It was almost, words can’t describe. At halftime, down a bunch of goals, it was a bad night, an off night, for everyone. It’s disappointing.

“The good thing is the game was on a Wednesday and I’m glad we have a Saturday game. As a coach, a player, you put this out of your memory as soon as possible. We play Saturday, and I’m hopeful that’s a good thing.

“This had an impact on our chances but we’re still mathematically in it. We all realize the extent of the task in front of us. I can speak for the coaches and players, everyone is going to continue to fight until the very end. Crazier things have happened.

“We’re focusing on Kansas City now and trying to win that game and then focus on Toronto [Sept. 23]. We’ll need something extraordinary the last six games. That was a massive blow, but we’ll continue to fight.”
