Pass the oyster crackers. It’s time for Chowderfest in Ipswich

John P. Muldoon/The Local Ne.ws/File

Get your spoons ready for the Ipswich Chowderfest on Oct. 14 at the Downtown Riverwalk.

By Kathy Shiels Tully Globe Correspondent   October 06, 2017

CHOW(DER) DOWN If you didn’t get your fill of clam chowder during the summer, there’s still time to indulge.

The Ipswich Lions Club is holding its 22d annual Chowderfest from 11 a.m. to 4 p.m. on Saturday, Oct. 14, at the Downtown Riverwalk.

Advertisement

For $10 per person (children under age 10 are admitted free), you can sample tastings from several local restaurants, listen to music by the Orville Giddings Band, and pair that chowder with some fresh craft brew from the Ipswich Ale truck. There will be many activities for children, plus local crafters and vendors.

Funds raised by Lions Clubs support causes locally and globally. The Chowderfest supports the Ispwich club’s Eyesight Campaign, which provides free vision screening and collects eyeglasses to those in need around the world.

For information on buying tickets, go to e-clubhouse.org/sites/ipswich_ma.

MASKING THE MYSTERY How else to prepare for a costumed masquerade party than securing a unique, handcrafted mask, one that could rival those found at Rio’s Carnivale and New Orleans’ Mardi Gras?

Bidding has begun for the online auction of 14 masks created by Marblehead Arts Association artists to raise excitement and funds for its annual masquerade party.

Advertisement

This year, the masquerade takes place on the auspicious date of Friday, Oct. 13 in the hauntingly elegant King Hooper Mansion, 8 Hooper St.

What’s your fancy? A peacock-themed mask with feathers of blues and greens? Or a scarier skull mask designed to frighten? Or do you want to have an objet d’art to treasure for years to come?

Besides online, you can see the masks displayed in local businesses and retail shops.

To bid for your favorite mask, go to marbleheadarts.org. Tickets to the party ($35 in advance, $40 at the door) also can be purchased on the site.

OPEN BOOK Looking for a diverse and engaging series of author talks?

Check out the monthly program running through December at Temple Emanu-El in Haverhill, 514 Main St. Each program begins with a continental breakfast at 9:30 a.m. before the 10 a.m. talk.

Next up on Sunday, Oct. 29 is Eshkol Nevo and his new novel, “Three Floors Up,” which was released this month in English. In what Publishers Weekly calls a “transporting” and “multifaceted” story, Nevo examines a society in crisis through the turmoils, secrets, and problematic decisions of the residents of a Tel Aviv apartment building.

Start reading. Walk-ins are welcome, but to help with planning, you can contact the temple office at nancy@templeemanu-el.org. A donation of $10 per person per program is suggested. Visit templeemanu-el.org.

NATURE’S FLOOD INSURANCE Do you know what scientists are studying as a possible first line of defense against the worsening Atlantic coastal storms? Essex County’s vast salt marshes, provided they can survive rising sea levels.

Local writer, Doug Stewart, who contributes to the Globe, will present “The Great Marsh and Climate Change,” an illustrated talk, at 7:30 p.m. on Oct. 18 at the Ipswich Museum as part of its Wednesday evening lecture series.

The talk is $10, free for museum members, at the Heard House, 54 South Main St. Visit ipswichmuseum.org.
