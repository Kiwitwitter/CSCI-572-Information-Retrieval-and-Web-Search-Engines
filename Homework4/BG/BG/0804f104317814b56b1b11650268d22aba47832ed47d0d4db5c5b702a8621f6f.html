Facing sex-bias investigations, country club digs in with denials

By Shirley Leung Globe Columnist  July 30, 2017

I must apologize for my recent column about the Charles River Country Club and the brouhaha over a stunning new men’s grill. I was unaware of one crucial detail that members and guests have since pointed out to me. A sign had been hanging over the single-sex pub’s well-appointed bar, and it read: “A woman can only get SO mad.”

The sign, I am told, was removed two weeks ago, around the same time my column appeared on how some members are up in arms about the $1 million-plus renovation of the men’s locker room, complete with a new bar and stone fireplace.

Removed, probably, because it turns out women can get really mad — and act on it.

As I previously reported, someone tipped off the Alcoholic Beverages Control Commission, alleging that the private club in Newton is subject to the public accommodations law and as such a single-sex grill would be illegal.

A $1 million renovation of the Newton club’s men’s locker room includes a single-sex pub complete with food, booze, and a stone fireplace.

Someone also alerted the state’s attorney general’s office over the concern that the men’s grill discriminates against women. The AG’s civil rights unit has taken up the matter and has been in contact with the club.

The club has retained an attorney, according to an e-mail sent to members by president Ed Deveau and vice president Rich Wunder.

Advertisement

“While it is extremely disappointing to see The River described in such an unfair and unflattering way, together our community will weather this situation," wrote Deveau and Wunder.

The club declined to comment on the ABCC investigation or other questions. Dot Joyce, the PR professional retained by the club to deal with the controversy, has previously said there is no gender bias in the membership, and that men got an upgraded locker room because there are more male members who use the club’s facilities. The women have other dining options, including eating and drinking in the main pub.

Not lost on me in all of this is how the ABCC and the attorney general’s office each fall under the purview of a woman: state Treasurer Deb Goldberg and Attorney General Maura Healey. As powerful as they are, if they were members or even guests of the Charles River club, they would not be allowed to eat or drink at the new men’s grill.

Now that’s something to get mad about.

Again some of you will wonder why what happens in the men’s locker room of the Charles River club should even be a story in the Globe. It’s a private club, leave them alone. The women of a country club are hardly an aggrieved class.

But it’s worth reiterating why all of this matters: Business and civic leaders are members of this club, and they would never dare to approve a men-only lunch room in their workplaces. Yet in their privacy of their own clubs and social circles, it suddenly becomes OK to treat women as second-class citizens.

Yes, that’s a story worthy of these pages.

I also got a tip about another club tradition. It’s an e-mail blasted to members every week titled “Yep, Little Thursday Giggle!!” Members tell me they can contain sexist jokes that should go straight to your trash folder.

A few excerpts from one sent in May: “The old guy noticed her silky bathrobe was partially open, revealing what appeared to be a very nice figure . . . ”

“Don’t be silly!” she said with a smile, letting her robe fall slightly more open. “Stay for a while. Your wife won’t know anything.”

No, I am not making this up.

Whether the ABCC or the AG find the club in violation of the law is likely to hinge on whether it is truly a private club.

“Just because a club is a membership-based club that doesn’t mean it is a private club,” said Marsha Kazarosian , the attorney who represented nine women who won a gender-discrimination suit against the Haverhill Country Club.

That’s why no one mistakes BJ’s or Costco for a private club.

Authorities will need to determine that the Charles River club is so exclusive that they don’t fall under the public accommodation statue and are exempt from anti-discriminatory laws. They would consider a number of criteria such as selectivity in membership and how much revenue comes from outside sources.

But how about the notion that the new grill is just part of the men’s locker room, and that’s why the women aren’t allowed in?

“They think they are very clever,” said Marcia Chambers, author of “Unplayable Lie: The Untold Story of Women and Discrimination in American Golf.”

In her book, Chambers details how a Michigan country club moved the men’s grill into the locker room to avoid being subject to a new state anti-discrimination statute. Some female employees lost their jobs because they could no longer work in the men’s locker room, and they filed a lawsuit. The case was settled, and the women went back to work.

Chambers quotes a local politician who summed up the situation this way: “That they would confuse a bar with a urinal is preposterous.”
