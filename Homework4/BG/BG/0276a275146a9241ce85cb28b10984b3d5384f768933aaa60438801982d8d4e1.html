BRUINS NOTEBOOK

Patrice Bergeron still not ready but is expected to travel

matthew j. lee/globe staff

Bruins winger Anders Bjork is defensed by Colorado’s J.T. Compher.

By Kevin Paul Dupont Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

Two games into the season, the Bruins have yet to have No. 1 center Patrice Bergeron in the lineup. The veteran pivot again was on the sidelines for Monday’s matinee vs. the Avalanche, and coach Bruce Cassidy could offer no definitive timeline for the return of his Selke Award winner.

“He skated today on his own,” said Cassidy prior to the 4-0 loss at the Garden. “It’s day to day. We want to be cautions. We don’t want him to be chasing it all year, so let’s give him the appropriate amount of time.”

Cassidy was quick to add that planning around injuries can change suddenly, “when the urgency level of everything goes up.” Translation: A couple of losses could influence what level of pain or discomfort the 32-year-old Bergeron is willing to tolerate.

“We’re not trying to hide anything here,” noted Cassidy. “It’s just that he’s not 100 percent, doesn’t feel ready. So we are going to give him the time.”

The Bruins will practice Tuesday morning in Brighton, and Bergeron is expected join them for the flight west later in the day for a three-game tour that goes through Denver, Glendale (Ariz.), and Las Vegas.

Roster moves

Along with Torey Krug, the Bruins also activated center/wing Austin Czarnik, who saw his first action since last season, when he played 49 games toggling back and forth between the varsity and Providence (22 games/23 points).

To make room for both, the Bruins assigned ex-BU defenseman Matt Grzelcyk to Providence.

As expected, the Bruins also designated Noel Acciari (fractured finger) and David Backes (diverticulitis) to the injured reserve list.

Acciari, who had the finger surgically repaired Friday, is expected to be lost until mid-November. Backes likely will be lost through the end of October.

He’ll be back

Grzelcyk impressed the coaching staff, his game more assertive and proactive, as training camp progressed. He had an outstanding preseason game a week ago Saturday in Chicago, which helped him earn a spot on the opening night roster. But with Krug back, management wants him to get heavy minutes in the AHL rather than spot duty as the No. 7 D with the varsity.

“Good,” said Cassidy, assessing Grzelcyk’s game. “Moved the puck. Stayed out of trouble. Good decisions with it. Defended well. No issues with it.

“No one enjoys going down, obviously, but he should be going down in a good frame of mind, knowing that he can play at this level.”

Blue liners under Cassidy’s direction are asked to play with more bandwidth than they were under ex-coach Claude Julien, whose overall defensive scheme was more rigid and plotted. Grzelcyk, skating among his assets, embraced the greater freedom.

“Move the puck, defend with your stick and your acumen,” said Cassidy, noting the style he encouraged Grzelcyk to play. “Try to avoid those physical confrontations — try to win the ones that you can.

“So, good for Gryz, and it’s good for us to know that he can play in the league. It was one game, but I do believe he made good strides.”

College reunion

Saturday night’s stop in Glendale, home of the Coyotes, will bring together ex-BU standouts Charlie McAvoy and Clayton Keller, who was the Coyotes’ first-round pick (No. 7), in the 2016 draft. Seven selections later in the same draft, the Bruins picked McAvoy.

Keller and McAvoy, who played together last season on Commonwealth Ave., are among the favorites to challenge for the NHL’s Rookie of the Year honors.

A natural center with a left-handed shot, the 5-foot-10-inch Keller has begun the season on the Coyotes’ No. 1 line, but as a right winger, working with ex-Ranger Derek Steapan at center and Max Domi at left wing.

Gloves are off

Adam McQuaid partnered with Krug, the two forming a regular tandem throughout last season — paired as frequently as captain Zdeno Chara and Brandon Carlo on the top shutdown unit.

Last Thursday vs. Nashville, McQuaid was featured in the night’s only bout, a respectable throwdown with ex-Avalanche slugger Cody McLeod.

“I think in general there is less of it,” said McQuaid, asked about fighting in today’s NHL. “Obviously, there [are fewer instances] of guys just going out to find fights for little or no reason.

“I don’t know if it was anything I ever solely focused on, but probably, by the numbers you see it is happening less and less. If something happens, you react.”

What triggered the bout with McLeod?

“I don’t really know, to be honest,” said McQuaid, breaking into a wide grin. “I didn’t have much choice in that situation. I mean, he plays a hard game and that‘s still his role. Maybe it’s because we had a decent start, and we were up, 1-0, and he was trying to change the momentum. So I just had to answer the bell.”

Eagle lands a deal

The Florida Panthers signed blue liner Mike Matheson to a max length (eight-year) contract extension that will pay the ex-BC Eagle $39 million, an annual payout of just under $5 million.

Matheson, 23, turned pro in the spring of 2015, after a three-year stay at the Heights, and inked his extension after only 86 NHL games, the Panthers embracing the trend of tying up young, core talent early.

A Montrealer, Matheson was a first-round pick (No. 23) in 2012, chosen one pick before the Bruins selected Malcolm Subban, who last week was lost to Las Vegas via waivers. The Bruins will see the Golden Knights, with Subban and ex-defenseman Colin Miller, when they play their first game in Vegas on Sunday.
