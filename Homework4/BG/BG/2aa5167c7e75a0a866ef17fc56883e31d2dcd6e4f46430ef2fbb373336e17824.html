HIGH SCHOOL FIELD HOCKEY

At Andover, field hockey and tough love

  • E-Mail

    Share via e-mail

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+
  • LinkedIn
Comments Print
The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Tom Petrini Globe Correspondent  September 15, 2017

In the early stages of her 20th season, Maureen Noone holds practice on the turf at Andover High on a quiet Sunday night, a time slot she chose years ago because nobody else would be there. The field belongs to Noone and the field hockey players willing to show up and work hard.

This is the Noone program: a six-day-a-week commitment built for the extremely dedicated — and nobody else.

“If we came out and fooled around all practice and had a great time, that’s fine, but that’s what they’re gonna do in a game,” said Noone, 54. “If you don’t raise the bar and raise your expectations, you’re gonna continue getting kids that really don’t want to commit, and I don’t think it’s fair to the kid that wants to.”

Noone took over when Dan Casper’s eldest daughter, Ellen, now 36, was a senior. “When she started here, basically all we got were soccer castoffs, and the very first year she put us in the playoffs for the first time in many years,” said Casper. “My younger one,” Stephanie, now 32, “played at Northeastern, my older one played at Union, and the best thing they ever did was play for Coach Noone.”

After his girls finished school, Casper came back to Andover as an assistant coach. He sees Noone’s no-nonsense style as an excellent way to teach discipline and accountability.

“Kids don’t always hear things that they don’t want to hear, they’re used to being told that they’re the best, and that everything they do is right,” said Casper. “If they do something not right on the field, she’ll let them know, and it’s great.”

She is the kind of coach who never yells because she never has to.

“I tend to get super nice kids, we’ve never had anybody get in trouble during the season, which I pride myself that they’re fearful to do that,” Noone said with a chuckle.

Her Belichickian reputation precedes her, so incoming freshmen already know what playing for Noone entails.

“You know that from the start in this program, it’s a full commitment, it’s a lot of work but it’s so worth it in the end,” said senior captain Emma Farnham. “It’s nothing personal, so I think all of us really take it as constructive criticism, which makes us work harder, and we get better from it.”

“Honestly, freshman year I was terrified of her,” assistant captain Kate Gregory half joked. The senior was the team’s leading scorer and a Globe All-Scholastic last year, and she’s exactly the type of player that excels under Noone. She plays year round, and calls field hockey her life.

Andover’s players needed every bit of the hustle that Noone has instilled in them to beat a talented Central Catholic team in the pouring rain Sept. 7 in Andover. Andover usually scores first, so was in unfamiliar territory after Kaylee Thomas buried a shot off a feed from BU-bound Casey Thompson.

“To go down in the first half is not what we’re used to, and I don’t mean that in a cocky way, but they’re not used to it,” said Noone, who complimented the speed, skill, and passing ability of Central Catholic. Andover came back to score three straight goals, including the eventual game-winner on a penalty shot by Gregory.

“It showed us that we’re really capable of staying at that level with those better teams,” said Thompson, a longtime Central standout, of the game. “Andover has always been really strong, and now we’re able to really compete with them at that level, and hopefully beat them next game.”

Josselyn Wilson is now in her ninth year coaching Central Catholic, and she knew she wanted to build her team around Thompson early in her freshman year, calling her the best all-around player she’s ever coached.“She’s your All-American field hockey player, a kid who does everything,” said Wilson.

Andover is Central’s main competition for the D1 North title, which Noone has won many times over. Wilson joked that she needs a spy on the other sideline to figure out what makes Noone so effective. “I told my team that I don’t feel like in any way they should have been upset with the way that they played,” said Wilson of the close 3-2 loss.

Noone’s career has been one of tough love, commitment, and winning. She coached her team to back-to-back state championships in 2010 and 2011, and wants to get back there with this year’s team. When she looks back, though, the wins aren’t what she thinks of first — it’s the players.

She joked about feeling old now that some of them are getting married and having kids of their own. “It did fly by,’’ said Noone, “and every once in awhile you think back, and you think of the kids that you’ve had, and where they’re at.

“Watching the kids go on and play in college is a nice thing,” said Noone, who keeps in touch through social media. “If field hockey somehow got them excited to go to a school, or gave them an opportunity maybe at a better school, that’s pretty exciting.”
