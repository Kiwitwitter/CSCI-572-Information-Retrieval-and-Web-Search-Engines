Topsfield Fair pumpkins fall short of record size

CRAIG F. WALKER/GLOBE STAFF

Growers (left-right) Joe Jutras, of North Scituate, RI and Steven Sperry of Johnston, RI, admiredWoody Lancaster's (of Topsfield) Atlantic Giant Pumpkin during the New England Giant Pumpkin Growers Association's Giant Pumpkin Weigh-Off at the Topsfield Fair.

By Jeremy C. Fox Globe Correspondent  September 30, 2017

Weighing about as much as 10 adults, and standing waist-high, the biggest pumpkins in competition at this year’s Topsfield Fair were impressive by almost any standards.

But they didn’t measure up to last year’s 2,075-pounder submitted by Steve Connolly, of Sharon, which set a record for the All-New England Giant Pumpkin Weigh-Off.

“Unfortunately we did not have a record-breaker. We came very close,” said David Thomson, a spokesman for the fair.

This year’s winner, an Atlantic Giant Pumpkin nurtured by Topsfield grower Woody Lancaster, was a relatively svelte 2003.5 pounds — crossing the one-ton mark, but falling short of both the Topsfield record and the even more gargantuan North American record of 2,261 pounds.

“That’s an impressive fruit,” grower Steve Sperry said of Lancaster’s pumpkin, which he expected to be the winner. Sperry, of Johnston, R.I., knows a great pumpkin when he sees one. His 1,938-pound fruit — yes, technically a pumpkin is a fruit — took second place.

Third place went to a pumpkin submitted by Mark Breznick, of Pittsford, Vt., which weighed in at a relatively modest 1578 pounds.

“What’s surprising is the variation in the weight. Usually our second and third places are much closer, but to have a 500-pound difference was kind of surprising,” Thomson said.

Advertisement

This year’s fluctuating weather conditions created a less-than-ideal growing season for pumpkins, Thomson said, and four pumpkins that had been entered in the contest either collapsed or succumbed to rot before Friday’s judging. In the end, the weigh-in included only 20 entries, noticeably shy of the usual 30 to 35 competitors, Thomson said.

Thomson was hopeful that a new record might be set next year, when he said the fair will celebrate its 200th anniversary.
