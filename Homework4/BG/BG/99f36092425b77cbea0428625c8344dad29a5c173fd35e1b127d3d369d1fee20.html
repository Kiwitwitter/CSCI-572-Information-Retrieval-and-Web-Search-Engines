FLUTO SHINZAWA/ON HOCKEY

Bruins rookies learn some hard lessons

Matthew J. Lee/Globe Staff

Tuukka Rask and Charlie McAvoy couldn’t prevent J.T. Compher’s first-period goal.

By Fluto Shinzawa Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

Plus-minus does not tell a complete story. A minus next to a player’s name is not necessarily a measure of being defensively compromised on a particular goal.

 Young players, however, cannot help but look at stat sheets postgame and notice the statistic when it’s deeply in the negative. Jake DeBrusk and Anders Bjork, both sparkling in their NHL debuts, were tagged with dash-3s in Monday’s 4-0 loss to Colorado. The numbers might as well have been tattooed on their foreheads.

“Game I want to forget,” said DeBrusk, who landed one shot in 13:00 of ice time. “Also use it as a learning experience. Good we get to play them the next game in Colorado.”

 DeBrusk, Bjork, and Charlie McAvoy made a hard game look easy against the Predators. DeBrusk and McAvoy scored their first career goals. Bjork recorded an assist while seeing prime-time duty with DeBrusk and David Krejci.

 The thing about rookies, however, is their natural inclination toward peaks and valleys. DeBrusk and Bjork started Monday next to Krejci. Everyone liked the line against Nashville. It took Bruce Cassidy one period against Colorado to give the unit, like most of his lineup, a thumbs down. 

 By the second period, DeBrusk and Bjork flanked Ryan Spooner. The shuffling did not end there. 

 Bjork took a shift with Spooner and Sean Kuraly. DeBrusk rode with Austin Czarnik and Frank Vatrano. By garbage time, DeBrusk and Bjork were back with Krejci, doing as little with their center in the third as they did in the first.

“I’m not going to put it on them,” Cassidy said of his youngsters. “We had plenty of time to get back in the game with the core group we rely on. It just wasn’t a good effort from young, old, in between. Clearly, I put myself in that category. We’re supposed to be ready to play at home, especially after a couple days off. That was the biggest disappointment to me.”

In the first period, Sven Andrighetto found a pocket in the slot. It is not an area the Bruins like to cede. Yet with Krejci marking Nathan MacKinnon and Brandon Carlo manning the front of the net, DeBrusk could not cut off the passing lane and Bjork could not collapse quickly enough to seal off Andrighetto before he buried Colorado’s first shot.

 Later in the first, while on the power play, Bjork, Vatrano, and Matt Beleskey got trapped on the right-side wall when the Colorado killers made their escape. McAvoy thought about pinching down the wall to keep the puck going. But McAvoy slammed on the brakes and put himself in no-man’s land as Carl Soderberg and J.T. Compher initiated their jail break. A backtracking McAvoy couldn’t stick aside Soderberg’s cross-ice pass to Compher, nor could he get over in time to impede the forward’s shot.

 “My initial thought was to get back and put myself in a position between me and the goalie, not let up a breakaway or anything,” McAvoy said. “It was a clean two-on-one. Anders was trailing their guy. At the end of the day, I maybe could have taken away the pass a little more. I tried taking away the back door. I had my stick on the ice to prevent that. But maybe I go at him more and take away the shot.”

 Mistakes happen to everyone. Veterans, however, are better at parking their bad shifts and making the next ones shine. They do not let mistakes trouble their thinking and harden their hands.

 McAvoy tried to shake off his misplay by staying aggressive in the third. Such decisions are at the heart of his game. 

 When Matt Duchene received an outlet pass from Patrik Nemeth on the right-side boards, McAvoy slammed down on the Colorado center, knowing Spooner had dropped back and Kevan Miller was also sprinting into defensive formation. It just so happened that Duchene eluded McAvoy’s pinch and chipped the puck ahead to Nail Yakupov. In turn, Tuukka Rask made the hasty decision to leave the crease, allowing Yakupov to settle the puck and shoot it around the goalie.

 Prodigious talent, playoff experience, and a go-go approach allowed McAvoy to put previous shifts in the past. This is how the Bruins want McAvoy to play. They’re similarly OK with DeBrusk and Bjork making mistakes, but they also want them to follow McAvoy’s lead in burying them before their next shifts.

 It’s not as easy as it looks. Doubt creeps into players promptly, especially young ones. DeBrusk and Bjork did not seem as eager as McAvoy to have pucks on their sticks and do what they do best.

 “I think it’s natural for it to linger a little bit at this level if it’s not going their way,” Cassidy said. “They’re used to playing big minutes. They’re used to being able to play through mistakes, which we’ll allow them to do. There comes a time when there’s other guys that have performed in this league or in Providence that will get their opportunity. Tonight was a bit of both.”

 Against Nashville, McAvoy (22:00), Bjork (16:14), and DeBrusk (14:05) got regular shifts. One game later, their ice time went down, respectively, to 19:28, 14:38, and 13:00. 

 Coaches like consistency from all players. Cassidy’s kids are chasing that quality on the job.
