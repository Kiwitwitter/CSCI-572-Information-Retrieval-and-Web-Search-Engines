Group chat gave BC players a head start on their football careers

By Julian Benbow Globe Staff  September 08, 2017

Two years ago, when Boston College was piecing together its 2016 recruiting class, the football players the Eagles were after were already laying the foundation for their future at The Heights.

Quarterback Anthony Brown, receiver Kobay White, and linebacker Max Richardson were among the first to commit, but they had been in constant contact with each other long before they did.

As the recruiting class was starting to come together, they set up a group chat. It was at least 20 players deep.

“Once other people started committing, I started getting in touch with them and that kind of really put it over the edge,” White said. “We all had a huge group message — basically our whole class — like, years before we got here. So we were all pretty close before any of us got here.”

“That helped so much,” Brown said. “I guess our technology now is, just, it’s very easy to connect with others, and that helped us out a lot. We had the group chat and we’ve seen each other before. It was just very easy to connect with each other.”

Once they got on campus, they bypassed the awkward orientation phase.

“Honestly, when we got here, there really wasn’t any, like, introducing each other,” White said. “Even when we went on our official visits, everyone knew each other’s name. No one really had to introduce anyone. It’s good to have guys that you can bond with.”

As the group traded messages, they laid out a vision for themselves at BC. They wanted to be the recruiting class that came in and made an immediate impact.

“We made a statement early in our group chat saying that we needed to change the atmosphere around here,” Brown said. “The older guys on our team, they help us do that a lot. But we wanted to be the younger group that is more mature instead of being the young group that’s just young and they have time. We wanted to grow up a lot faster, mature a lot faster.”

Along with a core of established veterans that includes All-America defensive lineman Harold Landry and all-Atlantic Coast Conference linebacker Connor Strachan, the Eagles will rely this season on the crop of young talent they brought in a year ago.

Brown became just the second freshman quarterback in school history to start a season opener when he led the Eagles to a 23-20 win over Northern Illinois last week. His most trusted target was White, his roommate last year with whom he developed a bond as they spent the season as redshirts. Richardson, a sophomore, got the first start of his career, slotted in the heart of the defense at middle linebacker.

In all, seven members of the 2016 class touched the field, and the bulk of them left their fingerprints on the win. Brown threw for 191 yards and two touchdowns, and his 42 pass attempts were the most by a BC quarterback since 2012. White grabbed six passes for 76 yards. Richardson made two tackles. Ray Marten caught two passes, including a 4-yard touchdown. Korab Idrizi made two grabs for 17 yards.

“I think if you get all of the guys that have the right mind-set, if you get enough of them on the same page, it can really make a big impact,” said Richardson, “because on the team, there’s really 120 guys, but if you get that 25 in the freshman class to have that right mind-set, that way, when we get a year older, the next 25 can have the right mind-set and the next 25 and so on.”

Coach Steve Addazio knew they were champing at the bit to get on the field, but they needed time.

“They weren’t ready when they got here,” said Addazio. “But they’re getting ready now. They’re now redshirt freshmen, and that’s still early, but they’re doing a good job.”

The connection between White and Brown was on full display against Northern Illinois. Brown targeted him three times on third down and once on fourth, and Brown converted all of them.

Last year, it was at first unclear whether Brown would play or redshirt. But once the decision was made to give him a year to develop, he worked constantly with White to develop chemistry.

Richardson was thrown into the mix as a true freshman last season. Most of the action he saw came on special teams, but he took every snap seriously, whether it was in-game or on the practice team.

“He’s intense. He’s tough,” said Addazio. “He’s one of those guys where you tell him exactly how you want it done, he gives it back to you exactly the way you tell him how you want it done. He’s very detailed, he’s really smart, but he’s a tough son of a gun. He’s an impact guy.”

Once linebacker Matt Milano moved on to the NFL, Strachan slid to the strong side, and the opportunity opened up for Richardson at middle linebacker. The experience as a freshman made it easier to adjust to an increased role as a sophomore.

“I think getting snaps early really helped me,” said Richardson, “because definitely the speed of the game, it changes and it gets faster, and you have to flow with that and grow with that.

“But also the mental aspect, the preparing for a game. You can’t just prepare the night before. It takes the whole week, it takes watching tape, it takes getting a little bit of extra work in on the field. It takes the whole process to get ready for that one kickoff play or that one kickoff-return play.

“And that just grows. This year, I’m playing linebacker, so now it takes the whole week to prepare for 50 plays on defense whereas it’s not just 25 snaps on special teams. So you’ve got to work a little bit harder to adjust, but it does prepare you and shows you what the process is like.”

The plan before they ever arrived was to speed up the growth process.

“It’s just becoming more mature at a younger age,” Richardson said.
