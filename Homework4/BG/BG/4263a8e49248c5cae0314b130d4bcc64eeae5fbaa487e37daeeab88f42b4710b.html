Street bands take over Somerville for HONK!

John Tlumacki/Globe Staff

Members of various bands cheer during a performance on stage at last year’s HONK! Festival.

By Kathy Shiels Tully Globe Correspondent  September 29, 2017

HONK IF YOU LIKE IT Maybe you have been living in an underground bunker the past 11 years and managed to miss HONK!.

The festival of activist street bands — all about building community, standing up for social justice, and celebrating an inclusive, participatory culture — draws thousands to Somerville every year.

Advertisement

The 12th annual festival will take place Friday, Oct. 6, through Sunday, Oct. 8 in various neighborhoods, spreading to Cambridge and Boston.

Returning are the family-friendly Lantern Parades through Somerville neighborhoods; the HONK! parade down Massachusetts Avenue to “Reclaim the Streets for Horns, Bikes, and Feet;” and continuous, free performances by more than 25 bands.

Local favorites, aNova Brazil and Boycott (Somerville) will perform, along with bands from Atlanta (Black Sheep Ensemble); San Franscisco (Brass Liberation Orchestra); and Greensboro, N.C. (Caka!ak Thunder). Joining them will be two all-women bands, Damas de Ferro from Rio de Janeiro and Seattle’s Filthy FemCorps.

HONK! is free, open to all, and takes place rain or shine. Hours at 4 to 8 p.m. Friday, Oct 6; noon to 8 p.m. Saturday, Oct 7; and noon to 6 p.m. Sunday, Oct 8. To volunteer, donate to support travel and housing costs for visiting bands, or for updated information, visit honkfest.org or call 617-383-HONK (4665).

HANG ONTO SUMMER Linger longer along beautiful marsh and ocean views at “Time and Tide,” a show of contemporary seascape paintings by Susan Cole Kelly on display through Oct. 29 at The Grog, 13 Middle St., Newburyport.

Advertisement

The local artist, whose photos have appeared in magazines such as Yankee, Down East, and Vermont Life, explores the horizon and its relationship to sea and sky, dark and light, water and air.

The show is free and open to the public Sundays through Thursdays, 11:30 a.m. to 10 p.m.; Fridays and Saturdays, 11:30 a.m. to 11 p.m. To see her work, go to kellypix.com.

SUPER WOMAN Before parades of pink hats were worn around the world to demonstrate womens’ power, there were strong-minded women like Harriet Hanson Robinson.

In the 1800s, she was a poet, author, and leader of the women’s suffrage movement.

After her father died, a 10-year-old Harriet went to work in a Lowell cotton mill in 1834 to help her mother support her four children. She wrote about her experiences with factory life and the role Massachusetts played in the women’s suffrage movement in her book, “Loom and

Spindle.” Later, after she and her husband, William, moved to Malden, Robinson became active in women’s suffrage issues and other reform movements.

You can learn more about the fascinating and oft-quoted Robinson on Tuesday, Oct. 10, when social historian and author Claudia Bushman presents a talk to benefit the Malden Historical Society at 7 p.m. at the Malden Public Library, 36 Salem St.

Bushman, who wrote “A Good Poor Man’s Wife,” a biography of Robinson, in 1998, has published a new book based on Robinson’s 1870 diary: “Going to Boston: Harriet Robinson’s Journey to New Womanhood.”

The free talk, being held in the Ryder Gallery, is open to the public. The new book will be available for $35 with proceeds benefiting the Malden Historical Society.

SECRET LANGUAGE How can a “women-only” language exist undercover in China?

Find out about Nüshu at the Gallery @ Spencer Lofts at 60 Dudley St. in Chelsea, where “The Language-Producing Factory,” a multimedia exhibit, appears through Friday, October 6.

Furen Dai, fresh off residencies at New York’s Art Omi and Boston’s Samsøñ Gallery, has drawn upon her diverse background in Russian language and literature, international relations, and translator in her native China to create this tour-de-force. It all began with her research on Nüshu, a covert, language spoken and written among women in the Hunan Province.

In her video, Dai sheds light on a “language factory” where women are employed for menial wages and pressured to write the covert Nüshu script as art objects and perform cultural activities to entertain tourists.

The exhibit is free. Hours are by appointment. E-mail info@galleryspencerlofts.com.
