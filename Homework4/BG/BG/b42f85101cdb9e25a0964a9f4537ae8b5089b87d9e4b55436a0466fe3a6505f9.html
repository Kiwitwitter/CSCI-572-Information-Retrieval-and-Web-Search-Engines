CELTICS 94, HORNETS 82

Celtics look sharp in their preseason opener

Jim Davis/Globe Staff

Kyrie Irving was poised and ready in his first preseason game for the Celtics, finishing with 9 points, 4 rebounds, and 3 assists in 19 minutes.

By Adam Himmelsbach Globe Staff  October 03, 2017

Kyrie Irving sat at his locker after making his Celtics debut Monday and smiled as he had a FaceTime video chat with his young daughter, Azurie Elizabeth. He lifted the phone to show her the locker room, and then smiled again when he told her he would call back in a few minutes.

It seemed like a pleasant end to what was an appropriately feel-good start in Boston for Irving, whose request to be traded this summer sent shockwaves through the NBA. On Monday night, there he was, wearing a jersey other than Cleveland’s for the first time in his pro career, as the Celtics took a 94-82 exhibition win over the Hornets.

“When we start off the game, like, my eyes were just wide,” Irving said. “And the basket was a little bit smaller, and things were just a lot bigger than what they are. And that’s just literally [from] not playing basketball on an NBA floor for a few months. So I was taking in all that newness before I could even get so nervous and anxious.”

After a frenetic, change-filled summer, Boston started the year with a team that hardly resembled the one that reached the Eastern Conference finals four months ago. So there was an unusual anticipation and curiosity leading up to this game, much of it focused on Irving.

After longtime public address announcer Eddie Palladino introduced Boston’s other marquee addition, Gordon Hayward, it was Irving’s turn. He stood out instantly, wearing white warmups while the rest of his teammates were draped in green. But he was easily identifiable anyway.

Irving received a standing ovation from a small but lively crowd that occupied about two-thirds of TD Garden. The last time these fans saw him, he was crushing the Celtics alongside LeBron James in Game 5 of the conference finals. But now he was with them, and the people here were ready to cheer.

On the first possession, Irving carved into the lane and lofted a floater, but it thudded off the rim. He gathered the rebound and fired a pass to Hayward at the left arc, and his 3-point try was no good, either. But there was plenty of time for Irving to better introduce himself, and there will be even more time when it matters.

With 8:41 left, he drilled a jumper from the top of the key. Ninety seconds later, he took a nifty backdoor pass from Al Horford and put in a two-handed dunk, something that was not seen when the 5-foot-9-inch Isaiah Thomas, who was scintillating in his own way, played here.

On Boston’s next possession, Irving sped along the right baseline and converted a tough running shot with his left hand. In just two minutes, he had given the crowd here a taste of his diverse and devastating arsenal, and everyone enjoyed it.

“It was awesome, man,” Irving said, “beautiful to be a part of. It was beautiful to watch. Purposeful cuts, understanding what we were trying to do offensively and defensively, which is executing at a high level.”

Irving finished with 9 points, 4 rebounds, and 3 assists in 19 minutes, 22 seconds. Hayward had 5 points and five rebounds in 16:32.

The biggest question entering the night surrounded coach Brad Stevens’s choice for a starting lineup. It was obvious that Irving, Hayward, and Horford would be there, but the rest remained a bit unclear, especially with forward Marcus Morris’s assault trial in Phoenix still wrapping up.

Stevens chose center Aron Baynes, who signed a one-year deal in July, and second-year guard Jaylen Brown. The coach said before the game that he expects his lineups to be fluid this year, making some decisions based on matchups. But he also reiterated that he places great value in bringing fourth-year guard Marcus Smart off the bench.

“I told him, ‘Whatever you decide, I’m with it,’ ” Smart said. “That’s the uniqueness about this team. No one really cares if they start or come off the bench. Everybody has to be ready, everybody’s going to play a role, a big role, when they come in the game.”

Smart generated quite a bit of buzz when he arrived at training camp weighing 223 pounds, nearly 20 pounds lighter than his playing weight a year ago. Smart, who made just 28.3 percent of his 3-pointers last season, hit his first two 3-pointers and finished 3 for 7 in the first half.

Rookie forward Jayson Tatum appeared uneasy at the start, missing badly on several early shots before finding his rhythm against Charlotte’s reserves in the second half. Tatum had 10 points on 4-of-11 shooting, along with six assists and five rebounds.

“Bottom line is he can get so much better,” Stevens said. “And he’s already a really good player. But he can get better, and that’s one of our charges, to make sure that every day is a learning day.”

Baynes, the burly Australian, will give the Celtics some muscle in the paint they have lacked in recent years. He had 10 points and five rebounds and helped neutralize Charlotte’s Dwight Howard in the post.

“We got guys that command so much attention offensively that it gives some of us a lot of space to work with,” Baynes said. “I was just trying to knock down some open shots. I missed a few, so I’m not too happy about that. I’d rather reward those guys for hitting me, but it’s fun being out there with them.”

With the stars on the bench in the second half, some of the other new Celtics were able to get an extended look in their debuts. Daniel Theis, the 25-year-old German rookie, had the most noticeable impact, throwing down three dunks before finishing with a team-high 12 points and seven rebounds.

“I just tried to bring some energy and have fun out there,” Theis said, “and I think I did a pretty good job.”
