Trey Flowers is getting after it on defense for Patriots

Jason Behnken/Associated Press

Patriots defensive end Trey Flowers pressured Buccaneers quarterback Jameis Winston last Thursday.

By Jim McBride Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

Trey Flowers’s pressure off the edge has been the most consistent component of the Patriots defense this season. The man known for his flawless technique — so much so it’s become a nickname — is usually within striking distance of the quarterback.

Flowers, who burst onto the New England scene last season after an injury-shortened rookie campaign, possesses an initial burst that offensive tackles must respect and a closing burst that quarterbacks must fear.

According to Pro Football Focus, Flowers has 24 total quarterback pressures, good for ninth in the NFL.

The 6-foot-2-inch, 265-pounder plays with great leverage and strength and he has the versatility to play off the edge or off the line — there was a one-snap glimpse of that against the Buccaneers — and leads the Patriots with 3.5 sacks this season and leads the team’s defensive linemen with 331 snaps played. Only Devin McCourty (338) and Kyle Van Noy (333) have played more on defense for the Patriots.

“[His] level of conditioning is good,’’ Patriots coach Bill Belichick said Monday. “Trey works hard. He’s given us a lot of quality snaps. He has been on the field quite a bit, but you know, he’s earned that playing time.’’

Flowers’s relentlessness allows him to be effective off the edge and his strength makes him tough for blockers to handle up the middle.

His ability to play multiple positions keeps him on the field during all of the Patriots’ varied looks.

“He’s got good versatility for us,’’ said Belichick. “There’s a number of different things that he can do well. He works very hard to perform well. He trains hard. He’s done a good job for us and I’m glad that we’ve had him out there.’’

A fine line

When it comes to pressuring the QB, there’s a fine line between being aggressive and being disciplined.

Defensive coordinator Matt Patricia talked about where that line is drawn when he was asked about the back-to-back roughing-the-passer calls against the Patriots that gave the Buccaneers a chance at a field goal at the end of the first half last Thursday.

“Obviously penalties are something that are not acceptable,’’ said Patricia. “We’re just trying to make sure we coach it the right way and educate those guys on what is allowed and what is not allowed and trying to play within the confines of the game. Certainly something like that is a huge teaching moment for us.’’

Under siege

Among the more concerning issues for the Patriots is the amount of abuse Tom Brady has been taking. The Patriots quarterback has been sacked 16 times and hit 32 times through five games. Brady was sacked just 15 times in 2016.

Left tackle Nate Solder has had a slow start to the season. He’s allowed three sacks and seven total QB hits over the last four games.

“I think that overall as a team there are things that we all need to do better — coaching, playing, adjustments and so forth,’’ said Belichick when asked about Solder. “Sometimes we haven’t just executed things the way that we want to do it. There have been a number of reasons for that.”

Solder missed significant time during training camp and Belichick was asked if that is starting to catch up to him.

“I think any player that practices has more of a chance to work on his fundamentals and improve them than a player that doesn’t practice,’’ the coach said. “But that being said, most players somewhere along the line miss some practice time or a game, so that’s something that each individual player has to deal with from time to time.’’

Brady said he hasn’t pinpointed a reason why he’s been under siege.

“I’m not sure,’’ the quarterback said during his weekly appearance on WEEI. “We really haven’t sat here and evaluated those things. I’m not sure.’’

Then it sounded as if Brady was going to be more expansive before he caught himself.

“I think it’s . . . I don’t even know,’’ he said. “I don’t know.’’

Unfinished business

The Patriots took care of business Thursday night, grinding through a short week and coming up with a win against a quality opponent in a not-so-hostile environment — Raymond James Stadium was loaded with Patriots fans — to move to 3-2.

The 19-14 win over the Buccanneers, which featured a much better effort from a defense that had taken its share of criticism, helped alleviate the sour feelings from the last-second loss to the Panthers.

It isn’t, however, being treated as a landmark victory by the Patriots. There were still some breakdowns in all three phases, including big plays on defenses, big hits on Brady, and big mistakes on special teams.

“I think in general, we’re not a finished product right now,’’ Brady said on WEEI. “We’re still trying to figure out what we are and what we’re good at and what we need to do consistently to score more points.’’

Brady said the team is constantly compensating for injuries and other factors that force changes in the game plan.

“Any time you lose players that happens — like [Rob Gronkowski] the other night. You have a plan and then all of the sudden he’s not available. Things shift and you’re trying to still find ways to move the ball down the field and score points. No matter who is out there the goal is still the same. It may look a little different based on the skill set of the players that are out there.’’

Brady said those adjustments are constant, whether it’s a major injury or minor ailment that might just affect a game or two.

“We’re making adjustments with injuries like a season-ending injury with Julian [Edelman] and then weekly injuries, whether that is to the offensive line, running backs, or tight ends or receivers,’’ the quarterback said. “Those go into the course of a long football season. It’s still relatively early. We’ve only played five games. There is still a lot football left to be played. Hopefully our best football is ahead of us.’’
