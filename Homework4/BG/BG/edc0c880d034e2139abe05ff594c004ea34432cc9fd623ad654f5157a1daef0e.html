EDITORIAL

In Myanmar, genocide loves a vacuum

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • Google+
  • LinkedIn
Comments Print
The Boston Globe Tweet Share

  September 20, 2017

The images of Rohingya refugees fleeing Myanmar, so freighted with privation and grief, are clear evidence of a horrific genocidal campaign in the country formerly known as Burma. Over the last several weeks, some 400,000 Rohingya Muslims have walked across the border into Bangladesh, fleeing a murderous military and the mass burning of more than 200 villages, according to Human Rights Watch.

The top human rights official at the United Nations has called the unfolding tragedy in Buddhist-majority Myanmar “a textbook example of ethnic cleansing.” Genocide takes root in the face of political cowardice, and flourishes in a moral vacuum. Lamentably, both conditions were on display on Tuesday, in the United States and in Myanmar itself.

At the United Nations, President Trump’s much-awaited address to the General Assembly was a befuddling miasma of campaign platitudes and the kind of clownish japes he usually reserves for Twitter. “The United States is one out of 193 countries in the United Nations, and yet we pay 22 percent of the entire budget and more,” he intoned. Perhaps warming up for his rally Saturday in Huntsville, Ala., he put America first. He threatened to, you know, “totally destroy” North Korea and called dictator Kim Jong Un “Rocket Man.” He called the painstaking US-Iran nuclear deal “one of the worst,” labeled Iran a “rogue state,” and threatened that we haven’t heard the last of it — “believe me.”

At the same time, in Myanmar, Aung San Suu Kyi, the Nobel laureate once hailed globally for her human rights activism, seemed vanishingly small indeed. In a disingenuous and defensive speech, she said her government “does not fear international scrutiny” and claimed not to know the cause of the mass exodus. That directly contradicts a report issued last month by former UN Secretary General Kofi Annan, which rightly placed blame on military and police actions against the stateless — and economically oppressed — Rohingya Muslims. It was a performance more worthy of Marshal Philippe Pétain, in Nazi-occupied France, than of Nelson Mandela, whom she has been compared to.

Even more troubling: This week, the Hindu nationalist government in India also took steps to expel some 40,000 Rhohingya refugees, arguing that some have ties to Pakistani militants and pose a national security threat. If only Trump had used his platform at the United Nations to call on the global community to impose a travel ban, sanctions, and an arms embargo for the Myanmar military. If only Suu Kyi could display the courage she demonstrated when she endured years of house arrest for her stand on democracy. Without strong political and moral suasion, the genocidal violence in Myanmar poses risks for the entire region.
