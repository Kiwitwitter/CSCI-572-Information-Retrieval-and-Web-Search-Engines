Charter school lands in controversy again — this time over music

Jonathan Wiggs/globe staff

Exterior of Mystic Valley Regional Charter School.

By Michael Levenson Globe Staff  September 14, 2017

It is a rah-rah ritual: Team captains make a playlist of songs — usually hip-hop or Top 40 — to pump over the loudspeakers before home games at Mystic Valley Regional Charter School in Malden.

But as students returned to school this week, they learned the superintendent had pulled the plug on the warm-up music after, he said, parents complained about “inappropriate language and content.” School administrators have not explained what genre, artist, or lyrics prompted the ban, and did not respond to requests for comment.

More than 850 names have been added to an online petition calling on Mystic Valley’s leaders to reverse the decision and allow music to once again fire up the players and get the crowd dancing at practices, warm-ups, and games.

The uproar is the latest controversy to buffet the school, which generated national attention earlier this year when black female students who wore hair extensions were subjected to detentions and suspensions.

“Ninety-nine-point-nine percent of the music played is clean, it’s not sexually suggestive, and the school had some complaints from what I understand are a minute amount of people,” said Michael Sullivan, a parent who has run the public address system at the school’s football and basketball games for the last 10 years.

He said team captains are told to download the radio-edited versions of their favorite songs and their playlists are screened by the coaches before being plugged into loudspeakers. He said one or two offensive songs might have slipped through the screening system.

“There were some sexually suggestive songs that may have come out,” he said. “But immediately it was pulled and the player was told to not ever play this list again. They were told it has to be clean. It can’t be offensive. There can’t be swear words.”

Colleen Cook, a parent of three Mystic Valley students who helped lead the effort to overturn the school’s ban on hair extensions, called the prohibition on pregame music “yet another Draconian rule that has nothing to do with academics but was solely made up by the board to be imposed on the children as punitive.”

Attorney General Maura Healey joined the fight against the hair extensions rule after some parents called it discriminatory. In May, she ordered the school to stop punishing black and biracial students for wearing hairstyles that the school said violated its dress code. The school dropped the ban on hair extensions from the student handbook in August and, this week, hired an attorney, Howard Cooper, to ensure its policies and practices are not biased.

Mystic Valley, which is one of the state’s biggest charter schools, with 1,500 students, prides itself on its high test scores and a disciplined academic climate. But students said the ban on music sounds like an overreaction.

“It’s just stupid,” said Kiryannah Burkett, a Mystic Valley high student who was waiting to be picked up after school this week. “It’s weird to not have music at sporting events.”

Josh Vear, a ninth-grader, said it’s pointless to try to prevent students from hearing explicit lyrics. “I mean, everyone knows the words,” he said.

In a letter to parents on Monday, Alex Dan, Mystic Valley’s superintendent and interim director, wrote that since first fielding complaints about the music several years ago, the school has had “mixed results in installing safeguards to ensure the music played is appropriate.” Parents, he wrote, have continued to complain.

“Faced with these challenges, I asked our athletics director, Mr. Furlong, to put a halt on music being played at practices and games until we could devise and implement a reasonable way to ensure our sports events continue to be inclusive and suitable for all members of our community,” Dan wrote.

Marilyn Slattery, president of the Massachusetts Interscholastic Athletic Association, which governs high school sports, said she had never heard of a school banning music at games. Slattery, a house principal at Malden High School, who used to screen the music that students wanted to play at her school’s sporting events, said most of what she heard was “PG-rated” radio edits of hip-hop music.

“I don’t dislike that kind of music so I don’t mind listening to it,” she said. She could recall rejecting only one song as unsuitable: “Love the Way You Lie” by Eminem featuring Rihanna, which includes the lyric: “If she ever tries to [expletive] leave again, I’m gonna tie her to the bed and set this house on fire.”

“It was violence and it was language,” Slattery said.

Sullivan said he likes that Mystic Valley has strict rules to ensure a respectful environment for students. “It has discipline, it has uniforms, it’s not like every other school,” he said. “It’s not supposed to be. That’s why we send our kids there. We don’t want ‘anything goes.’ ”

But he said he hopes Mystic Valley can devise a new screening system so music can again be a part of the Eagles’ home-game experience. “These kids want to have fun,” he said. “It doesn’t want to be known as the no-fun school.”
