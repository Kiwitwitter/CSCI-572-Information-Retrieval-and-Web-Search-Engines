Kay Hanley talks ‘Josie and the Pussycats’

Mireyah Marcinek

Kay Hanley (and band) rocking the “Josie and the Pussycats” soundtrack in LA.

By Margaret Willison Globe Correspondent   September 29, 2017

Outside LA’s Theater at the Ace Hotel, the line of 30-year-olds sporting cat ears began forming at 5 p.m. — a full two hours before the doors to Mondo Records’s sold-out “Josie and the Pussycats” soundtrack release party were to open.

But when you’ve waited 16 years for pop culture to finally recognize the brilliance of one of your fave movies and albums, waiting two more hours to get the best possible seat is a no-brainer. Although it was a critical and commercial flop in 2001, Deb Kaplan and Harry Elfont’s satire on the commercialization of the music industry and the little teen girl band that takes it down is finally getting its due — and giving Boston native Kay Hanley, frontwoman for Letters to Cleo and the voice of Josie on the movie’s re-released soundtrack, a surprise moment in the sun.

Before delighting a euphoric crowd with a live set of the movie’s sparkling pop-punk hits, Hanley sat down and answered a few questions.

Q. How weird is it to be singing songs from a movie that came out 16 years ago?

A. Right? It’s an incredible treat to get to revisit this time in my life, and so unexpected. One of the things that really struck me was seeing Deb [Kaplan] tonight and realizing that she was the first female film director or a female screenwriter I ever met. I didn’t realize how remarkable it was until later, after I moved to LA and began to work in show business and saw how unusual it was to have women in positions of power behind the scenes.

Q. Do you think that we’re better positioned now, as a culture, to receive a movie like “Josie and the Pussycats” that aims to satisfy teenage girls without condescending to them, that tells a sweet love story but isn’t afraid to be sharp and satirical at the same time?

A. Oh, I think it would be just as hard for it to be a hit today . . . I go to the movies a lot with my 14-year-old son and it’s made me realize that the whole industry is geared toward his demographic. It really brings home to me how much our culture has shifted since “Josie and the Pussycats” ’ release. When it came out, me and all of my peers in female-fronted rock bands had hit songs and were headlining festivals and had a real voice, business-wise, in the music industry. I still had this sense, when this movie was made, that things were going to continue in that direction, that women were going to continue to have a major voice in the music industry. And they do, just not as rockers. There are acts coming up that I love, like Speedy Ortiz and Charley Biss — they just aren’t big names the way Veruca Salt and The Breeders were. Yet!

Q. Your band, Letters to Cleo, has a weird history of popping up in unexpected places — most famously, perhaps, as the favorite band of the character Ben Wyatt on “Parks and Recreation.” How did that come about?

A. That was wild — it wasn’t supposed to be such a thing! Mike Schur, “Parks and Recreation” ’s head writer, is from Boston, so he knew us and just thought it would be really funny to have Ben’s character wear a Letters to Cleo shirt — which it is! It’s so random!

Q. It is random, but it’s also such a telling character note.

A. Absolutely. And I think it said more about him than another band would have. We were the perfect amount of famous. Not so well known that people would take for granted a 34-year-old feminist man would know us, but well known enough that people knew who we were when they saw the shirt, and got the joke. And from there, it just took on a life of its own. Appearing on that show was such a blast.

Q. What do you think it is about you, and Letters to Cleo, that’s led to a career with so many unexpected twists?

A. I just go where the open door is. That’s always been my M.O. I have a vague idea of where I’m going, but then stuff just happens, and I’m like ‘Hey, I’ll go over there and do that!’ And I’m able to capitalize on opportunities, but I could never have planned any of this. Left to my own devices, I would have been waiting tables, or working at The Edison. I’m not being falsely modest, I’m just saying that the career I could have imagined for myself and the career I have achieved by saying yes to unusual opportunities, and doing well at them, are two totally different things.

Q. Finally, now that a new incarnation of Josie and the Pussycats exists on the CW drama “Riverdale,” is there any chance that you and Rachel Leigh Cook could come in and make a past-Josie cameo?

A. Wouldn’t that be funny! I haven’t given it any thought and no one has approached us, but I’d love that. We could come in as music teachers, or lesbian moms of one of the high school students, or maybe Bible-thumping activists trying to shut the band down. You should pitch that and make it happen!

The newly re-released “Josie and the Pussycats” soundtrack is available at mondotees.com, and Letters to Cleo will perform its three biggest albums Nov. 16, 17, and 18 at The Paradise.
