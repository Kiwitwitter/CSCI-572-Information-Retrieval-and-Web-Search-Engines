Sun breaks over Carson Beach as two churches join in Easter worship

Pat Greenhouse/Globe Staff

The Rev. Liz Walker spoke as the sun rose behind her on Easter Sunday.

By Eric Moskowitz Globe Staff  April 16, 2017

As the sun crept over the horizon, lighting the sky with a spray of pastel colors and reflecting off the harbor waters, the sweeping vista from Carson Beach made it clear why the two pastors — one from Roxbury, one from South Boston — had picked this site for their first shared Easter sunrise service.

But they also knew their history.

Though neither arrived in Boston until after the most turbulent initial years of the busing crisis, both were aware that this beach had been a flashpoint in a city torn apart over race. It was the scene of demonstrations, of swung bats and hurled rocks, of waves of police in riot gear — of so many clashes over who could use a litter-strewn stretch of sand lapped by polluted water.

And the older members of both parishes knew it, too. There was no way to forget a time like that, even if it could scarcely be imagined by the younger parishioners who pushed strollers or unzipped Patagonia jackets as the morning sun warmed the deck of a refurbished bathhouse overlooking the beautified Carson Beach.

Which is why the Revs. Liz Walker and Burns Stanfield picked it for the 6 a.m. service with their two churches, one mainly white and one predominantly black, though both are becoming increasingly diverse.

“This is a place where a lot of Boston’s racial disease, at least in the ’70s, got played out,” Stanfield, pastor at South Boston’s Fourth Presbyterian Church, told the 80 or so worshipers. “But God has this way, this amazing way of taking uncomfortable places and making something good happen.”

Stanfield and Walker, of Roxbury Presbyterian Church, saw in the story of the beach — rebirth from pollution, and an arc that led from racial strife to cross-parish harmony — echoes of the Easter story of the resurrection of Jesus Christ.

But they also drew hope from it for the country at a time of bitter national division.

“We are living in probably the most polarizing times that we have ever seen, at least in my life, where there’s a voice that says, ‘It’s us against them, and if they win, we lose, and there’s no way that we can all win,’” said Walker, a former WBZ-TV news anchor, drawing an amen.

“[But] the fact that these two neighborhoods can come together” — “Yes!” “Mm-hm!” — “[means] we know that God is bigger than anything that would divide us, and we can do anything,” she said, encouraging worshipers to work for social change and understanding. She returned to the resurrection story, speaking of an angel descending at the empty tomb. “That’s when the angel said, ‘Don’t be afraid. There’s work to do.’”

The two pastors, who became friends through the Greater Boston Interfaith Organization, each took unconventional paths to their churches via Harvard Divinity School: Walker after decades in TV news, Stanfield after touring with the “roots-pop-cowpunk” band Scruffy the Cat.

They often trade ideas and have sought ways to bring their parishes together, such as teen retreats, though this was their first collaborative service. Last year, amid the charged atmosphere of the presidential election, they hit upon the idea of sharing their Easter sunrise worship as a way to bring people together at the start of a day of ritual and celebration.

Walker’s parish has traditionally held an Easter sunrise service indoors at its Victorian Gothic puddingstone church on Warren Street. Stanfield has been taking his parishioners outdoors from their Andrew Square church on Easter for more than a decade, first to Castle Island — longstanding home of a sunrise Mass drawing Catholics from across the region — and then to quieter Carson Beach nearby.

At first, most of the worshipers who gathered in the dim light before sunrise gravitated to people they knew, which led to a kind of separation by race: Most of the African-American worshipers, largely from the Roxbury church, gathered on one half of the deck, and most of the white parishioners filtered to the other half.

But a transformation of sorts occurred within the half-hour service, which blended music from both congregations (with keyboardist Issa Bibbins of the Roxbury church, guitarist Eric Ogi of Fourth Presbyterian, plus Stanfield on trumpet).

The pastors encouraged the congregants to link hands in a broad circle for two renditions of the gospel song “Because He Lives” — the second livened up with what Walker called “a little Island flavor” — and a round of “Amazing Grace.”

They also invited everyone to a reception afterward at the home of a South Boston parishioner who lives midway between the two churches. And they encouraged people to shake new hands during the closing exchange of peace.

“Share the peace, share some love, and share some names,” Stanfield said.

“Make sure you share the peace with somebody you don’t know,” Walker added. “Otherwise this is in vain!”

After the last handshakes, Roxbury native Yvonne Powell said she could not help but think of all she had witnessed in nearly 70 years in the city.

“I grew up in a time when it was really very difficult to be from Roxbury and to come here to South Boston,” said Powell, who did not venture to Castle Island until four years ago. “I praise God for today and the worship service and the opportunity to get together this early in the morning, and share with one another.”

Fellow parishioner Angel Simpson agreed. Growing up in Jamaica in the 1970s, Simpson had heard about Boston’s explosive racial tension from friends who had immigrated here already.

Simpson said she saw God’s work in this service, in the beauty of the sunrise over Carson Beach, and in the way the region came together after the 2013 bombing, on her mind with Patriots Day approaching.

“Things have changed,” she said. “Whether you are from South Boston, East Boston, West Boston, all Boston unites as ‘One Boston’ this weekend.”
