Getting Salty

Getting Salty with Ashley Stanley, founder of Lovin’ Spoonfuls

  • E-Mail

    Share via e-mail

  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • LinkedIn
Comments Print
The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Kara Baskin Globe correspondent  October 10, 2017

Ashley Stanley doesn’t cook food — she rescues it. The Wellesley native founded Lovin’ Spoonfuls in 2010. The organization distributes fresh, healthy food that would otherwise be thrown away from grocery stores, wholesalers, and farms and delivers it to community organizations that feed Boston’s hungry, like Haley House and the Pine Street Inn. Her seventh annual Ultimate Tailgate Party fund-raiser happens Nov. 5, with food from restaurants like Bar Mezzana, Coppa, and Eventide Oyster Co.

What’s the first restaurant you ever ate at in Boston? It’s not around anymore! It’s the old Stella’s on India Street. We lived in Harbor Towers before moving to Wellesley, and Quincy Market was our backyard. I remember going around with my mom every day and we’d stop into almost every shop. There was always a croissant from Au Bon Pain, corned beef from Durgin Park, and stuffed shells at Stella’s.

What’s one thing you’d like to fix about the restaurant industry here? I know from hearing from all my friends that there’s a staffing shortage. There’s a new restaurant popping up every five minutes. As a consumer, that’s awesome, but maybe our favorites can’t be sustained due to a lack of traffic. From one weekend to the next, people are pulled, and some of my favorite places had to transition. I think, as a consumer and as someone who has watched the food community really change throughout my whole life, I want to see the kind of support that people need to make it and build up sustainability. We have restaurant ADD. On the one hand, it’s amazing. It represents growth and a willingness on people’s part to try new things, but I certainly think it can be distracting. At the end of the day, these are people’s livelihoods. These are small business owners.

How has the restaurant landscape changed since you arrived in Boston? There’s a lack of the old-school elegance and fine dining. This is representative of everything: going to synagogue, the theater, even going shopping. It used to be an occasion. We would go to Jasper’s or Café Budapest, all these places that aren’t around anymore. It was truly an occasion. It was the people, your server, the captain, the maître d, the chef. . . . It was less about the celebrity nature of what food is now, and it really celebrated people coming together and breaking bread. You have to try really hard to make something an occasion.

What other restaurants do you visit? I can tell you right now: You can find me at Myers + Chang. Lovin’ Spoonfuls was conceived on a piece of paper with soy sauce on it. They thought I was a food critic. I moved back, I didn’t have any friends, and I loved the food. It was an easy place to go and eat by yourself. People were friendly; it felt like home. It was an easy place. As Spoonfuls was coming into focus, I would sit at the last seat at the food bar, in between the kitchen and the counter, with a legal pad and jot down notes. Christopher [Myers] thought I was this weird food critic who couldn’t decide which way to go with my story. And Bar Mezzana is my favorite thing that’s happened in Boston in a long time, so much so that my fiancee and I are having our engagement party there.

What’s your earliest food memory that made you think, “I want to work in restaurants”? It was at Chanterelle in New York City, which is no longer open, for a birthday party. I was a child. My grandfather was a vice president of Bloomingdale’s, and we traveled to New York a lot, to these wonderful, great restaurants. It was understated and theatrical at the same time. The choreography struck me. The menu-writing. To me, they were like books, the way the menu was explained. It was exciting. Line after line, wondering how the story of the food was being put together, has always stayed with me, and it was always something I wanted to be around. Food is the most shared, universal experience we can have.

What’s the worst restaurant experience you’ve ever had? When you go somewhere and it’s 10 degrees outside, and the table is ready, and the rest of your party isn’t there, and somebody says, “Sorry, we can’t seat you.” And it’s like, “But the table is open, and it’s there, and I will order food for the people who are coming! I just want to sit down.” Is it a lack of hospitality? Nobody should feel like they’re doing anyone a favor.

How could Boston become a better food city? In some ways it’s great because it’s so small. It should be so easy to connect. It should be so easy to move within different sectors and neighborhoods. I think we could make it easier for folks to interact with each other and different projects, different organizations. . . . It can be pretty siloed.

Name three adjectives for Boston diners. Hungry, impressionable, excited.

What’s the most overdone trend right now? Always, the most overdone thing is aioli of any kind.

What are you reading? “Kennedy and King” by Steve Levingston. My fiancee bought it for me. It tells the story of both MLK and JFK and their own journeys of putting their vision for social justice into action around the same time and how it brought them to intersect and what they were able to take from each other. I’m a Kennedy connoisseur in many ways, and now is a relevant time to understand why these things are popping up again.

How’s your commute? I live in JP, and I work in Brighton. It sucks for public transportation, but I’m grateful for a car.

What’s the one food you never want to cook again? Fish. Not shellfish. I can cook shellfish. I don’t want to cook fish fillets.

What kind of restaurant is Boston missing right now? Do you remember Brasserie in New York? [We need] a leather-booth, very old-school, proper, true brasserie. Something bordering on formal but that sort of retains elements of comfort and elegance and just really good food and familiar hospitality, but [where] you don’t have people sitting up at the bar doing shots and throwing F-bombs.

What’s your most missed Boston restaurant? The European. It was in the North End, just a family place. Whenever we got off a plane as a family or were going out, we’d go to the European. My parents knew them for the longest time. It’s not the food I miss; it’s the neon sign and the way that it smelled of garlic and tomatoes and meat sauce and all that stuff.

If you had to eat your last meal in Boston, what would it be? It’s no longer on the menu, but the panko chicken at Myers + Chang. My heart hurts every day! It’s in their cookbook, though.
