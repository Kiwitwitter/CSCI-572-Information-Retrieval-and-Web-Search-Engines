DAN SHAUGHNESSY

David Price comes out of the bullpen and into the fans’ hearts

Stan Grossfeld/Globe Staff

David Price, who pitched four scoreless innings in relief, struck out Houston’s Marwin Gonzalez to end the seventh inning.

By Dan Shaughnessy Globe Columnist  October 09, 2017

He was Sidney Wicks, Jack Clark, Rick Pitino, Adalius Thomas, Carl Crawford, and Adrian Gonzalez. He was a top talent who made his professional bones and bags of cash in some other town, then came to Boston and offended an entire base of loyal fans.

Some of the treatment David Price received was unfair. Some of it was not.

But in the end, the lessons of professional sports are universal. Boston fans are no different from fans in any other sports-crazed town: If you perform, they will love you. If you don’t perform, be prepared to hear slander, libel, and words you never heard in the Bible.

Price performed in Sunday’s elimination Game 3 of the ALDS, coming out of the bullpen to stop the bleeding when it looked as if the Red Sox would suffer an ignominious sweep for the second straight October. Price’s four innings of shutout relief kept the Astros on the launch pad while the Sox ran to a 10-3 season-saving victory over Houston.

“The story of this one is David Price,’’ said Sox manager John Farrell, who might have been saved from the gallows with this win.

Fans embraced Price for his outing, but Price made it clear that the reaction of the fans does not matter to him anymore. It’s about his teammates and the team now. Only them. Price dodged two attempts to get him to say he appreciated the new support in the stands.

“It feels good to put up zeros in the playoffs,’’ he said when asked about the fans. “That’s why I signed here . . . I think we all understand how good of a team we have in this clubhouse and just focus on today.’’

Right, but were you feeling the love from the fans, David?

“Yeah, that was a good performance.’’

All right, then.

Price knows what it’s like to be the target of Boston fans and media. He’s been in the crosshairs since he signed his whopping $217 million contract in the winter of 2015-2016. In Price’s first season with the Red Sox, he produced a 17-9 slate and led the majors in innings pitched. But fans never warmed up to the petulant portsider. After his pitiful 3⅓ -inning losing stint vs. the Tribe in the playoffs, Price was bludgeoned by fans, sports talk shows, and (social and mainstream) media. Folks even went after his lovable dog, Astro.

All the bad karma carried over into this season when Price went on the shelf with elbow woes, then came back to the rotation and made himself the most unlikeable local sports figure in recent memory. The trouble started when he went off on a bunch of reporters in New York in May. That’s when he announced he would talk only on the days he pitched. He said that from now on, it was all about his teammates and nobody else. Not even the fans.

Swell. But things got really ugly in July, when details of Price’s cowardly airplane ambush of Dennis Eckersley were unveiled. Price never apologized for the tantrum and never backed off his position. He certainly seemed to have the backing of his teammates. He went back on the DL in late July, and we didn’t see him again until the Sox made him Javier Lopez in mid-September.

None of it matters if he can keep pitching like this. Price has been terrific out of the pen, never better than Sunday. His four-inning relief stint against the ’Stros was longer than his playoff start with the Sox in Cleveland last year. It was longer than Drew Pomeranz’s start in Game 2 of this series and way longer than Doug Fister’s 1⅓-inning effort at the start of Game 3. It was as long as Clay Buchholz’s start in Game 3 of last year’s sweep at the hands of the Tribe. It was nothing short of spectacular. It extended Price’s scoreless string out of the bullpen to 15⅓ innings; he has not given up a run since his last start, in Anaheim, July 22.

He said he’ll be fine to pitch in Game 4 or Game 5. But he wants everybody to know that this is a temporary situation.

“I can do this as a starter, too,’’ he said. “I just haven’t done it yet. Period. Pitching suits me well and that’s what I did. It has nothing to do with relieving or starting. I just threw the ball well today . . . Coming out of the bullpen, I mean, if I throw well out of the bullpen that doesn’t mean anything. I got to do this as a starter. I know that, y’all know that, y’all write it, and it will be talked about . . . whatever the team asks me to do, that’s what I’m going to do. They know I want to start. They know I want the ball.’’

There you go. Suddenly, the Red Sox are alive, and they have the best bullpen weapon since . . . Dennis Eckersley.

Price could move into the David Ortiz pantheon if he takes the ball to start a Game 5 of this series, then leads the Red Sox to World Series glory. But he says he doesn’t care about the reception anymore. It’s just him and the guys in the clubhouse.
