Park plan envisions fun — and flood control — along Fort Point

By Tim Logan Globe Staff  September 20, 2017

Boston officials and one of the state’s biggest conservation groups are launching a bid to create a string of parks along Fort Point Channel that would rejuvenate the old industrial canal and — they hope — help protect low-lying neighborhoods from the threat of rising sea levels.

The Walsh administration and the Trustees of Reservations on Wednesday unveiled plans for a “landmark” network of parks along the channel, which runs from Boston Harbor to the railyard between South Boston and the South End. The spaces would be connected by pedestrian bridges, marshlands, and artificial islands.

Designs are preliminary, and both the timing and the cost remain unclear. Financing, the Trustees said, would come largely from private donations and developers, though the park would probably be city-owned, and some public funding is possible.

Mayor Martin J. Walsh said the parks could boost a rapidly changing swath of the city.

“These parks will protect not only Fort Point but also downtown, Chinatown, and South Boston” from rising sea levels, Walsh told a group of business leaders. “They will foster new possibilities for growth from South Station to Widett Circle. They will be a new jewel in our park system, accessible by public transit in a Boston for everyone.”

Today, much of the area is dominated by a huge Postal Service distribution center on one side of the channel and Gillette’s World Shaving Headquarters on the other.

The project will be spearheaded by the Trustees, who manage more than 46,000 acres of conservation land around Massachusetts and has been working for two years to develop one or more major parks on Boston’s waterfront.

The group has studied several sites, including spots in East Boston and a decommissioned dry dock in the Seaport. But with the urging of the Walsh administration, the group chose to start with Fort Point because of its central location and the chance to design parks in an area prone to flooding.

“Fort Point Channel presents a unique opportunity, in terms of its location,” said Nick Black, managing director of the Trustees’ Boston Waterfront Initiative. “It has the potential to be a place where we can connect downtown to South Boston and the Seaport, as well as to Dorchester Avenue and neighborhoods along it.”

The Trustees said the plan is in its early stages, and a conceptual drawing released by the nonprofit group did not show much detail. Black said the project could include filling in some of Fort Point Channel, to create more green space in an area where most land is privately owned — and expensive.

Black said the Trustees hope to minimize the amount of land that would need to be purchased for the park and would probably turn to developers to help finance it. Some developers in the city’s “100 Acres” zoning area for Fort Point — which calls for blocks of new housing and office space near Gillette’s campus east of the channel — already contribute to parks funding as part of their projects. The Trustees also will probably seek to raise funds through corporate and other private donations, Black said.

Boston’s parks commissioner, Chris Cook, said the city will soon solicit ideas for the park from the public. The city has environmental goals, Cook said, but also wants to create a place Bostonians will come to love, one that connects to park networks and neighborhoods across the city.

“The idea is to create green space that can absorb water,” he said. “The trick with that is to make sure it is relevant the rest of the time.”

That’s a smart strategy, said Laura Marett, a landscape architect at the architecture firm Sasaki who works on climate-resiliency projects. A recently issued report, “Climate Ready Boston,” identified Fort Point Channel as a throughway that could allow water to inundate low-lying areas of the South End and Roxbury. Shoring it up — by changing the topography, installing water-absorbing marshlands, and giving water someplace to flow safely — could have great benefits.

“This is a pathway where water would enter the city,” she said. “It’s really a key strategic place that they’ve identified.”

It’s also one of the hottest pockets in Boston’s real estate market. General Electric Co. is building its new world headquarters along Fort Point Channel, and Amazon.com Inc. recently announced plans to move 900 employees into a building next-door on Summer Street. A corner of the South End where the waterway peters out is home to hundreds of new apartments and condos. And more complicated sites such as Widett Circle and the Postal Service complex have long been eyed for redevelopment.

Great parks in the neighborhood could make those projects more attractive, Walsh said.

Neighborhood groups said they were intrigued. Interesting parks that enliven the channel while reducing flood risk would be welcome, said Rachel Borgatti, executive director of Friends of Fort Point Channel.

“We can have amenities for people and for the environment,” she said. “There are so many cool things we can do to bring people to the water.”

That’s what the Trustees officials want to do. The group launched its park-planning effort about two years ago with funding from the nonprofit Barr Foundation, part of Barr’s campaign to push for better public waterfront access amid surging development along Boston Harbor. So far, Barr has donated $1.85 million to the effort, said president Jim Canales.

Black and Canales said they expect the Trustees will continue working on other waterfront parks in East Boston and the Seaport. Walsh said he’d like to see that happen. Fort Point is the place to start, Black said, but it’s not likely to be the end.

“We see this waterfront initiative as a broader long-term project,” he said. “It’s not just one park.”

Jon Chesto of the Globe staff contributed to this report. Tim Logan can be reached at tim.logan@globe.com.
