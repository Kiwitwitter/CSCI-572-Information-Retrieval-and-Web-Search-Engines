ALDS, GAME 3: RED SOX 10, ASTROS 3

Red Sox come home with a vengeance, throttle Astros

Barry Chin/Globe Staff

It was high fives all around after Rafael Devers (right) belted a two-run home run in the third inning to give the Red Sox a 4-3 lead.

By Peter Abraham Globe Staff  October 08, 2017

Game 3 of the American League Division Series against the Houston Astros on Sunday started much the same way the first two did for the Red Sox, their starting pitcher getting pummeled in the first inning.

The crowd at Fenway Park was booing after Doug Fister allowed three runs four batters into the game.

But when center fielder Jackie Bradley Jr. returned to the dugout, he sensed no panic among his teammates.

“It was early,” he said. “We never thought it was going to be easy. We were willing to accept the challenge.”

In a must-win situation, the Red Sox roared back to beat the Astros, 10-3.

“This was a fun day here,” said John Farrell, who well could have been fired had the Sox not reacted to adversity in the convincing way that they did. “Just an energetic day all the way around.

Game 4 will be Monday at 1:08 p.m. Rick Porcello will pitch for the Sox against Charlie Morton.

A 10-run swing required contributions from across the roster.

Rookie Rafael Devers’s three-run homer in the third gave the Sox their first lead of the series. David Price then worked four shutout innings, holding down the potent Astros.

Price had the sellout crowd behind him from the first pitch, the noise building as he threw 57 pitches in all. When Price finished the seventh inning with a strikeout of Marwin Gonzalez, he received a standing ovation.

“By far,” Price said when asked if it was his best moment in a Red Sox uniform.

No Red Sox pitcher had worked four or more shutout innings in a postseason game since Hall of Famer Pedro Martinez’s legendary six no-hit innings against Cleveland in Game 5 of the 1999 ALDS.

With Price as the centerpiece, Farrell was able to coax 7⅔ shutout innings from his bullpen.

“David Price took over from the mound today,” Farrell said.

Hanley Ramirez came out for introductions waving a green “Believe in Boston” flag. He then went 4 for 4 with a double and three RBIs to spark a lineup that had scored only four runs in the first two games of the series.

“It’s about winning here. This attitude we have, everybody here wants to win and we just tried to continue that legacy to keep going on this team, to keep it going,” Ramirez said.

The Sox had 15 hits in all, every starter outside of Xander Bogaerts collecting at least one.

Mookie Betts also played a leading role. He robbed a three-run home run from Josh Reddick in the second inning, saving the Red Sox from falling into a 6-0 hole. Betts had nine putouts in all, covering what seemed like every blade of Dave Mellor’s well-tended grass in right field.

It was the first playoff win for the Sox since the clinching Game 6 of the 2013 World Series. They snapped a five-game postseason losing streak.

The game started as poorly as would seem possible for the Red Sox as Fister allowed three runs in the first inning.

George Springer led off with a single, moved up on a wild pitch and scored on a single by Reddick. Carlos Correa than homered to center field as the crowd booed.

That Fister was hit hard in the first inning was no surprise. He gave up at least one run in seven of the last eight starts in the regular season, 11 runs in all.

Red Sox starters have a 15.12 earned run average in the series, allowing 14 runs on 18 hits — six of them home runs — over only 8⅓ innings.

The Astros are 7 of 16 in the first inning, scoring seven runs and hitting four home runs.

Fister came into the game with a 1.78 ERA in eight previous playoff starts. The three runs were the most he has allowed as a playoff starter.

Fister returned for the second inning and walked Carlos Beltran. Yuri Gurriel followed with a single.

With one out, Joe Kelly came in. He escaped damage only because Betts tracked down a deep fly ball by Reddick just before it would have landed in the front row for a three-run homer.

It was a catch reminiscent of Dwight Evans robbing Joe Morgan in Game 6 of the 1975 World Series.

Betts credited the fans with giving him enough space.

“In Fenway Park, everybody understands baseball,” he said.

Mookie Betts made like Dwight Evans and took a home run away in right field.

The catch seemed to inspire the Sox in the bottom of the inning.

Mitch Moreland and Ramirez led off with singles before Devers drew a walk to load the bases. Sandy Leon lined an RBI single to left.

With two outs in the third inning, the Sox struck again.

Moreland and Ramirez had doubles to cut the lead to 3-2. Houston manager A.J. Hinch then called on veteran lefthander Francisco Liriano to face the lefthanded-hitting Devers.

Liriano left a slider up that Devers crushed, the ball sailing over the Red Sox bullpen and into the bleachers.

It was the first lead of the series for the Sox.

Price came out of the bullpen to start the fourth inning. Four innings later, the lead was still one run. The lefthander allowed four hits and a walk but never cracked.

Price has worked 6⅔ scoreless innings in the series.

“If there was any doubt on what players are willing to do or what they can do, today shows you that, what playoff baseball will bring out of you,” Hinch said.

A six-run seventh inning put the game away. Ramirez had a two-run double and pounded his chest at second base, looking very much like Shane Victorino in Game 6 in 2013. Devers delivered an RBI single and Bradley knocked a three-run homer down the line in right field.

“Got to have good stuff happen and we had good stuff happen today,” Price said.
