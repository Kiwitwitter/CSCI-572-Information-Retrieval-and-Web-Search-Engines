A welcoming leader steps down in Roslindale

By Brian MacQuarrie Globe Staff  August 25, 2017

Nancy Braceland cannot walk more than a few minutes in her Roslindale neighborhood without someone waving and calling her name.

“Sister Nancy!” a young man shouts down from the second-floor balcony of his subsidized apartment near Washington Street.

“Hi, how are you doing?” Braceland replies with a smile from the sidewalk. “It looks pretty nice up there.”

A few yards later, the 81-year-old nun greets another of her neighbors.

“We’re going to get rid of this smell,” she says, grimacing at the odor emanating from a leaky sewer line.

Chances are she will, just as this Sister of St. Joseph has been doing for nearly two decades in the thickly settled, immigrant-heavy area between Roslindale Square and Forest Hills.

Braceland retired Monday as the live-in director of Casserly House, a front-line resource since 2000 for immigrants in need of English classes, basic job skills, and a path to citizenship.

When they heard the news that Braceland had retired, some residents cried. Others expressed alarm. But the indefatigable Roman Catholic nun with sky-blue eyes and snow-white hair said this week that although she is stepping down, she isn’t leaving the three-decker that has helped countless newcomers find their way in this country.

“I’ll stay as long as I don’t get in the way of the program,” Braceland said.

The neighborhood program on Stellman Road has been a wellspring of education and advice since the Sisters of St. Joseph, searching for an underserved multicultural neighborhood, bought the building for about $370,000 in 2000. In celebration of 125 years of service in Boston, the order was seeking another challenge, a new way to give back.

In order to do the most good, they wanted to find a low-income neighborhood that also was struggling with violence. The nuns searched for months, but no place they visited combined the mix of needs they wanted.

“We told ourselves we’d give it one more month,” Braceland recalled. “You’re not sure if your plan is God’s plan.”

But when they arrived on Stellman Road, their search ended. It was a Sunday, police cruisers clogged the street, and “a million kids” were buzzing about. At that moment, Braceland knew she had found a home.

Finding a home was one thing; being accepted was another. Social service groups had come and gone, and residents were skeptical that this time would be different.

“It took a long time for people to believe in us,” Braceland said.

Today, Casserly House is a hive of activity. Adults from 19 countries who speak 11 languages are taught English in the morning. After-school programs are held in the afternoon. And immigrants are prepped for citizenship tests. Three nuns live upstairs.

The building also serves as a place for the community to gather and hash out both the mundane and the momentous in their lives, such as murders in the neighborhood. Nine killings occurred within a half-mile of Casserly House in 2003, but none have been recorded in the past four years, Braceland said.

City Councilor Tim McCarthy, whose district includes the neighborhood, said Braceland has been a forceful catalyst for change.

“That section of Roslindale has for a long time been forgotten. She was one of the first persons who sat down to talk about better services and a better voice over there,” McCarthy said. “Whether you’re Haitian, Dominican, Irish, or Italian, there is just no barrier for her.”

Just the other day, McCarthy said, Braceland called to ask if a neighboring family facing foreclosure could be helped.

Examples of her influence abound. When Evelyn Rosario Aguasvivas arrived from the Dominican Republic two years ago, she knew only a few words of English. Now, after language classes through the program, she speaks fluently and has gained confidence to navigate her adopted homeland.

“She cares a lot. She cares about everything that the community needs,” Aguasvivas said of Braceland. “She’s always there for us.”

The nun laughingly dismissed a suggestion that she is fearless. Still, she walks around her neighborhood with purpose and serene confidence, a gentle woman with a city sensibility.

“Somebody got hurt here,” Braceland said, gesturing toward a makeshift shrine on a dark, narrow path of cracked asphalt. Five candles had been arranged in a triangle beside an empty bottle of Hennessy cognac.

“I’ve lived in violence, and I need to understand what’s happening,” said Braceland, who once worked as a missionary in Peru. “How do we continue to live together?”

She stood outside Casserly House and pointed at the three-deckers across the street. There’s a family from El Salvador, Braceland said. There’s one from Mexico, another from Nigeria, and some from Bangladesh. And on and on.

“What we do on a very small scale hopefully will make a difference,” said Braceland, who was raised in Newton. “I know it makes a difference in the everyday life of the people around here. Part of it is, they have to find their voice.”

Braceland said her neighbors are keenly aware of the anti-immigrant rhetoric coming from Washington and elsewhere in the country. They worry that family reunification could be affected, she said. And the future for many immigrants already in Boston is clouded with uncertainty.

Braceland does not ask the immigrants who seek help at Casserly House whether they are documented. It’s better that way, she said. If she does not know, she can answer without hesitation if she is questioned by authorities.

Braceland credited a broad partnership for improved conditions in the neighborhood. The police, government officials, community groups, and a commitment by residents to band together have all fostered a sense of progress, she said.

Even though she has retired, at least formally, Braceland said she has no plans to leave a community she loves.

“You could stay in bed, you know,” she said with a laugh, noting that she had “slept in” that morning until 7.

“But if I’m going to have another life, I need to get started on it.”
