Boardroom battle at Procter & Gamble may be epic, costly

  October 08, 2017

Consumer goods

Boardroom battle
at Procter & Gamble may be epic, costly

Corporate board elections are usually dull affairs best left to investor newsletters and government filings. But the battle between Procter & Gamble and activist investor Nelson Peltz, who is seeking a board seat, has hallmarks of a heated political campaign: eye-popping amounts of spending, acrimonious exchanges, and aggressive grass-roots lobbying of the small shareholders whose votes are normally meaningless but, in this case, may have an outsize effect on the result. It’s the biggest corporate proxy fight in history, and staggeringly expensive; the two sides will have spent at least $60 million, and probably more, before the vote on Tuesday at the annual meeting in Cincinnati — a referendum on the leadership of a $235 billion company — parent of Boston-based Gillette, and other brands including Tide and Pampers — as its stock has languished for a decade. The 180-year-old corporation has more than $65 billion in annual revenue. Peltz, of Trian Fund Management, aims to revamp the company. Procter & Gamble is fighting Peltz’s attempt to get a seat, saying he is a late arrival to a corporate turnaround that is already underway. Peltz who owns a $3.5 billion stake but has said he is not looking to oust any board members or CEO David Taylor. “This is different from the normal activist slate,’’ said Charles M. Elson, director of the corporate governance center at the University of Delaware. “He seeks a change of direction, and I think he’s right. I think the company has stalled.” — NEW YORK TIMES

Energy

OPEC sees ‘extraordinary’ steps needed
for stable market in 2018

Oil producers are succeeding in rebalancing an oversupplied market but may need to take further steps to sustain the recovery into 2018, said OPEC’s secretary general, Mohammad Barkindo. Saudi Arabia and Russia are leading consultations between the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries and other major suppliers about the future of their agreement to cut oil output, Barkindo said Sunday in New Delhi. The pact expires in March. “To sustain this into next year, some extraordinary measures may have to be taken,” he said. Barkindo didn’t elaborate. OPEC and allied producers agreed in December to pare output to clear a glut and bolster oil prices. The cuts have helped revive crude, which had fallen to half its 2014 peak. Benchmark Brent crude ended trading Friday at $55.62 a barrel, down 2.1 percent for the year. OPEC plans to meet Nov. 30. — BLOOMBERG NEWS

Dove body wash ad sparks consumer backlash

Advertisement

The Dove brand sheepishly admitted it ‘‘missed the mark’’ with a not-so-vaguely racist advertisement that has made it the latest target of consumer rage. But many angry and befuddled Dove lovers spent the weekend wondering what mark Dove was trying to hit in the first place. The ire-inducing advertisement — a static compilation of four photos — was released Saturday. The first frame showed a dark-skinned woman in what appeared to be a bathroom, a bottle of Dove body wash in the lower right-hand corner of the picture. In subsequent frames, the woman reaches down and lifts up her shirt (and apparently the rest of her skin/costume) to reveal a smiling white woman. Dove has removed the post from its Facebook page and posted comments on social media Saturday saying it ‘‘missed the mark in representing women of color thoughtfully.’’ Offended Dove users erupted, and the company quickly apologized. But the two-sentence Twitter note and a slightly longer message on Facebook left it unclear what exactly the ad was intended to convey. ‘‘What exactly were yall going for?’’ one self-described Dove consumer said on the company’s Facebook page. ‘‘What was the mark. . . . I mean anyone with eyes can see how offensive this is. Not one person on your staff objected to this? Wow. Will not be buying your products anymore.’’
— WASHINGTON POST

Others wondered whether the problem was a lack of diversity at Dove. They pointed to historical examples of racist ads about soap so good that it apparently washes the melanin right out of your skin.

Social media

Facebook security chief warns
of dangers
to fake news solutions

Facebook Inc.’s chief security officer warns that the fake-news problem is more complicated and dangerous to solve than the public thinks. Alex Stamos, who’s handling the company’s investigation into Russia’s use of the social media platform ahead of the 2016 US presidential election, cautioned about hoping for technical solutions which, he said, could have unintended ideological bias. It’s difficult to spot fake news and propaganda using just computer programs, Stamos said in a series of weekend Twitter posts. “Nobody of substance at the big companies thinks of algorithms as neutral,” Stamos wrote. “Nobody is not aware of the risks.” The easy technical solutions would boil down to silencing topics that Facebook is aware are being spread by bots — which should be done only “if you don’t worry about becoming the Ministry of Truth,” he said. Stamos’s comments shed light on why Facebook has added 1,000 people to review its advertising, rather than attempting an automated solution. The company told advertisers it would start to manually review ads targeted to people based on politics, religion, ethnicity, or social issues. It’s trying to figure out how to monitor use of its system without censoring ideas, after the Russian government used fake accounts to spread political discord ahead of the election. — BLOOMBERG NEWS

Energy

Average US gas prices fall
7 cents thanks
to crude costs

The average price of a gallon of regular-grade gasoline fell 7 cents nationally over the past two weeks to $2.56. Industry analyst Trilby Lundberg of the Lundberg Survey said Sunday that the drop comes as crude oil prices decline and flooded refineries on the hurricane-battered Gulf Coast return to operation. Lundberg said she expects further price cuts as refiners make cheaper ‘‘winter grade’’ gasoline. The current price is 27 cents above where it was a year ago. Gas in San Francisco was the highest in the contiguous United States, an average of $3.11. The lowest was in St. Louis, at $2.20. The US average diesel price was $2.76, down a penny from two weeks ago. — ASSOCIATED PRESS
