People (and footage) lost and found

Ramona Diaz and POV

A view of Dr. Jose Fabella Memorial Hospital in Manila in Ramona Diaz’s “Motherland.”

By Peter Keough Globe correspondent  September 29, 2017

In “Motherland,” Ramona Diaz takes the style and techniques of Frederick Wiseman to one of the busiest maternity wards in the world, the ramshackle Jose Fabella Memorial Hospital in Manila in the Philippines.

At first glance, the place looks like a giant pajama party with more than 150 women crammed into beds lining an open ward, some chatting and laughing, their humble belongings surrounding them. But the women are impoverished. Many are in their teens and have just delivered babies, or are near term. All are bringing children into a world of desperate poverty.

The camera wanders about observing nurses, like kindly drill instructors, demonstrating the basics of maternal care, prescribing medication, and offering family planning advice to patients. Most decline the advice out of fear or family pressure.

Individuals emerge from the chaos. Waiflike Lea, who has just given birth to twins, already has one child and lives with her unemployed boyfriend in a home without electricity or running water. Worldly wise, wisecracking Lerma has delivered her seventh child; she advises others to avoid her situation and then leaves early, against medical advice, with her still ailing infant, to avoid racking up any more expenses.

In a surreal moment at the end of the film, government officials and the media crowd around a mother who is being honored for giving birth to the “100 millionth Filipino.” Meanwhile, outside in the rain, Lea, newly discharged with her twins, discusses with her boyfriend how they will pay for the taxi to take them home.

“Motherland” screens on Monday at 8 p.m. as part of the DocYard series at the Brattle Theatre. A Skype discussion with the director follows. The film can also be seen on Oct. 16 at 10 p.m. on the PBS series POV.

World’s best dad

At the age of 6, Charles M. Mully was abandoned by his parents in his village in Kenya and was forced to beg in the streets for his survival. He worked his way up from menial work to becoming a wealthy entrepreneur. But material success was not enough; he was determined to help other poor orphans.

To do so, he sold all his property and businesses and, despite having eight children of his own, began taking lost kids into his own home. He expanded his activities to found the charity organization “Mully Children’s Family,” which the film’s website says has rescued and rehabilitated more than 12,000 children throughout Africa. Scott Haze’s docudrama “Mully” tells the story of the travails and triumphs of man whose faith has guided him to create what has been called “The World’s Largest Family.”

“Mully” screens Tuesday through Thursday at 7 p.m. at the Regal Fenway Stadium 13 and other area theaters.

For more information go to www.FathomEvents.com.

Join the debate

Two documentaries presented by Emerson College’s Bright Lights film series offer insight into the intensifying debate over race in America.

Raoul Peck’s “I Am Not Your Negro” (screens Tuesday at 7 p.m.; a panel discussion led by professor Kimberly McLarin follows) takes as its starting point an unfinished manuscript by James Baldwin about the lives of assassinated civil rights giants Medgar Evers, Malcolm X, and Martin Luther King Jr. Peck combines readings by Samuel L. Jackson from that text and other Baldwin essays on race with archival footage of Baldwin interviews to create an eloquent, electrifying, and all too timely indictment of an inveterate American injustice.

Damon Davis and Sabaah Folayan’s “Whose Streets?” (Thursday at 7 p.m.; a discussion with Folayan follows) examines the uprising in Ferguson, Mo., following the killing of Michael Brown, an unarmed black teenager, by police. Davis and Folayan show you those streets from the point of view of the protesters seeking to reclaim them.

The Bright Lights series takes place in the Paramount Theatre, Boston.

For more information go to web.emerson.edu/brightlights/upcoming-events.

Not fair

Jamie Sisley and Miguel Martinez’s documentary “America ReFramed: Farewell Ferris Wheel” offers a unique insight into the ongoing immigration dilemma by examining the relationship between carnival owners and migrant Mexican workers.

The carnivals need cheap, reliable seasonal labor and the migrants are looking for livable wages. But although workers earn far more than they can expect in Mexico, they are still getting unfair pay and work long hours in sometimes hazardous conditions. Legal advocates have been pleading the workers’ case, but the carnival owners say that the changes sought will put them out of business and likely end an already struggling outdoor amusement industry.

Filmed over the course of six years, the film gives equal time to each side and shows in microcosm how complex the immigration problem really is.

“Farewell Ferris Wheel” can be seen on the PBS World Channel on Oct. 10 at 8 p.m. and will stream for free on Oct. 11 at www.americareframed.org.

For more information go to worldchannel.org/programs/episode/arf-s5-513-farewell-

Found art

For years the Found Footage Festival has been searching attics, closets, yard sales, landfills, and dumpsters for zany videos for their programs, stretching the boundaries of good taste and the definition of what constitutes a documentary. It returns to the Brattle Theatre with a new lode of buried treasure including instructional films made during the 1980s panic about Satan worship (among them the eye-opening “Law Enforcement Guide to Satanic Cults”), wacky bloopers and outtakes from North Dakota news stations, video of a “Welcome Home Desert Storm” parade featuring Roseanne Barr, and mystery entries culled from David Letterman’s VHS collection.

The festival takes place
Oct. 14 at 10:30 p.m. at the Brattle.
