HIGH SCHOOL TRACK

Having the right stuff for a good long run

John Cetrino for The Boston Globe

Brookline High cross-country standout Lucas Aramburo, center, took a recent practice run with teammates at Farm Pond Park in Framingham.

By Logan Mullen Globe Correspondent  September 29, 2017

When Lucas Aramburu approaches the starting line for a race, he’s not shaken by much. His mind is focused on the task of running his best race to that point. Fully expecting some measure of pain to be involved, he has one goal — to outrun the competition.

Just a junior now at Brookline High, he’s already made quite the impression on the local cross-country scene.

In last year’s All-State meet he finished in third place. But that’s a tad deceptive, as he lost to the runner-up by mere milliseconds, crossing the finish line of the 5-kilometer at 15:59. The then-sophomore was the youngest finisher in the top ten.

With the bulk of his junior — and senior — seasons still to come, Aramburu knows he has much room for improvement, but he appreciates how much he’s grown as a runner.

His mellow disposition before races didn’t come naturally, he said.

“I used to be a wreck before races,’’ he said. “I would be incredibly nervous, and I would be practically just staggering to the starting line. But now I’ve realized that you have to go into it with a calm and collected demeanor, and just be ready to give it your all. But that also doesn’t mean that it’s a life-or-death situation. You’ve got to relax and do what you have to do.”

But Aramburu is just one of a crop of local high school runners who appear to have mastered what they need to do early on.

On the girls’ side, Natick’s Grace Connolly is the runner to beat in the state. Not only did she win the state meet last year as a sophomore, she trounced the competition. Her 5-kilometer time of 18:27 bested the runner-up by 58 seconds.

The first two years of running in high school can often be a compelling indicator of success. It’s not uncommon to see young athletes turn heads; for some of them, being able to maintain success can be the challenge. In Connolly’s case, her success is unmatched in the state in recent memory, allowing her to approach this season a little differently and focus more on long-term, national races.

“I’m trying to focus on not overdoing anything too early in the season,” said Connolly. “I’m trying to run a lot of the similar, big invitationals that I ran last year, because I knew that worked really well with my training and I went all the way up to December for Nationals.

She said she and Ashley Davidson, the Natick coach, “are just trying to mimic that same schedule this year.

“But I definitely think being a junior now and having another year of everything under my belt,’’ she continued, “ . . . gives me some more experience with the courses and just my feel in general with all the different girls.”

Of course, Connolly’s early success puts her very much on the radar of every opponent who comes down the pike.

“Every race that she’s in, people know her now,’’ said Davidson. “And . . . their goal is to try and beat Grace Connolly. There’s a lot of pressure there to just bring it all in and just focus on one race at a time, and that’s the one thing that I think we’re trying to do.”

But like Brookline’s Aramburu, Connolly appears to be blessed with the right disposition — in her case, a blend of confidence and cheerfulness — to handle it all well. That personality combined with candid conversations with Davidson about goals and what she can expect on a day-to-day basis should serve her well.

On the boys’ side of the local crosscountry world, parity is the name of the game. While last year’s state champion, Peabody’s Marcelo Rocha, has graduated, St. John’s Prep’s Tristan Shelgren, the runner who edged out Aramburu last year, is back as a senior. In fact, so are five more runners who finished in the top 15 last year, each of whom has his eyes set on the state crown.

So while it is important for Aramburu to focus on what he is doing and how he can create the best version of himself, part of his growth is not only looking at what other runners are doing, but becoming a better runner by competing against them.

“It would be lazy of me not to notice what the other guys are doing, how they’re performing,” said Aramburu. “I see them as competition, but I also see them as assets.

“We have in our state amazing runners,’’ he continued, “ . . . and as much as I seem them as competition, I see them as vehicles to improve myself.”
