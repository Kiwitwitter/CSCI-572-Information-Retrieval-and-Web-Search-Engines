Why do comedians talk about gun control? Because our leaders won’t

Chris Pizzello/Invision/AP

Jimmy Kimmel (pictured at the Oscars in February) spoke tearfully and angrily in his “Jimmy Kimmel Live!” opening monologue Monday night about the mass shooting in Las Vegas.

By Ty Burr Globe staff  October 03, 2017

Why is Jimmy Kimmel talking about gun control?

Because our governing class is too frightened to.

Advertisement

To say that late-night TV hosts have become our thought leaders on pressing national issues is nothing new. Pundits began wringing their hands about this development as soon as Jon Stewart and “The Daily Show” started outpacing the nightly news among wisenheimer millennials in 1999. And when Kimmel recently used his forum, “Jimmy Kimmel Live!,” as a lectern to personally and purposefully take on the Republicans’ latest attempt to dismantle the Affordable Care Act, not only did he actually move the needle of public discourse, but many onlookers granted him a new measure of respect.

Still, it has to be said: The only reason Kimmel and Stephen Colbert and John Oliver and Samantha Bee and an entire chorus of funny men and women have become our national town criers is that few others seem up to the task or willing to warm to it.

We had another gun massacre the other day; perhaps you’ve heard. Fifty-nine innocent people were mowed down in Las Vegas because an angry, insane person was able to amass at least 23 firearms in a hotel room. Purchased legally, it appears and thank you very much. It’s the worst gun disaster in American history — again. The last one, at a Florida nightclub, was 16 months ago.

The killer’s ideology remains unclear at the moment, but it doesn’t actually matter, not in the least. What only matters is that he was able to arm himself (again, legally) to act out his hatred of whatever in a way that caused the maximum amount of damage, sorrow, heartbreak, and loss of life. That he was able to do so, and that equally disturbed individuals continue to be able to do so, and that we are unable to meaningfully discuss the situation and enact laws to affect it only indicate that our society has become insane.

The Trump White House and other commentators weighed in on Monday that it was “too soon” to discuss gun control while the blood was still fresh on the streets of Las Vegas. Bill O’Reilly, the exiled king of Fox News, called the massacre “the price of freedom,” which may possibly be the single most sickening thing the man has ever said.

There were lots of calls for “thoughts and prayers” on social media, and a sharp backlash that felt perhaps that legislative policy might be more effective. Even supposedly progressive politicians had their feet held to the fire. Bernie Sanders tweeted, “It is long past time for Congress to take action on gun control to save innocent lives,” to which a number of people reminded him he voted against the Brady Bill no fewer than five times.

Others posted lists of politicians who have been funded by the National Rifle Association as well as the amounts donated to them, and that is getting closer to the heart of the matter. The NRA has spent so heavily on our elected representatives and has so polarized this country with a divisive propaganda campaign based on a fundamental misreading of the Second Amendment — the three words the organization doesn’t want you to think about are “well,” “regulated,” and “militia” — that it essentially constitutes an instrument of national sabotage. That’s even before you learn that certain high-ranking Russians are lifetime members of the NRA.

Why is there still no sensible, meaningful discussion of gun control in America, even after children in Newtown, moviegoers in Colorado, clubgoers in Orlando, and concertgoers in Las Vegas were shot to death? Because our politicians refuse to engage, whether they’re owned by the NRA or scared of losing the next election, or both? Why does the media fall short in applying pressure on those politicians? Because it’s over-invested in notions of “balance,” or terrified of the frothing fringe that promises to start a new Civil War if they can’t have their semi-automatic power fantasies? Why are the regulations that already exist so easily end-run through private sales and gun fair gray areas? Because the government’s scared of another Waco siege? At what point do we understand that our country is being held hostage and act accordingly?

Never, apparently, so it’s up to our entertainers to try to shame us into thinking clearly. Kimmel, who grew up in Las Vegas, spoke tearfully and angrily in his opening monologue Monday night, showing the faces of over 50 senators who voted against gun restrictions in the wake of last year’s Orlando shooting and accusing the Republican leadership of having “their balls in a money-clip” owned by the NRA.

Other angry voices speaking out from behind talk-show desks included Seth Meyers (“It would be so much more honest if [Congress] would just admit that your plan is to never talk about [gun control] and to never take any action”); Conan O’Brien (“the sounds of those automatic weapons last night are grotesquely out of place in a civilized society”); Stephen Colbert (“what, then, are we doing to combat ‘pure evil’? It can’t be nothing. . . . Doing nothing is cowardice”); and Trevor Noah (“If you say after a mass shooting is never the time, then you’ll never have the conversation in America because there’s a mass shooting almost every single day”).

When professional comedians are the only ones able to talk about this issue seriously, we’re in deeper trouble than we thought.
