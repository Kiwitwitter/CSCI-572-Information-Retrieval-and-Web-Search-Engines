Backstage at ‘Phantom of the Opera,’ a maestro runs the show

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Terry Byrne Globe Correspondent  September 22, 2017

Before the curtain goes up on “The Phantom of the Opera” at the Opera House, backstage is humming. Actors are warming up, dancers are stretching, dressers are milling about in front of racks of costumes, and stagehands are discussing the placement of set pieces in the wings. In fact, it’s surprisingly noisy.

“It’s a crowd,” says Heather Chockley, the production stage manager for the Broadway in Boston presentation, which runs through Oct. 1. “But this is the largest touring show, with 20 semis traveling with all of our equipment from city to city, and we need a lot of people. We have a travel crew of 20, plus 35 locals, including carpenters, stagehands, electricians, and dressers, not to mention the cast and production staff.”

But when Chockley calls “Places, please,” the crowd shifts into their positions for the opening scene, and she dons her headset and checks the seven small screens that give her a view of every angle of the stage. When she’s confident everything is ready, she calls for “house lights to half,” and then “go cue one.”

With that, the curtain rises and Andrew Lloyd Webber’s lavish musical love story unfolds, complete with visits to the Phantom’s lair beneath the opera house depicted in the story, a quiet cemetery with a looming monument, a theater producer’s office, an elaborate opera set, and a mirrored dressing room. Set pieces must roll on, a boat must cross a mist-filled stage, a 10-ton wall must turn, explosions must happen and, oh, yes, did I mention that a one-ton chandelier must come crashing down and hover over the audience?

Chockley is responsible for launching every one of the more than 400 light, automation, and special effects (including several pyrotechnics), making sure they happen at the precise moment they should, and that the performers not only are in the right place at the right time, but aren’t in danger of being crushed or run over by an errant set piece.

“Safety is our number one concern,” Chockley says.

As she says this, it’s time for the Phantom to lead the young opera singer Christine Daaé down to his lair. The pair appear on a balcony, and when Chockley says go, the show’s huge central curved wall turns and a set of stairs emerges from what appears to be a sheer facade. As the Phantom and Christine start descending, Chockley cues the stairs just ahead of them to appear and the stairs behind them to retract into the wall.

It’s a dramatic moment, enhanced by the sense that with one false move either one might fall into what is now a mist-filled abyss. What the audience can’t see is that each actor has a safety tether attached by an assistant stage manager and a carpenter for the descent.

“The bad news is that the two crew members are trapped in that closet space for the next 30 minutes, because the audience would be able to see them if they moved,” Chockley says. “They check their phones, catch up on news, chat. We did install a vent in there because it got kind of hot.”

There’s no time to worry about bored crew members, because Chockley must cue the Phantom’s gondola — a remote-controlled boat with hidden wheels that is guided through the mist to its exit spot with the help of an airport-style directional wand. She is constantly shifting her gaze from her script to the screens, not only to prepare for the next cue, but to make sure the actors are ready, and the next setup is complete.

“Things do go wrong,” she says, “and part of my job is to solve it before the audience notices.”

In fact, Chockley is remarkably relaxed and easygoing considering her responsibilities. During the show, a dresser, one of her assistant stage managers, an actor, and a stagehand come to her with comments or complaints, and she makes a note or a suggestion and moves on. She has the final word on anything and everything to do with the production during the run. To make sure she knows every member of the cast — and they know her — she and her two assistant stage managers rotate, so that each calls the show on a different night, and Chockley too, will spend the half-hour stuck in the closet at the top of the wall.

“We’re lucky the creative team remains very involved in the show,” says Chockley, who has been with the “Phantom” tour since a revamped version launched four years ago. “Because this is a long-running show, we have a rehearsal once a week, new cast members need to be brought in. When we come to a new city, I’ll call the first few shows, then watch from the audience and make notes for the dance captain or director if things don’t look quite right.”

Sometimes problems don’t become obvious until the tour is underway. The Phantom’s gondola, for example, kept getting bumped when it was stored off stage, so now after its scene, the boat is wrapped and hoisted above the wings. In the cemetery scene, a dusting of snow on the Phantom’s costume looked too much like dandruff, so he now wears a broad-brimmed hat to keep it off his shoulders.

Chockley, 38, has been working on touring productions as a stage manager for 15 years, including stints with “Les Miserables,” “Fiddler on the Roof,” and “The King and I” as well as serving as head of props for tours of “Sweet Charity,” “Joseph and the Amazing Technicolor Dreamcoat,” and “The Sound of Music.” She grew up in Michigan and now calls Washington, D.C., home; her husband just got a job as production manager at the National Theatre there.

“I got involved in theater sort of by accident,” she says with a smile. “I got a speeding ticket right after getting my license and instead of paying the fine, I had the option of community service. My service was volunteering for a community theater. I was hooked.” Later she would study theater at the University of Michigan.

Chockley says she has “a gypsy soul.” Good thing because the company doesn’t get too many weeks off. She says she is happy to continue with “Phantom” as the tour continues (next stop: Montreal). “This company really has a family feel,” she says, “which makes it fun to come to work every day.”

Chockley turns her attention back to the stage. The company is making a final curtain call — she never tires of hearing the audience roar their approval. Chockley calls the final cue, pulls off her headset, and turns to congratulate an understudy who had gone on for the first time that night.

“Did you hear everyone in the cast shouting, ‘Nailed it’ when you took your bow? Great job.”
