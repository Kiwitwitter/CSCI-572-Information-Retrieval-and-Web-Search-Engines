Brookline school settles claim it barred disabled child

Barry Chin/Globe Staff/file 2017

Dawn Oates’ daughter Harper (center), a quadriplegic who uses a wheelchair, was denied admission to Park School in Brookline.

By Michael Levenson Globe Staff  September 15, 2017

The prestigious Park School in Brookline violated the Americans with Disabilities Act and discriminated against a 4-year-old girl who uses a wheelchair when it denied her admission without properly assessing whether it could accommodate her, the US attorney’s office said Friday.

As part of a settlement with federal prosecutors, the elite private school agreed to provide children with disabilities an equal opportunity to attend and to not apply any application or eligibility criteria that screen out such children. The 129-year-old school also agreed to train admissions staff and remove architectural barriers to ensure the school is accessible to children with disabilities.

No fine or financial penalty was imposed, and the school was not required to admit Harper Oates, the girl who was at the center of the investigation. She now attends Dexter Southfield, another private school less than a mile away in Brookline.

William D. Weinreb, the acting US attorney for Massachusetts, said he hopes the settlement will serve as a blueprint for other private schools to ensure their admissions processes do not discriminate against children with disabilities. “Children with disabilities deserve equal opportunities to attend independent schools,” he said in a statement.

Harper’s mother, Dawn Oates, called the agreement “very fair.”

“While it’s a really, really great day for us and our family, it’s an even better day for every other family in America that won’t have to go through what we went through over the past year because of the outcome that was reached,” she said.

Despite signing onto the agreement, the Park School maintains that it did not discriminate against Harper when it denied her application for pre-kindergarten last year. The school said in a statement Friday that it will obey the terms of the agreement and has already begun training its admissions staff to comply with the Americans with Disabilities Act. The 1990 federal law forbids schools, movie theaters, restaurants, and other businesses from discriminating on the basis of disability.

“We take the DOJ’s finding very seriously, and our enhanced admissions guidelines are already in effect for the upcoming admissions cycle,” the school said, referring to the Department of Justice, which oversees the US attorney’s office. “We are also offering broader training for staff and faculty to ensure Park remains welcome and nurturing for all students, including those with disabilities.”

The Oates family always expected Harper would go to the Park School, which has 560 students in pre-kindergarten through eighth grade. Harper’s father, Justin, had gone to the school, and her 7-year-old twin siblings, Jack and Olivia, were already enrolled. But the school denied Harper’s application, saying that, despite conversations with the family and careful consideration, “serious concerns remained that the Park School could not meet Harper’s educational needs.”

Harper uses a wheelchair because she was born with a spinal cord injury that left her a quadriplegic. But her family says her disability is limited to her mobility. Cognitively, doctors have found Harper to be one to two years ahead of her peers.

The Oates family filed a complaint with the US Department of Education after Harper was rejected. The US attorney’s office later reached out to the family and opened an investigation. On Friday, the office said the investigation found the school violated federal disability law by failing to conduct “an appropriate, individualized assessment” of the modifications that would be necessary to accommodate Harper. Federal law requires schools to accommodate children with disabilities unless the changes they would have to make would “constitute a fundamental alteration” of a school’s services and facilities.

In a letter to the Park School community on Friday, Cynthia A. Harmon, the head of school, maintained that the school had “engaged in open, good faith discussions” with the family but ultimately determined that “we simply could not fully meet the child’s educational needs.”

“Please know that while The Park School has never purposely discriminated based on disability or any other status protected by applicable law, members of the administration take the DOJ’s findings very seriously,” Harmon wrote. “We are determined to do everything we can to ensure our admission process is as equitable as possible.”

Dawn Oates said she never hoped the settlement would result in the Park School being forced to admit Harper. “We didn’t want to have to bang a frying pan over anyone’s head to get our child into a school,” she said.

Last year, the family decided to enroll Harper and her siblings in Dexter Southfield. There, the admissions staff saw Harper as a person first and focused more on her capabilities and less on her disability, Dawn Oates said. She said Harper, who just started kindergarten, loves the school and “has a ton of friends.”“You want to be a part of a community where you’re welcomed, appreciated, and accepted, and we have that at Dexter Southfield now,” she said.

Travis Anderson and Cristela Guerra of the Globe staff contributed to this report.
