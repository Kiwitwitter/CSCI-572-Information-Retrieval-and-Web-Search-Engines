Newton split on Nov. 7 vote to shrink City Council

By John Hilliard Globe Correspondent  October 04, 2017

NEWTON — A ballot question calling for a new charter that would shrink Newton’s City Council and change how its members are elected has divided local leaders ahead of the municipal election next month.

In addition to picking a new mayor, voters on Nov. 7 will decide whether to reduce the size of the City Council from 24 members to 12, and put each seat to a citywide vote. The charter changes would take effect with the 2019 municipal election.

Opponents said the plan sacrifices neighborhood representation, as it eliminates the current practice of each ward electing its own city councilor.

“That ward resident represents me more closely, because if I don’t like what that ward [councilor] does, I can vote him out,” said Sallee Lipshutz, president of the Waban Area Council.

Losing that representation means local neighborhoods “don’t have a voice anymore,” she said.

Frieda Dweck, a cochair of the Yes for a New Charter Campaign, said the proposal would allow every voter to have a say in who serves on the City Council. Every councilor pays attention to local neighborhoods, she asserted, and are responsive to the needs of their wards.

“Newton is small enough that what is in the best interest of the city is in the best interest of the neighborhoods,” Dweck said.

Advertisement

The proposed charter is the product of an elected nine-member charter commission, which was formed in late 2015 to study ways to improve Newton’s existing charter to make the city more responsive to residents.

In a final report published in May, the commission said citywide elections would give voters more influence over the City Council, and a smaller board would be more responsive and effective.

“City councilors who answer to all voters are the best way to ensure that our villages are uniformly supported and thoughtfully integrated into the whole city,” commissioners wrote in the report.

Under the commission’s plan, all 12 seats would be decided by citywide votes. Four councilors could live anywhere in the city, while the other eight must live in the ward they represent.

The current City Council has 16 members elected by voters across the city and eight councilors elected only by voters of the wards they represent.

The charter would also impose term limits for city councilors and the mayor’s office, plus provisions for more public comment during committee meetings and other measures intended to improve communication between residents and city leaders.

Mayoral candidates Ruthanne Schwartz Fuller, a ward 7 councilor-at-large, and Scott F. Lennon, a ward 1 councilor-at-large who is also council president, both support the charter commission proposal. Mayor Setti Warren, who is leaving office to run for governor, also backs the plan.

The Nov. 7 charter question also has the support of the Newton-Needham Regional Chamber, which said in a statement that the proposal “would be vastly superior to the city’s current city council configuration.”

Susan Flicop, president of the League of Women Voters Newton, said her organization supports the measure. With citywide elections for councilors, “voters can vote for 100 percent of the city council,” she said.

But running a citywide campaign for office would be costly and difficult for many Newton residents, argued Emily Norton, a ward 2 councilor. Norton, who is elected by residents of her ward alone, said having ward-only elections can help improve economic diversity among city councilors.

“You want to lower barriers, not limit them to the well-off and connected,” said Norton, one of 15 councilors who oppose the charter proposal. “You are going to have a whole [group] of people who can’t compete and be a part of a pool of candidates.”

Concerns over the proposed charter have also spurred 14 city councilors, including Norton, to back an alternative measure, should voters reject the charter commission’s plan at the ballot in November.

The councilors’ measure would replace the existing board with 16 members. Under that plan, half would be elected by a citywide vote as councilors-at-large. It would also retain the current practice of having voters in each of Newton’s eight wards elect their own councilors to the board.

“There are local issues that don’t matter to the rest of the city, and you want someone who has to care,” said Norton.

Norton’s ward 2 colleague, councilor-at-large Jake Auchincloss, dismissed the alternative as a political maneuver intended to “muddy the waters ahead of the election.” Auchincloss is among eight councilors in favor of the charter commission’s proposal.

“This is their decision,” Auchincloss said of voters. “They should judge the charter commission’s recommendation on its merits alone.”

The division over the charter issue could give ward 4 councilor-at-large Amy Mah Sangiolo — who came in third in the September preliminary vote for mayor — a way to shape the mayor’s race in its final weeks. Unlike Fuller and Lennon, Sangiolo supported the 16-member alternative plan.

Tim Snyder, a supporter of Sangiolo’s mayoral run, said he opposed the charter commission’s proposal and backed Sangiolo partly because of her objection to the plan.

Snyder asserted the commission’s plan would hurt local representation on the board, and called the Nov. 7 vote a “critical issue” for the city.

“It is something that is a high priority for a lot of Amy’s supporters,” said Snyder.
