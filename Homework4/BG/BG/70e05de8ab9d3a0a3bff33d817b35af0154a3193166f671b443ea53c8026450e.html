LOCAL FARE

Dining at Beauport is like taking a luxury cruise

Kathy Shiels Tully

The seafood bouillabaisse, a large bowl filled with generous portions of shrimp, swordfish, mussels, and lobster.

By Kathy Shiels Tully Globe Correspondent  October 05, 2017

IN THE KITCHEN Executive chef Bill Poirier — former chef and partner at Sonsie, the acclaimed restaurant in Boston — has only been at the 1606 Restaurant at the Beauport Hotel in Gloucester since mid-September.

Modern American fare with a strong local influence is the concept he is developing with a new seasonal menu, due out this month, to illustrate fall/winter .

Poirier is looking to connect with local fisherman and farmers in the Cape Ann area to utilize what’s available, said hotel general manager Ray Johnston, and “transform it to something terrific.”

THE LOCALE The site of the former Birds Eye frozen food factory, vacant for many years, has been completely transformed. Only the breathtaking ocean views remain.

Named “Beauport” (French explorer Samuel de Champlain, who discovered the area in 1606, called Gloucester “Beau Port,” or beautiful harbor), Peter Niemitz designed the restaurant and hotel using inspiration from grand New England hotels of years past. Even more, it conveys a sense of being aboard an elegant cruise ship.

Limitless ocean views stretch out to the horizon, no matter where you sit: inside at the 44-seat bar, inside the 110-seat dining room with floor-to-ceiling windows and dark wood paneling lining the walls, or outside on the 120-seat deck where one small section has a roof overhead wicker seating. The larger, open section has tables with rocking chairs around a fire pit. Guests can borrow cuddly blankets on cool evenings.

The Beauport Hotel has been open more than a year. But since we’ve not been guests, the thought of dining at the 1606 Restaurant never occurred to us until friends who live in Gloucester told us it’s open to the public.

Picturing a stellar sunset sinking into the ocean, we headed there, forgetting to consult Mother Nature. It being New England, the most dense fog rolled in, engulfing Gloucester.

Weather, however, didn’t stop the many hearty locals and out-of-town guests who dined outside, warmed by pyramid-shaped gas patio heaters.

The moist, herb-crusted native haddock, served with roasted fingerling potatoes and garlicy broccolini.

ON THE MENU A bowl of creamy clam chowder ($10) seemed the perfect starter for the night. Chock full of clams and minced potatoes, its consistency was — unlike the fog — not too thick. One surprise was an unidentifiable, untraditional background taste of heat. The extra ingredient wasn’t listed on the menu.

Entrees aim to please everyone at the table, from roasted Statler chicken ($25) to char-grilled New York sirloin ($34), and of course the local favorite, boiled Gloucester lobster (1.5 lbs., market price, $30 when we visited).

The seafood bouillabaisse ($27) was a gift from the sea — a large bowl filled with generous portions of shrimp, swordfish, mussels, and lobster — all swimming in a saffron broth, easy to soak up with the hearty chunk of herb-grilled crostini.

The native haddock ($27) was a much smaller dish, much to my hungry husband Joe’s dismay. A small but moist portion of herb-crusted haddock sat atop a handful of roasted fingerling potatoes and garlic broccolini that could be dipped into a swirl of tangy Meyer lemon butter sauce.

For a sweet finish, we shared an apple crisp ($8) served in a mini-cast iron skillet with vanilla ice cream that melted onto thick slices of juicy, cinnamon-flavored apples, topped with a light sprinkling of oatmeal.

The meal was delicious, but service was slow, from drinks to dessert, during our much-anticipated experience. But like the sun that rose the next day, we will return and give 1606 another chance.

1606 Restaurant at The Beauport Hotel, 55 Commercial St., Gloucester. 978-282-0008,

beauporthotel.com.
