TCAN made believers out of Natick

Rose Lincoln for The Boston Globe

Executive director David Lavalley and operations manager Erin Basile show off the new movie theater at The Center for the Arts in Natick.

By Lenny Megliola Globe Correspondent  October 06, 2017

NATICK — It seemed like a dreamy long shot, turning the old, cob-webbed firehouse into an artistic jewel in the heart of downtown.

The building went back to 1875. It had historical and sentimental value to some older Natick residents. “A lot of folks wanted to preserve that history,” recalled Dave Lavalley.

Still, the firehouse had seen better days. There was talk of leveling it and building a much-needed parking lot when it closed in 2000. It was also open for purchase, but it would be costly to renovate and still preserve its old-timey facade. Any takers?

Enter The Center for the Arts in Natick, which had been operating out of a small storefront on Main Street.

When the firehouse closed, Michael Moran, who started TCAN, saw an opportunity.

“Michael was a passionate, outspoken patron of the arts,” said Lavalley, who succeeded Moran as executive director. A fund-raising campaign raised about $1 million. A generous loan from an “angel donor” nearly matched that. Suddenly, TCAN was in business. Volunteers cleaned up the place. The first floor became a theater space with a small lobby.

It was a modest beginning. “We brought in local talent and coffee house performers, a lot of amateurs,” Lavalley said. “There was nothing going on in the early 2000’s. We thought an arts center would revitalize downtown.”

Today, two decades after its founding and 14 years after its move to the firehouse, TCAN anchors Natick’s cultural scene with concerts, theatre productions, literary events and art exhibitions. On Oct. 21, it will hold a 20th anniversary benefit featuring Grammy Award winner Lisa Fischer and Grand Baton.

Jay and Erica Ball have been TCAN mainstays from the start. “My wife and I were looking for something to do after dark in Natick,” said Jay, a former TCAN board member. “Michael Moran had bigger ideas than just a coffee house. He had a vision. Was he controversial and a curmudgeon at times? Yes.”

The board ultimately fired Moran in 2004. “But there’d be no TCAN without him,” said Jay Ball.

Moran went on to open the Amazing Things Arts Center in Framingham, retiring as executive director in 2012.

“Anyone could have done what I did,” said Moran, who now lives in Dudley. “They just had to really want to do it and put their nose to the grindstone. It was an idea and a whole bunch of work....we won the town over.”

After the move to the firehouse, TCAN started booking bigger names. Richie Havens. Jonathan Edwards. Leon Redbone. It was starting to click. “We wanted to draw different audiences,” said Lavalley. Singers, musicians, comics, bands. In came Judy Collins, Don McLean, Jefferson Starship and standup comics like Paula Poundstone, Jimmy Tingle, and Robert Klein.

In January, TCAN opened a state-of-the-art movie theater on the second floor with 120 seats and a 20-foot screen for showing foreign and art-house films. “TCAN is not just a performance space anymore,” said Erica Ball.

“You don’t have to go to Newton, Cambridge or Brookline to see these films anymore,” said Lavalley. Anyone up for “The Maltese Falcon,” “The Rocky Horror Picture Show,” or “Two Trains Runnin’”? They’re booked for October. You won’t see them at Framingham’s behemoth AMC complex.

“About a third of our [movie] audiences come from Sherborn, Wayland, Needham, Wellesley and Natick, a third from other Metrowest towns and a third from Worcester and Boston,” said Lavalley.

The organization wants to take driving out of the equation. “People don’t have to trek to Boston to see a show,” said Arlington resident Libby Smelker, TCAN’s new head of fund-raising.

Natick’s downtown population has risen as numerous apartments have gone up in the last few years, all of them within walking distance of the center. “There’s intrinsic value in having arts so accessible,” said Snelker.

TCAN has formed a partnership with the \Walnut Hill School for the Arts, which is just a 10-minute walk away. “We’re two of the biggest [theatre] players in the area,” said Antonio Viva, Walnut Hill’s head of school. “We’ve worked closely since I got here six years ago. We’re getting the word out of the culture in Metrowest. We hosted TCAN’s gala about four or five years ago. We have the facilities and parking for it.

“I connected with [Lavalley] my first year here. I wanted to learn more about TCAN. We connected right away and got involved in a number of projects. We were off and running. David’s a great resource for us. We’re thinking about more joint ventures.”

TCAN has outlasted the doubters and fostered a multi-faceted entertainment destiny. Audiences have caught the rhythm. Downtown Natick shines brighter these days.
