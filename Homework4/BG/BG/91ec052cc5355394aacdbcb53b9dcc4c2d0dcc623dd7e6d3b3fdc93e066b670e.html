Actress Rebecca Hall, back in Boston, talks about ‘Christine’

Mike Windle/Getty Images for Entertainment Weekly

Actress Rebecca Hall at the Toronto International Film Festival in September.

By Mark Shanahan Globe Staff  October 20, 2016

British actress Rebecca Hall is back on our shores, shooting “Professor Marston & the Wonder Women” around Boston. (The film is about the unconventional romantic life of Harvard psychologist Dr. William Moulton Marston, creator of the feminist superhero Wonder Woman.) But Hall, who starred opposite Ben Affleck in “The Town,” is also promoting her new movie, “Christine,” the true story of Christine Chubbuck, a TV news reporter who committed suicide on the air in the 1970s. The movie screens Saturday at the Kendall Square Cinema, after which Hall will answer a few questions. We rang her up this week.

Q. Talk about the appeal of the character Christine for you. When you got the script, you cannot have thought this would be a terribly uplifting film.

A. When I read it, my first instinct was, yes, it’s a story about a woman who commits suicide. But the way the script was, and the way the film is, is something that in no way revels in darkness. In fact, there’s a huge amount of life in the movie and a huge amount of humor, and I found that to be moving. Also, I didn’t know anything about Christine Chubbuck until I read the script. I felt like keeping this person at arm’s length or looking at her from a distance would allow people to say, ‘What a shocking thing’ or ‘How far away from me she is’ or ‘What a horror story.’ But I actually feel like the film asks you to get very close. Here’s person and you’re going to spend some time with her suffering. I’m not going to diagnose what it is or tell you why she did what she did, but I am going to ask you to relate and to understand and to sympathize. Truth is, it’s quite straightforward to humanize people who do virtuous things. But I think it’s necessary to try to humanize people who we would rather look away from or people who challenge us or frighten us on some level.

Q. I think that’s one of the great achievements of the film. We do get a sense of how mixed up and sad your character is, yet she seems quite ordinary. She isn’t a caricature.

A. The film doesn’t say look at this person who did this crazy thing. It actually says we all know what it’s like to have a terrible day at work, and we all know what it’s like to feel depressed from time to time, and we all know what it’s like to go unloved or to feel lonely or isolated. What we don’t think about is how close the margin is between that being one day of your life and the circumstances that tip it over to tragedy, which is quite arbitrary — like brain chemistry or gender or social historical context. Yes, Christine’s unusual but there’s also something we all know about her. I’m not a fan of films that are slow and navel-gazing and don’t have any substance and depress you for no reason. I don’t think this film does that. I think it’s quite exhilarating and has so much humanity.

Q. It’s a good role, though I’m guessing you want to be in movies that are widely seen. Was there any part of you that said, ‘Gee, I wonder what the audience will be when they discover what the story is?’ Or does that not matter?

A. Of course it matters. But I don’t believe in patronizing an audience and I don’t believe in saying I’m only going to make one kind of anodyne film. I believe in pushing the boundaries. I believe in making good art. I mean, this is the kind of film I’d like to see. I find it stimulating and challenging and confusing and affecting. And it’s about something. It’s about a lot of things, actually. It’s meaty. It’s good. That’s reason enough to see it.

Q. There’s an existential vibe to the film, and I wonder, as you were playing this character, were you able to go out and have drinks with friends and be your normal self or were you kind of a wreck throughout?

A. I pretty much kept to myself throughout filming, but I also felt it was important to leave work when the work day ended. If doing this film or thinking about this person taught me anything about my own life, it taught me to live it and live it thoroughly and live it for who I am. I felt it would have been indulgent to let whatever I was feeling at work to carry through to my life. In a strange way, that felt like exploiting the material. That said, it wasn’t always easy and I’ve got endless amounts of gratitude for my husband, who came with me and lived with me during that period and I’ve never let a partner do that in my work life. Ever. I’ve always thought if I was going down a rabbit hole, I have to go down on my own.

Q. Talk to me about the objections of Christine’s family to the film.

A. My feeling on this is it’s hugely regrettable when any media, and I mean drama or news or whatever, covers actual stories and the people that are swept up in those stories don’t want them to be covered. That’s not something anyone takes lightly. That said, we still do it and art is the place for debate and analysis. I’m in no way condoning some sort of polemical drama. I respect that for the family this was a private trauma, but for the world it was a public tragedy and Christine left us with a lot of questions that we’re forced to grapple with or address or consider. Part of me does wish [the family] would see the movie because it is a fair and loving and generous portrayal of a human being.

Q. You’re currently shooting a movie in Boston, which you’ve done a few times before. . .

A. Yes, this is my fourth time back. I love it here.

Q. I’ll bet you say that wherever you go.

A. Probably, but I mean it. I went for a walk around Harvard Square the other day, which was fun. I ate at a very nice restaurant there, which I can’t remember the name of. Darn. That’s a shame. Anyway, it was a lovely.
