MFA partners with Japanese retailer Uniqlo

Pat Greenhouse/Globe Staff

Uniqlo employees learned art-making techniques at the MFA based on themes found in exhibitions at the museum. They will then teach classes at their stores.

By Malcolm Gay Globe Staff  September 15, 2017

The Museum of Fine Arts is joining the Japanese retailer Uniqlo in a unusual 10-year partnership that museum officials hope will showcase the MFA’s celebrated collection of Japanese art.

The partnership, which museum officials said involved Uniqlo funding in the “seven figures,” will not support exhibitions. Rather, it will fund a variety of programs centered on the museum’s Japanese collection and exhibitions, including art-making sessions at the museum and at Uniqlo’s Boston-area stores, as well as a Japanese film festival set to launch in 2018.

“It’s a significant commitment for both of us,” said Katie Getchell, the museum’s chief brand officer and deputy director at the MFA. “It will give us time to learn and grow and respond — to exhibitions, to what’s going on in the world, or other themes in Japanese culture.”

The partnership kicks off this month with art-making sessions at Uniqlo stores based on techniques and themes found in the MFA’s current exhibition “Showdown! Kuniyoshi vs. Kunisada,” and in the upcoming “Takashi Murakami: Lineage of Eccentrics,” which opens Oct. 18. Art-making at the museum begins in October and is geared toward families.

The museum is also developing Japanese-themed programming for its annual school vacation week in December, and this February the MFA will launch The Boston Festival of Films from Japan, featuring 10 films including Koji Fukada’s “Harmonium,” Murakami’s “Jellyfish Eyes,” and Konrad Aderer’s documentary “Resistance at Tule Lake.”

“The Japanese film festival we envision being an annual event,” said Getchell. “Other things will change year to year.”

Uniqlo, which arrived in Boston in 2015, has doled out sponsorships to a number of museums, including Boston’s Institute of Contemporary Art and the Museum of Modern Art in New York.

“The partnership with MFA was a natural decision for Uniqlo,” Justin Kerr, director of brand partnerships for the company, said in a statement. “The donation supports our Japanese heritage and connection to the Arts, but most importantly, it conveys our commitment to the Boston community.”

Getchell said that a brochure related to some of the programming will be available at the museum and Uniqlo stores, and that the partnership could eventually include a retail component.

“At this point we’re not talking about having Uniqlo products in the gift shop,” said Getchell. “We have talked with Uniqlo and have some designers here who are looking potentially at licensing images from our collection to be on Uniqlo products. If that came to pass I’d want to sell that in the gift shop.”

MFA director Matthew Teitelbaum said the partnership is an extension of the museum’s recently announced strategic plan, MFA 2020, to attract new audiences, while also serving the museum’s longer-range goal of renovating its Japanese galleries.

“It’s no secret that we are actively developing plans and creating advocacy in the community to rethink the galleries,” said Teitelbaum. “There’s no doubt that the energy that we believe we can create through new and sustained programming is going to lead to revitalized galleries. That is our plan.”
