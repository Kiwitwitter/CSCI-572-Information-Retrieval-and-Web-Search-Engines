A new 3-D film takes a gander at the Gardner Museum

By Peter Keough Globe Correspondent  September 15, 2017

In 2009, David Wilson, artist, designer and filmmaker, held the enviable position of artist-in-residence at the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum. During that time and for years afterward he delved into archival materials and took 3-D photographs and video of the galleries, collections, and exhibits. He returns now with the 3-D film he made from that material,
“Duerme Mi Niña” (“Mother Don’t Make Me Cry”) . It will be screened along with live narration by Wilson and a live performance of 17th-century Catalan music on Sept. 28 at 7 p.m. in the museum’s Calderwood Hall, 25 Evans Way.

www.gardnermuseum.org/calendar/event/duerme-mi-nina

Middle East missives

Gertrude Bell was a British spy, explorer, and political player who journeyed throughout the Middle East and was recruited by British Military Intelligence to help draw the border of Iraq after World War I. Zeva Oelbaum and Sabine Krayenbühl’s “Letters From Baghdad,” narrated and executive produced by Tilda Swinton, draws on documents from the Iraq National Library and Archive and Bell’s own letters to tell the story of a woman ahead of her time and a story about policy wranglings and secret dealings that explain a lot about what is happening in that part of the world today.

“Letters From Baghdad” will screen from Thursday to Sept. 29 at the Museum of Fine Arts, 465 Huntington Ave., Boston. Co-director Oelbaum will be present for a discussion after the screening at 8 p.m. on Friday.

www.mfa.org/programs/series/letters-from-baghdad

lessons from Costa-Gavras

Strictly speaking, Costa-Gavras’s films are fiction. But his “Z” (1969), about a conspiracy to assassinate an opposition party leader in Greece in 1963, and “Missing” (1982), about the 1973 Pinochet coup in Chile, masterfully merge the conventions of documentary and political thriller. His “The Music Box” (1989), about a Nazi war criminal, and “Amen” (2002), about the Vatican and the Holocaust, are pretty authentic and dramatically compelling as well. All four films will be screening in the MFA series “Costa-Gavras: Encounters With History,” curated by Boston College film scholars John and Susan Michalczyk.

Giving ‘Peace’ a chance

In recognition of the United Nations’ 36th annual International Day of Peace, on Thursday, the Kendall Square Cinema will be hosting on Tuesday night a screening of filmmaker and environmental activist Greg
Reitman’s documentary “Rooted in Peace.” It is a self-described “journey of self-analysis,” confronting a world in the throes of war, global warming, and overall spiritual malaise. Reitman travels from the United States to Costa Rica to India to discuss the situation with Deepak Chopra, Donovan, Mike Love, Pete Seeger, David Lynch, Nobel Peace laureate Mairead Maguire, Ted Turner, Archbishop Desmond Tutu, and others. Donovan and David Lynch? Count me in.

“Rooted in Peace” screens on Tuesday at 7 p.m. It will be followed by a question-and-answer session with Lama Surya Das, a Tibetan Buddhist and a participant in the film.

www.landmarktheatres.com/boston/kendall-square-cinema/film-info/rooted-in-peace

Doctors without borders

Advertisement

Undocumented immigrants confront many crushing difficulties these days, not the least of which is access to health care. Maxim Pozdorovkin’s documentary “Clinica De Migrantes” takes a look at the volunteer-run Puentes de Salus health clinic in South Philadelphia, which, despite scanty resources, treats around 10,000 patients each year. The film brings the volatile immigration debate from the abstract to the human level, showing how 11 million people may be forced to rely as a last resort on emergency rooms.

“Clinica de Migrantes” debuts Sept. 25 at 10 p.m. on HBO. It will also be available on HBO Latino, HBO On Demand, HBO NOW, HBO GO, and affiliate portals.

‘Food’ for thought

The debate over genetically modified organisms (GMOs) and whether they are a blight on our health and environment or a panacea for world hunger has passionate advocates on both sides. Scott Hamilton Kennedy’s documentary “Food Evolution” is not likely to resolve anything. Narrated by everyone’s favorite scientist, Neil deGrasse Tyson, it includes reports from papaya groves in Hawaii, banana farms in Uganda, and cornfields in Iowa, as well as interviews with numerous experts. The film also has its share of critics. Among them are some of those who participated in the film, including Timothy A. Wise from the Land and Food Rights Program of the Small Planet Institute in Cambridge.

“I was interviewed for the film,” he writes in an e-mail, “as an agricultural development expert at Tufts and a critic of GM foods as a solution to ‘feeding the world.’ I pulled my consent when it became clear that the filmmaker was biased and his funding came from a food technology group, which he did not disclose. The film is a very biased treatment of a controversial issue. Two of the prominent food writers in the film, Michael Pollan and Marion Nestle, have denounced it as industry propaganda in which their on-screen statements are taken out of context.”

So what’s the truth? Judge for yourself when the film screens at the Coolidge Corner Theatre on Sept. 24. Christopher Lydon moderates a post-screening event that includes Wise and Jing-Ke Weng, of the Whitehead Institute for Biomedical Research.

www.coolidge.org/films/food-evolution
