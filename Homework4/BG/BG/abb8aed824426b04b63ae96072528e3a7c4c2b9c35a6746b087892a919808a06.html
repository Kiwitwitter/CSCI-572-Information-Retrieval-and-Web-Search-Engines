Hitting all the polarizing topics

By Peter Keough Globe Correspondent  March 31, 2017

If any issue is more polarizing in America than gun control, it is abortion. Though the Supreme Court acknowledged a woman’s right to choose in its 1973 Roe v. Wade decision, the matter is far from being resolved. Since 2011, more than half the states have weakened that right with restrictions, none more so than Missouri, where only one clinic remains open and patients must submit to a 72-hour waiting period before having the procedure done.

“Abortion: Stories Women Tell” by Tracy Droz Tragos (director of the compelling 2014 documentary “Rich Hill”) interviews several Missouri women about this situation, not just those who believe in a woman’s right to choose but those who believe that abortion is wrong. There’s Amie, a single mother with two children who works 70 hours a week and must drive to a clinic 200 miles away to obtain an abortion pill. There’s Kathy, an anti-abortion activist who says that she was almost aborted, and so has always felt “a kinship with the baby in the womb.” There’s Erin, a doctor at the clinic who has had protesters show up at her house. And there’s Reagan, an anti-abortion activist for Students for Life of America who scoffs at the stereotype of abortion critics as old people holding up pictures of aborted fetuses.

“Abortion: Stories Women Tell,” debuting Monday at 8 p.m. on HBO, is an important documentary. It might not change many minds, but it could open a few.

For more information go to www.hbo.com/documentaries/abortion-stories-women-tell.

Women on wheels

The title makes it sound like some deranged horror film, but Joanna Sokolowski and Kate Trumbull-LaValle’s documentary “Ovarian Psycos” is about a subject that is more inspiring than scary. On the troubled streets of East Los Angeles, the band of the title is on patrol, young women of color out to confront and stop incidents of racist and misogynist abuse and violence.

Among those on wheels is Xela de la X, the first Psyco and a street artist and activist who has recruited fellow “Ovas” from this diverse working-class and immigrant neighborhood. They are a latter-day version of the Chicano Power movement in LA in the late 1960s, except now they tackle not just racism but the equally pernicious problem of sexism. And their bikes are really cool.

“Ovarian Psycos” can be streamed on the PBS Independent Lens website at www.pbs.org/independentlens/films/ovarian-psycos.

A massacre revisited

In a country almost inured to mass shootings, the massacre at the Sandy Hook Elementary School in Newtown, Conn. on Dec. 14, 2012, by a young man armed with an arsenal elicited a degree of grief, horror, and revulsion that has never been forgotten. Certainly not by the families and loved ones of the 20 children and six adults who were murdered. Along with teachers, doctors, and first responders, they recall the events of that day and its aftermath in Kim A. Snyder’s documentary “Newtown,” which premieres on PBS Independent Lens on Monday at 9 p.m.

For more information go to www.pbs.org/independentlens/films/newtown.

Truth and consequences

Jared P. Scott’s alarming documentary “The Age of Consequences” looks beneath the chaotic surface of terrorism, civil war, famine, poverty and disease to find one common cause: climate change. As a Pentagon spokesman puts it, this trend is bringing about the food shortages, droughts, extreme weather, and sea-level rise that are “accelerants of instability” and “catalysts for conflict” in vulnerable regions of the world.

These are not tree-huggers saying this, but admirals, generals, and military veterans who argue that climate change has instigated or contributed to the conflict in Syria, the unrest and repression of the Arab Spring, the rise of terrorist groups like ISIS, and the European refugee crisis.

Is there any hope of saving the planet? A seemingly endless field of solar panels presents an alternative to the direction in which we are heading; the real obstacle, the film suggests, is human stubborness and a failure of the imagination.

“The Age of Consequences” screens as part of the newportFILM series on Tuesday at 7 p.m. at the Casino Theatre in Newport, R.I. A discussion with producer Sophie Robinson and Dave Polatty, an associate professor at the US Naval War College, follows the screening.

For more information go to bit.ly/AgeDoc.

Pandemic panic?

Speaking of apocalyptic scenarios, how is that strange cough you just developed? In Janet Tobias’s documentary “Unseen Enemy,” CNN’s chief medical correspondent Sanjay Gupta and anchor Anderson Cooper investigate current and potential pandemics and what is being done about them. For those who complacently think such outbreaks take place only in poor countries far away, they point out that population growth, globalized commerce, and easy travel make such outbreaks everyone’s problem, as recent encroachments of Zika, Ebola, and the latest strain of the flu make clear.

Tobias interviews experts and caregivers such as Vanessa Van der Linden, a pediatric neurologist who alerted the world to the rise in microcephalic infants in the Zika outbreak; Soka Moses, a Liberian physician who left his family to treat Ebola patients; and Gwen and Terry Zwanziger, parents of a teenager who died of flu complications who now work to make the case for preventive vaccines.

Tobias’s proposed course of treatment? She urges a closer collaboration between medical professionals, researchers, governments, the media, and the public. Also, be sure to always wash your hands and cover up your sneezes!

“Unseen Enemy” can be seen on CNN on Friday (World Health Day) at 9 p.m. and midnight. In addition to the CNN telecasts it will also stream live for subscribers via CNNgo (www.CNN.com/go) and via CNNgo apps for AppleTV, Roku, Amazon Fire, and iPad. The film will also be available the day after the premiere on demand via cable/satellite systems and CNNgo.

For more information go to www.takesallofus.com.
