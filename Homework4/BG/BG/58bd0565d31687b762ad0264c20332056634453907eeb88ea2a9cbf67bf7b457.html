For family of kidney donors, love and medicine go hand in hand

By Steve Maas Globe Correspondent  September 07, 2017

Bruce and Laurie Karten call it the no-kidney zone.

It’s the space on the bed between the Needham couple. Bruce donated his right kidney to his brother 27 years ago, and Laurie donated her left kidney to her sister on March 28.

Organ generosity runs in the family. Last month, when Bruce’s brother’s kidney failed, their older sister provided a transplant.

“I feel that I’ve been blessed because of the kind of people they are,” said Claire Levovsky of Natick, mother of the Karten siblings and now a veteran of the waiting-room vigil.

Anne Paschke, media representative for the United Network for Organ Sharing, a nonprofit established by Congress to administer organ transplants nationwide, said she knew of anecdotal cases of two spouses being donors, but not of any official count.

The Karten story goes back three decades and is as much about the power of love as it is about the miracles of modern medicine.

When Louis Karten of Sharon got sick at 21 after a healthy childhood, doctors found that he had been born with just one kidney. The odds of that happening are one in a thousand, and in most cases people live normal lives. For reasons no one knows for sure, Louis’s kidney began to fail.

The bean-shaped organ, about the size of a fist, filters blood by removing toxins and other waste. As disease reduces kidney function, the organ must work harder and deteriorates faster.

A cascade of symptoms ensues, including severe fatigue, fuzzy thinking, labored breathing, and heart problems. Louis, who is now 51, recalls that his feet swelled to twice their normal size because his kidneys could not remove excess fluid.

After three years, he had two options for survival: dialysis, which could require being plugged into a machine for up to four hours, three days a week, or a transplant.

Fortunately, brother Bruce had two healthy kidneys, and testing showed that he was a 100 percent match to Louis, sharply reducing the chance of rejection.

Within a year, both brothers were back to leading normal lives. Louis proposed to his girlfriend, Carolyn Marcus, two months after the operation; despite some doctors’ doubts, they were able to have children — five of them, all boys.

Bruce, now 47, went on to marry Laurie Neiman and have three children, and he has run two marathons.

Life for Laurie’s older sister, Julie, was more challenging. Julie was diagnosed with Type I diabetes when she was 7. With careful monitoring of her insulin levels and injections, she kept complications at bay.

‘I tell all my kids’ friends in the swimming pool that it was a shark bite.’

The Sharon native met her future husband, Steve Schechter, at a high school summer program at Wellesley College. On “Sept. 27, 1985,” as they said in unison, they were going steady, and eight years later they were married.

When Julie was pregnant with the couple’s son in 1995, doctors noticed that she had kidney damage. About 30 percent of people with Type 1 diabetes develop kidney disease, according to the National Kidney Foundation.

Over the next two decades, Julie’s kidneys declined but continued to function; however, her diabetes exacerbated foot problems and led to two strokes.

In June 2016 as she was driving to her Wellesley home, Julie lost consciousness from a plunge in blood sugar, and crashed into a street sign. Normally, a glucose sensor would have alerted her before her level fell so far, but she had left it at the house to be recharged.

The next thing Julie knew she was in the hospital. While the crash did no lasting damage, tests revealed that her kidney function had dropped to 19 percent. She was now in the danger zone where her only choices were dialysis or a transplant. “I knew it would happen, but I didn’t think it was going to happen so fast,” Julie said.

She called her sister Laurie about a possible transplant. “I think it took her a second or so to process it,” Julie said. “Then she was like, ‘Of course, I’ll get tested.’”

After Laurie was found to be a perfect match, she cleared a series of tests to make sure she was physically and psychologically up to being a donor. Donors don’t have to be relatives, but the closer the blood and tissue match, the less likely the recipient is to reject the transplant and to require heavy doses of immunosuppressants for preventing rejection.

Dr. Amy Evenson, who implanted Julie’s kidney, said that the donated organ still functions for at least half the recipients after 10 to 15 years. Evenson, surgical director of living donor kidney transplantation at Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, said she performs a third of the 60 to 80 kidney transplants the hospital does each year.

She said that to prolong the donated kidneys, doctors treat the underlying conditions that led to the problem and are seeking ways to refine immunosuppressants, which over time can take a toll on the organ. Julie, now 48, hopes to receive a pancreas transplant within a year, which would cure her diabetes.

Donors and recipients each have their own medical teams. Donors are repeatedly told they can back out; the staff stands ready to come up with an explanation about why the transplant wasn’t possible, so that there are no hard feelings.

“They gave me a big booklet of all the cons of what could happen to me by giving this kidney,” Laurie said. “I never opened the book . . . because I said I’m going to do it no matter what.”

Then there was the question of her family. When Bruce had donated his kidney, he was single and 20. Laurie, 46, is the mother of three daughters, ages 10 through 19.

“My children always knew that Auntie Julie’s kidneys were failing and that ultimately Mom or someone would have to donate,” said Bruce. “Because [I] went through it and the technology is so much better now than it was then, they didn’t really second-guess it.”

In the months leading up to the transplant, Julie felt so fatigued that she rarely left her bed. When she awoke from surgery, she felt alive again.

Two months later, Julie was walking several miles a day. When she and her sister went out to the movies, Laurie told her, “It’s so funny to look at you and know a piece of me is inside of you.”

Laurie bears a six-inch scar from her operation. Bruce’s scar is 27 inches. Today, kidneys are removed through laparoscopic surgery, which requires only two small incisions. When Bruce was a donor, the operation required removing a rib. “I tell all my kids’ friends in the swimming pool that it was a shark bite,” Bruce said.

The morning after donating her kidney, Laurie went down the hall for a tear-filled reunion with Julie. Helping her maneuver was her husband’s sister, Susan Mancall.

It was fitting that Susan was there, as she was about to become a lifesaver for a sibling, too. She was doing for Louis what Bruce had done decades before — donating her kidney to him.

“I couldn’t be prouder of [Laurie]. She was a brave, unbelievable example of what to look for,” said Susan, who is 55 and lives in Framingham.

Like Laurie’s family, Susan’s was strongly behind the operation. She and her husband, Matt Mancall, have a son, 21, and a daughter, 18.

On June 13, Susan and Louis underwent transplant surgery at Mass. General. The next day, Susan said, “Louis walked to me before I walked to him. He came in at 6:30 in the morning.”

The transplant has drawn the two much closer. “He must call me three times today to say, ‘I love you,’ ” she said. “There’s nothing better.”
