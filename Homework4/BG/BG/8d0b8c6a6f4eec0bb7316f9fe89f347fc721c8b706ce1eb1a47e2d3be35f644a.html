The Weekender: Artists, martyrs, and envelopes

By Michael Andor Brodeur  Globe Correspondent  September 14, 2017

Want the Globe’s top picks for what to see and do each weekend e-mailed straight to you? Sign up for the Weekender newsletter here.

Well hello there folks! Welcome back for another wallop of The Weekender. We’ve got legendary artists, a hip-hop hero, a multiday dance party, and, well, a couple movies that maybe aren’t so great. Are we going to let that stop us? NO WE ARE NOT. Off we go:

MAMA MEDIOCRE: Copy editors aren’t the only ones on edge over “mother!,” the latest night sweat from director Darren Aronofsky, starring Jennifer Lawrence and Javier Bardem as a married couple who — actually, that’s weird enough for now. Moved and then unmoved, Globe film critic Ty Burr tosses two stars at the screen and says with “mother!” “it’s as if Aronofsky had taken a Roman Polanski film — ‘Rosemary’s Baby’ or ‘Repulsion’ — and thrown out the plot, leaving only the high cinematic anxiety.” Worth noting, it also “devolves into a muddy canvas of sub-Boschian Hell,” so . . . yeah. I totally get why you’d go, though. I will probably have to go see this, too. It’s fine. Opens Friday!

Advertisement

DRESS TO DEPRESS: I’m not sure what quite to do with Arcade Fire’s new album, “Everything Now” (described by the Globe’s Isaac Feldberg as “smarmy sophomoric awfulness”), nor their proposed dress code for their live shows (“hip & trendy”), or just this band in general, so I’m going to tell you three things: They are playing TD Garden on Friday; if you go, you’d best not be showing up in flip-flops or you’ll get carried out of the building atop the gust of 19,000 sighs of disappointment; and Arcade Fire multi-instrumentalist Will Butler recently had this to say to the Globe: “I think we’re probably better than the Rolling Stones right now.” He was kidding, but also kind of right, and I’m getting upset and really need the next blurb to be something good. Tickets here.

BOI HOWDY: OK, now we’re getting somewhere: On Sunday night at the Paradise, you can catch the one and only Big Boi, who has extended his post-OutKast output with the new “Boomiverse,” featuring appearances from Adam Levine, Eric Bellinger, Killer Mike, Gucci Mane, and the late Pimp C. We haven’t hopped on the horn with Big Boi since 2015, when he rocked a packed Sinclair. This time around, he’s got all sorts of new boom for the room (and plenty of classics to boot). Side note: I wouldn’t wear flip-flops to this either. You can still snap up the last of the tickets here.

THE TRUMAN SHOW: If you’ve followed recent local gasping news, you know all about the mystery and intrigue (I guess?) swirling behind the scenes of American Repertory Theater’s anticipated world premiere of “WARHOLCAPOTE” — a play adapted by director Rob Roth from hours of taped conversations between aspiring collaborators Andy Warhol and Truman Capote — which found Leslie Jordan (originally set to play Capote) withdrawing from the show due to “unforeseen personal circumstances,” leaving replacement Dan Butler to play Truman opposite Stephen Spinella’s Andy. I KNOW, RIGHT? (Well, art is what you can get away with, after all.) Fortunately, Butler will get a bit more than 15 minutes of fame; the show is up through Oct. 13 at the Loeb Drama Center. Find tickets here.

NOVEL IDEA: If you’re wondering where Burr’s other two stars went, see “Rebel in the Rye.” Or maybe don’t. The initially juicy but quickly dry biopic treatment of reclusive “Catcher in the Rye” author J.D. Salinger (Nicholas Hoult) “tries to tell the whole story instead of just a good one” — and ends up kinda phony. Kevin Spacey “crackles” as young Jerry’s creative writing professor, but soon it starts to feel like “the filmmakers are ticking every incident in the life off a checklist rather than shaping a coherent narrative,” and “you start to mourn for the actors.” Good grief. Opens Friday.

THAT GUY: If you are a Denis Leary person, you’re likely also familiar with Adam Ferrara from his stints on shows like “The Job” and “Rescue Me.” He’s also put in hours on “Nurse Jackie,” and will live forever through his role in “Paul Blart: Mall Cop.” Ferrara is also a skillful buster of chops as a stand-up comic (you can sample a solid bit of his on couples therapy here), and on Saturday night he comes to Laugh Boston for two sets (8 and 10:15 p.m.). Grab tickets here.

FIRE STARTER: Tchaikovsky’s rarely performed “The Maid of Orléans” will kick off the season of Gil Rose’s adventurous Odyssey Opera company — one of four operas centered on the life of Joan of Arc that the company will present. Saturday night’s performance at Jordan Hall is a one-time thing (sung in Russian with English subtitles), and it’s nearly sold out. I would attempt some variety of Joan of Arc joke here, but whoever did the headline for Zoe Madonna’s visit with Rose beat me to it. Well done. Find tickets here. 

GET OUT! Forgot to mention: It’s beauteous out there. So, two things, all outdoorsy-like: There’s the Let’s Dance Boston! bash at Dewey Square Park featuring all kinds of different dance styles — swing! mambo! disco! salsa! klezmer! (?) — and presumably lots of exclamation points, plus live music, dance lessons, primo people watching, and more (which is just me referring to food being available in the general area). This thing is happening through Sunday. More info here. Meanwhile, WINTER IS COMING. That is, on Sunday, the 14th annual Revels Riversing will find a feisty procession from Winthrop Park in Harvard Square down to the riverbank, where the incoming chill will be greeted by dozens of voices united in song — plus the fumes of about 200 pumpkin spice lattes. Festivities start at 5 p.m. Full info here.

OR STAY IN! Maybe — just maybe — this weekend isn’t your thing. Maybe this weekend just isn’t that into you? No judgment here; it happens. The important thing is that you both move on. And on that front, you could spend the whole weekend wending your way through the Globe’s sumptuous Fall Arts Preview, eyeing all sorts of upcoming eligible weekend offerings in dance, music, art, theater, books, and more — just waiting to satisfy you in ways this weekend simply could not. You could also just watch TV.

There’s the new Ken Burns slow-panning epic, “The Vietnam War” — a 10-part, 18-hour attempt to, as James Sullivan puts it, “reconfigure the story of a war that made millions of Americans angry, as often as not at one another.” The Globe’s Mark Feeney calls it “easily the most ambitious Burns documentary, as well as the darkest.” And while this in-depth dig into the quagmire feels long overdue, the timing seems “just right”: “There are uncanny parallels with the present day,” says Sullivan, “beginning with the stark reality of a divided nation that can’t seem to conduct a simple dialogue without shouting. That’s Sunday, 8 p.m. on WGBH 2. 

Meanwhile, you can test the tensile strength of the word depressing by toggling back and forth between Burns and (dramatic pause while I open the envelope) the Emmys! The 69th installment of the awards will interrupt my “Big Brother” flow by airing Sunday at 8 p.m. on CBS. Host Stephen Colbert is the official silver lining of this looming cloud of whatever.

It’s cool. It’s good. I’m good. It’s gonna be a great weekend! The main takeaways this time around: Keep your head up, your expectations in check, your scarf handy, and your flip-flops safely stowed away. And until next week (when, really, I’m gonna have the best events, really terrific events, like you wouldn’t believe, believe me) however you decide to spend your weekend, make it one you’ll miss come Monday. 

See you next week!
