A lesson in baking — and life — from a personal chef

Aram Boghosian for The Boston Globe

Chef Edgar Ievins teaches Jennifer Beaumont Wilfrid (left) and Barbara Targum how to make croissants during a baking class at the Targum home in Arlington.

By Kara Baskin Globe correspondent  September 26, 2017

Pity the husband who tries to honor his wife on a Hallmark holiday: It is one of life’s truly thankless propositions. Opt for jewelry, and you’re sheepish. Opt for a gift certificate, and you’re thoughtless. Opt for a practical household item, and you might as well strangle yourself with the extension cord. Ignore it completely, and you best not come home at all.

And so it was with some curiosity that three neighborhood friends and I opened invitations summoning us to a private session with “Chef Edgar.” It seems our husbands had pooled their resources to purchase an immersive, offbeat Mother’s Day gift — a cooking tutorial with Edgar P. Ievins, a Jamaica Plain-based personal chef. We would learn to bake croissants on a pleasant Sunday in September.

None of us particularly cooks, much less creates scratch pastries.

A moment of explanation: Each of us is married to an alpha chef. We’re basically the anti-1950s housewives. Our husbands come home and make dinner while we happily eat it. They improvise. They whip up gourmet meals on the fly. They regale one another at poker with tales of grilling, braising, and craft cocktails. They out-sauce one another at soccer games. They plot Iron Chef-style competitions on the playground.

And sometimes we load the dishwasher. (In fairness, we also handle other chores. My husband might make a mean chili, but he has also turned my tank tops into cocktail napkins in the wash.)

I was grateful for the gift, but I was also nervous. I’m not a ballerina in the kitchen. I’m not even a lummox. I feel watched. Analyzed. I take my time. And, OK, the fire department was once called to my house during a nacho mishap. My husband hovers when I use the microwave. What would Chef Edgar think?

As it turned out, I had nothing to fear. We arrived at my friend Barbara’s abode greeted by freshly baked croissants stuffed with chocolate and a friendly Chef Edgar, who was hard at work assembling his dough station.

Baking isn’t like cooking, he assured us. There is no improvising. It’s foolproof. Follow an exact recipe with precise temperatures and proportions, and good results await.

Plus, it’s calming.

“People nowadays have these jobs we go to, we knock ourselves out and try to do the best we can, and we rarely get feedback. You go on faith. I go into the kitchen, a little flour, a little water, and I get something in return. It brings life back to a simpler place. I do it when the world gets a little too crazy or when I watch too much Rachel Maddow,” he said.

Relief! A chef with a sense of humor. For some reason I expected a temperamental demigod who would chase us with a rolling pin if we drank too much wine.

Aram Boghosian for The Boston Globe

But Ievins, 67, is broad-shouldered and mild-mannered, a gentle giant in a blue Under Armour headband. He began cooking as a 3-year-old, the youngest child in a large, poor Latvian family in upstate New York. Food wasn’t bought; it was prepared. The earliest dish he remembers making is lemon merengue pie, sweet and tart, fluffy as clouds on the top.

“Cooking was my currency to hang out with adults. I made tons of mistakes, but as long as I was attentive and wanted to participate, I was always welcome. It was a safe place, a fun place where folks were happiest,” he said.

He went on to teach high school math and physics, and studied education and psychology in graduate school. Then he moved to New York City to work as an administrator for teams that financed monster movies. (“Don’t look at IMDb until after this story,” he said with a laugh.) He moved to Boston in 2000 to care for his aging parents who relocated for a stronger Latvian community. He tended to them by day and went to cooking classes at night, working as a teaching assistant for chefs like Tony Maws and Stephanie Sokolove.

“It was my social outlet,” he said. “And as an assistant, I got to spend five hours there instead of three!”

And now he’s a chef for hire, specializing not merely in suburban women. He has worked with teen aspiring chefs at
EXPLO summer enrichment programs, with children and the elderly, and at adult community education centers throughout the area.

“We’re all there for a common cause. I don’t know that there’s any activity, short of cults, where you can get that,” he said.

Although I did feel a bit like a member of the Manson family wielding a gigantic pizza cutter above my friend Barbara’s quartz countertop.

We began the class by slicing pre-prepared dough, readied by Ievins a day in advance. Dough needs time to proof, part of fermenting, he explained.

“You must let time do its job. The bread has to taste as good as the fillings,” he said.

We began by slicing the dough into isosceles triangles. Like wild animals and infants, dough can sense fear. This is no time for timidity. Ievins urged us to slice in one long motion, without hesitation.

“You must demonstrate the courage of your conviction! You have to go for it. If you’re using a pizza wheel and trying to be delicate, you won’t achieve the type of success you would if you were having fun and leaning into it,” he urged.

Ah, a Sheryl Sandberg reference. Maybe things really would be OK.

Aram Boghosian for The Boston Globe

Chef Edgar Ievins oversees (from left) Jennifer Beaumont Wilfrid, the writer, and Heidi Rosenberg making croissants.

Next, it was time to roll. Have you ever rolled croissant dough? It’s awesome because you can’t do it wrong. I expected delicate origami napkins, but this stuff was stretchy like taffy, with plenty of give. You simply place the triangle point-side toward you and roll from top to bottom, like a Chipotle burrito. Place it on a sheet pan lined with parchment, brush with egg wash (even I knew what this was), and then — the best part — you can let it sit. For hours! Because it needs to plump!

Usually I’m so busy shoving pureed apples into my toddler’s mouth and tossing my older son his fruit roll-ups while unloading the dishwasher that any sort of thoughtfulness in the kitchen goes out the window or into my garbage disposal. I’m distracted. So the idea of being able to walk away from a dish for a while — on purpose, without something blowing up — was highly appealing.

Maybe it was the wine or maybe we just became increasingly comfortable with Chef Edgar, but over time, we were slicing and rolling like we’d just escaped from a Parisian boulangerie. We’re educated, smart, driven women, but it felt liberating to bake, away from the watchful eyes of our families, too, I think, and for the pure joy of it — without the express purpose of feeding a whiny child. (It didn’t hurt that Edgar made us sweet potato soup and crab cakes while we waited, either.)

Plus, we got to beat that dough to a pulp. Since Edgar had pre-made most of the dough for us — “a bit of TV magic,” he said — he also wanted us to experience the thrill of handling a sample in its original condition, which involves a degree of violence.

Each of us took turns thumping dough into oblivion with Edgar’s favorite type of rolling pin, the French kind, without handles.

“Handles are too blunt. You can distend the shape without knowing it,” he explained. “The entire country of France uses them, so they can’t be wrong.”

Who needs spinning or
Pilates? The dough was assaulted, splayed like putty atop the counter, then folded like a business letter in thirds (for layering), then thumped again, then folded, thumped, and folded. It felt good. It hurt.

There was a meditative pattern, elemental and repetitive, to pounding it, cutting it, brushing it with egg wash, shaping it into a lopsided and pudgy little roll, tucking in a wedge of ham or Gruyere, and knowing that the oven would warm off the imperfections. I work hard every day. I spend my time in traffic worrying about the bigger picture: Am I doing enough in my career? Am I a good enough mother? Am I staying in close enough touch with my friends? Why does my Fitbit tell me that I’m only getting six hours of sleep per night? (Do I look like I’m only getting six hours of sleep per night?) But at Barbara’s countertop, as the wine bottles were drained and lunchtime waned into afternoon, nothing else mattered for a few hours.

We got croissants and dough to take home, and I’ll be honest: It’s still in my freezer. I ate the leftovers the next day, and my stomach hurt a little bit from all the butter. I’m not going to eat like Julia Child every morning, even if the croissants really do taste almost as good as new after a 15-second zap in the microwave. And I don’t really fancy myself a baker, either.

But I liked Edgar’s style. He gave us a packet of notes as class was ending, with instructions to call if we ran into a baking crisis at home.

Yeah, right, I thought. I’ll probably light my kitchen on fire.

Then I stopped to read what he’d written.

“Remember that until you open a professional boulangerie, your goal is to make homemade croissants. Provided that they are delicious, the differences in sizes and shapes from one pastry to the next simply add character,” his instructions said. “Remember, in order to bake bread, you must bake bread.”

I haven’t preheated my oven just yet, but I’ve read those lines a few times.
