Cambridge biotech Agios wins OK for its leukemia drug

By Jonathan Saltzman Globe Staff  August 01, 2017

A drug hatched in the lab of a Massachusetts biotech won approval Tuesday from the Food and Drug Administration as a treatment for a devastating form of leukemia caused by a genetic mutation.

Idhifa is the first drug developed by Agios Pharmaceuticals Inc., a nine-year-old Cambridge start-up that has focused on medicines to stimulate the human body’s immune response against cancer cells. Agios sold the rights to the medicine in 2010 to New Jersey-based Celgene Corp., which will market it and pay Agios royalties.

The medicine also is the first FDA-approved drug for acute myeloid leukemia (AML) in adult patients with a particular genetic mutation who suffer relapses despite other treatments. Those patients account for 9 to 13 percent of the more than 21,000 people diagnosed each year with the disease.

“It’s a very exciting day for patients,” said Agios chief executive David Schenkein a hematologist and oncologist, after the FDA announced its approval. “Acute myeloid leukemia essentially hasn’t had a new treatment in the last 30 to 40 years.’’

But like other breakthrough medicines, Idhifa won’t come cheap: it’s priced at nearly $300,000 a year.

The rapidly-progressing form of cancer starts in the bone marrow but in most cases quickly moves into the blood. It sometimes spreads to other parts of the body, including the lymph nodes, liver, spleen, and central nervous system.

More than 10,500 people will die from the disease this year, according to the National Cancer Institute at the National Institutes of Health. It generally afflicts older people and is uncommon before the age of 45, according to the American Cancer Society. The five-year survival rate is less than 20 percent.

Advertisement

For decades, intravenous chemotherapy has been the standard treatment even though it kills good and bad cells, Schenkein said. In contrast, Idhifa comes as a pill and is a targeted therapy that works by blocking several enzymes that promote cell growth.

In clinical trials, the drug “was associated with a complete remission in some patients and a reduction in the need for both red cell and platelet transfusions,’’ said Dr. Richard Pazdur, director of the FDA’s Oncology Center of Excellence.

Idhifa is to be used along with a diagnostic test, developed by Illinois-based Abbott Laboratories, that is designed to detect the mutation.

Dr. Gwen Nichols, chief medical officer at the Leukemia & Lymphoma Society, was pleased by the FDA’s approval of Idhifa but noted that it’s not a cure-all. “I don’t want people to think that this is the answer for all AML patients,” she said. There are at least a dozen strains of acute myeloid leukemia, Nichols said, but she’s hopeful that similar precision drugs will be developed to treat them. “This is the tip of the iceberg,” she said.

Indeed, Agios has a second drug in the pipeline to treat acute myeloid leukemia caused by another genetic mutation, according to company officials. The experimental treatment has undergone a set of clinical trials, and Agios hopes to apply for FDA approval by the end of this year. The company has retained worldwide rights to sell the medicine.

As for Idhifa, Schenkein said it took less than four years to develop, test, and clear government hurdles — an unusually swift process.

Agios has 350 employees and expects to add as many as 100 by the end of the year, according to a company spokeswoman.

The FDA had granted Idhifa “priority review” designation, enabling the agency to speed its drug approval process. The medicine had also received “orphan drug” designation, which gives companies incentives to develop treatments for rare diseases.

The drug’s approval was expected, but Agios’s share price still rose on Tuesday — up nearly 5 percent on the Nasdaq exchange, closing at $58.65.
