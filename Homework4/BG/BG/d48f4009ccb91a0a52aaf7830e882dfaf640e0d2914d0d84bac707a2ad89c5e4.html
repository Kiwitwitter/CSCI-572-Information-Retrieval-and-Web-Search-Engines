BOB HOHLER

National anthem protests in Boston sports: risky and rare

By Bob Hohler Globe Staff  August 27, 2017

In Cleveland, nearly a dozen members of the Browns football team knelt in protest last week during the national anthem. A white player said he joined his African-American teammates in the name of racial equality.

Demonstrations aimed at promoting racial unity unfolded also before NFL games in Oakland, Seattle, and Philadelphia, where former Patriot Chris Long, who is white, wrapped his arm around a black teammate who was raising his fist during “The Star-Spangled Banner.’’

The demonstrations were part of a pattern: Athletes throughout professional and amateur sports are increasingly engaging in social activism to the sound and spirit of America’s song.

But the practice has yet to strike a major chord in Boston. In the year since then-San Francisco quarterback Colin Kaepernick inspired a wave of anthem protests over what he perceived as the mistreatment of minorities, New England’s professional athletes generally have hewed to tradition, standing respectfully during the anthem, while some of their peers across the country have mobilized to press for social change.

If there is a will among Boston’s sports stars to embrace the activist spirit the city has exemplified from the American Revolution through the abolition, civil rights, and anti-Vietnam War movements, they have yet to find a way of expressing it during the anthem.

Instead of passing judgment on his actions during the national anthem, examine how we ourselves act during the anthem.

One reason: Taking a stand — or a knee — can be risky in professional sports, as baseball trailblazers such as Jackie Robinson, Curt Flood, and Tommy Harper discovered long ago. Harper, a former Red Sox outfielder, coach, and front-office staffer, twice lost his job under the franchise’s Yawkey regime after he spoke out about racial intolerance.

Like Kaepernick today, Harper found himself unable to find work in his sport — for two years, in his case — after speaking up.

“Athletes can have social consciences, but a lot of teams and fans want those athletes to leave their consciences at home,’’ said Harper, who in 1985 blew the whistle on the Sox maintaining a long, friendly relationship with an Elks Club near their spring training headquarters that refused to serve African-Americans.

There have been flashes of activism among Boston athletes since Kaepernick’s first protest. The Celtics, before their 2016 preseason opener against the 76ers in Amherst, joined their coaches in crossing their arms and holding hands during “The Star-Spangled Banner.’’ Earlier, before the Patriots kicked off their 2016 regular-season opener in Arizona, defensive back Devin McCourty joined then-teammate Martellus Bennett in raising their fists after the anthem.

Otherwise, the campaign for social change among professional athletes has largely missed the city where Celtics great Bill Russell 60 years ago established himself as a champion of human rights en route to receiving the Presidential Medal of Freedom.

At Fenway Park, several racially charged developments this summer all but invited the type of activism Russell practiced generations earlier. Yet Sox players have given little voice to concerns about events that commanded national headlines.

Many players, violating club rules, have typically been absent this summer when the team has gathered on the field for the anthem at Fenway. But their absences have generally been excused by management because the players are involved in other pregame routines.

Anthem protests seem far from their minds.

“I personally would not do it,’’ said pitcher Matt Barnes, who is white. “We talk about a lot of things in the clubhouse. We’re together a lot. Nobody has said anything about protesting the anthem.’’

Barry Chin/Globe Staff

The entire Boston Red Sox dugout came out for the playing of the National Anthem.

Manager John Farrell said he has never discouraged the Sox from discussing politics. He described the anthem as a significant tradition.

“People are in tune with what is going on in the country and the stances some individuals have taken,’’ Farrell said. “But from a personal standpoint, you respect those who made the ultimate sacrifice for our country, and it’s important to acknowledge that.’’

When Baltimore outfielder Adam Jones alleged he was the target of a racial slur at Fenway on May 1 and a spectator acknowledged a night later that he uttered a racial epithet to demean a black student’s performance of the anthem, team executives responded forcefully. But few players spoke up, and those who did seemed reluctant.

Center fielder Jackie Bradley Jr., who is black, described the Jones incident as “disheartening’’ and “hurtful,’’ but also expressed a desire to keep his thoughts to himself — a common sentiment in the Sox clubhouse.

“I’ve got a lot on my mind about that,’’ Bradley told reporters. “But I feel like less is going to be more in the current situation just because I want the focus to be on my team, and I don’t want any outside distractions for me, my family.”

So it was that Sox players had almost nothing to say when principal owner John W. Henry called on Aug. 17 for changing the name of Yawkey Way because of the franchise’s racial intolerance during Thomas A. Yawkey’s long reign as owner.

David Price, who is biracial and the team’s highest-paid player, told the Globe before the season that he had been the target of racist taunts last year in the Fenway bullpen. But Price, like many teammates, has been all but silent about the racially volatile events that have captured the nation’s attention from Fenway Park to Charlottesville, Va., to Boston Common.

One teammate who stood up was Mookie Betts. The day after the Jones incident, Betts tweeted, “Fact: I’m Black too. Literally stand up for [Jones] tonight and say no to racism. We as @RedSox and @MLB fans are better than this.’’

Social media gives athletes greater control over their messages, but it does not eliminate the possibility of the messages creating distractions, which are widely considered taboo in team sports. Many managers and coaches abhor distractions, none perhaps more than Bill Belichick of the Patriots.

Belichick never publicly commented on McCourty and Bennett raising their fists to open the 2016 season, but no Patriot has since demonstrated during an anthem.

In training camp this summer, Belichick has shown no interest in addressing social or political affairs, despite his own involvement. The coach has joined Patriots owner Robert Kraft and quarterback Tom Brady in supporting Donald J. Trump, even as many Patriot players have been so opposed to Trump that they boycotted a White House ceremony in April honoring the team’s latest Super Bowl victory.

Belichick has declined to say if he has spoken to the Patriots about protesting during the anthem. But there has been no demonstration of any kind by a player since camp began, and McCourty has equivocated about whether he has anything planned.

“I’ve thought about it and how to get involved and how to help, but we’ll see,” he told reporters.

By contrast, his twin, Jason McCourty, a Browns cornerback, wasted no time joining Long, former Patriot Jamie Collins, and other teammates in kneeling during the anthem before an exhibition game against the Giants.

Jason McCourty is among scores of athletes who have spoken out since Kaepernick’s original protest.

“Guys are trying to show people that we want to stand up for this country and show that no matter what your color is, no matter what your background is, whatever, we can all come together and work together to make it a better place,’’ McCourty said after the game.

Still, anthem protests are provocative acts. They can enflame passions, as have some of the most memorable political expressions by American sports figures: Muhammad Ali refusing to report for his Army induction during the Vietnam War; sprinters Tommie Smith and John Carlos raising their fists in a black power salute at the 1968 Olympics; baseball slugger Carlos Delgado, a native Puerto Rican, refusing to stand for “God Bless America’’ in 2004 to protest the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan.

Critics have decried anthem protests as disrespectful and un-American. An untold number of spectators have complained that games are being sullied by political conflicts, and some team owners have worried about damage to their brands and bottom lines.

A recent JD Power survey examined a decline in NFL viewership last season and found that only 12 percent of respondents watched fewer games than prior years. Of those who watched fewer games, 26 percent blamed the anthem protests. However, that 26 percent represented only 3 percent of the national survey’s total respondents.

NBA television ratings also dipped last season, though social activism by players has not been tied to the drop.

The Celtics, for their part, have generally embraced the franchise’s history of pioneering racial equality — a legacy that dates to 1950, when a white rookie named Bob Cousy helped Chuck Cooper, the first American-American drafted by an NBA team, make his way at a time when blacks were not welcome at hotels and restaurants in some cities the Celtics visited.

Last year, the Celtics followed Kaepernick’s lead not only by holding hands in the preseason game but by releasing a video in which they appealed for social unity. It remains to be seen how their revamped roster proceeds next season.

The team’s former star, Isaiah Thomas, said before he was traded this past week to Cleveland in a package for Kyrie Irving, that he would not protest this season during the anthem. Irving has not disclosed his intentions, although he joined LeBron James and other NBA players in 2014 in donning T-shirts that said “I Can’t Breathe’’ to protest the death of Eric Garner during an encounter with New York police.

A new NFL season begins Sept. 7, when the Patriots host the Chiefs in Foxborough. The Chiefs, like Devin McCourty and Bennett, made a statement last season before their opening game by linking arms during the anthem while cornerback Marcus Peters raised a fist.

NBC will broadcast the Sept. 7 game at Gillette Stadium and has said it will televise the anthem. The nation will be watching.
