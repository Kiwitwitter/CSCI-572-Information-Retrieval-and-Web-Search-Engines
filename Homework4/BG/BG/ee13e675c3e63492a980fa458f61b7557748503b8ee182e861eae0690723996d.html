Evan Rachel Wood talks sexuality at Forbes conference

Benjamin Esakof for Forbes

Evan Rachel Wood at the Forbes Under 30 Summit Monday.

By Mark Shanahan Globe Staff  October 02, 2017

Evan Rachel Wood has accomplished a lot in 30 years, appearing in TV shows like “Westworld” and “True Blood,” the HBO miniseries “Mildred Pierce,” and a lot of movies, including “Thirteen” (for which she earned a Golden Globe nomination), “Running With Scissors,” and “The Wrestler.”

But Wood says it’s a speech she gave earlier this year to the Human Rights Campaign that is her proudest achievement.

Speaking at the Forbes Under 30 Summit Monday, Wood said the speech, delivered at an HRC gala in North Carolina last February, was so important because it was so liberating.

“I felt silenced and shamed for a really long time and I just decided to break that chain and share my story,” said Wood, who revealed her bisexuality in the speech. “I think that was my most proud moment. I really put it all out there.”

Wood was part of a panel Monday that focused on “gender and sexual fluidity,” and how pop culture deals with those issues. She was joined on the Faneuil Hall stage by “Billions” actor Asia Kate Dillon, TV/fashion personality E.J. Johnson, and YouTube star Tyler Oakley.

In addition to her sexuality, Wood had also revealed that she’s a sexual assault survivor. It wasn’t easy to talk about, but she’s glad she did, the actress said Monday.

“I receive such amazing letters and stories from young kids who are struggling with their identity who say, ‘Oh thank you for giving me a space to feel like it’s OK to be myself.’ ” Wood said. “I realized [after] coming out about my own sexual assault that they’re everywhere, we’re everywhere, survivors are everywhere. There are survivors in the room right now, and it’s something we talk about like it’s somewhere else.”

But addressing such hot-button issues publicly can have consequences. Dillon said she loses followers on social media every time she tweets about #BlackLivesMatter, but that doesn’t stop her from tweeting about it or other issues she cares about.

“You will lose followers and you will lose business opportunities and there will be people who don’t want to work with you anymore,” Dillon said. “And that’s fine because you didn’t want to work with them either.”

In big and small ways, Wood said, she uses the platform she has as a celebrity to get people thinking.

“Last year, I made a promise to myself to only wear suits to awards shows. That might seem like a very trivial thing, but it caused waves. A woman wearing pants is still a big deal, which means we have to keep doing it,” she said. “I’m not protesting dresses and saying you can only wear suits. I’m saying, ‘Let’s give people options.’ There’s not one heteronormative way to be all the time.”
