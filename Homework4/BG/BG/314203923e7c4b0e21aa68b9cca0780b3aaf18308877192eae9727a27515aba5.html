When will they ‘get there’?

By Meredith Goldstein Globe Staff   July 13, 2017

Have a question for Meredith? Submit here.

Q. I recently graduated from college and have been in a relationship for more than two years. It’s now long-distance because we’re both from different states. We’ve always had the school year to fall back on since summers are busy, but now real life is setting in as it does.

Advertisement

He’s trying to get a position across the country and plans to do a lot of traveling. I’m just saving money at home and taking whatever work I can get. I don’t have any specific goals yet. It just gives me the time to overthink things.

There’s been no real talks of the future and what we have in mind. The basic gist of things is that “we’ll get there when we get there” because we don’t really know what’s going to happen or where we’ll be. I’m just wondering if I’m overthinking or if I should ask what we’re trying to do here and what he sees for us in the future.

A. The big question you should ask right now is whether you should attempt to be an exclusive couple while you’re in two different places. Long-distance relationships can work best when there’s some sort of light at the end of the tunnel. You guys have no light — no plan to be together — so it would be nice to have some freedom instead.

You probably don’t want to lobby for a breakup, but this is an excellent time to wake up every morning and think only of yourself. Without a faraway boyfriend, you can figure out what you want to do when you’ve saved enough money to leave home. You can make plans for your own travels. You can come up with some great goals.

Advertisement

The thing to remember is that he’s been talking about a possible future for the two of you, but he hasn’t said anything about the present. You have to prioritize the now as you decide what to do next.

Meredith

READERS RESPOND:

This is called breaking up by default. Not only is he moving across the country without you, once he gets there, he plans to travel a lot. There is no you in that equation. Stop waiting for him to include you in his plans. Make your own plans — work, grad school, whatever, and accept that it is over. You can continue to communicate as you have been, but you are just delaying the inevitable. HEYITHINK

1) Get a job 2) Start adulting 3) Share your hopes and desires with your boyfriend but don’t hinge your future on “what he sees for us.” If your relationship stands the test of time and if you can find viable careers/opportunities in the same place then it may work out (but those are a lot of variables). NOMORESCREENNAMES

My wife and I went to different colleges but we spent the summers together. I followed her to Boston where she went to graduate school. I found work in Boston and we’ve lived here ever since. Living together helped us to decide that we wanted to stay together. I recommend living with him and traveling with him. This will help you to decide if you want to stay together. You don’t have an established career so there’s no reason not to hit the road. You don’t have to spend a lot of money to travel.
ADAM

He’s OK with a loose, undetermined future. You are not. It’s almost impossible to reconcile these two personalities. That’s what’s called, in real life (as you call it), a mismatch. Time to see what else is out there in boyfriend land. MCDIMMERSON

I have a friend who put off buying a house because she wanted to wait until she found a partner, even though housing prices were good where she lived and mortgages were cheaper than rent. She finally bit the bullet. A year later she met the man who became her husband who also had his own house. And guess what, they figured out how to figure it out. The point ... jobs can be left, houses can be sold, vacations can be changed. Choosing to live your life now will not prevent you from living a different version of it in the future, but putting off living your life can never be reversed.

GRETCHYNN

Your parents must be thrilled.

CRUCIFIEDZEOFF
