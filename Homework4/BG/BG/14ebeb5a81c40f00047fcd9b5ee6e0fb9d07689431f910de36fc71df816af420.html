The Republican party is trying really, really hard to just ignore Trump

By Annie Linskey Globe Staff  August 26, 2017

NASHVILLE — Republican Party leaders assembling for their summer meeting found themselves in a bit of a bind.

In public they swooned over President Trump, especially his pull among his base and his fund-raising power. In private, some fretted over his unwillingness to reach beyond core supporters, and his constantly divisive tactics remain unsettling to party elders who don’t know exactly what to make of him — still.

“As you change the rules of politics, which Trump has been doing, people get nervous,” explained James Bopp, a leading conservative lawyer and Trump supporter who attended the meeting. “They get insecure. They’re puzzled. They’re worried. The familiar is now changing. How is this going to play out?”

Trump gave party members plenty to muse over during the four-day Republican National Committee meeting. They distanced themselves from his hints to challenge incumbent senators facing reelection, threats to force a government shutdown over building The Wall, and his pardon of Joe Arpaio, the controversial former Arizona sheriff, which one member called “tone-deaf.”

And over the week, there were other signs of Republican leaders charting a separate path from Trump.

Ignoring his controversial, seesawing statements on white supremacy, the RNC unanimously approved a clear-cut resolution condemning racist beliefs. Separately, House Speaker Paul Ryan parried Trump’s threat of a government shutdown. “I don’t think most people want to see a government shutdown, ourselves included,” Ryan said at a news conference in Oregon.

Intraparty tensions fueled by Trump also erupted on two fronts. The president on Twitter attacked another party elder, Senator Bob Corker of Tennessee, for questioning whether Trump can succeed.

“I don’t read his tweets,” said Richard George, a Republican National Committee member from Wyoming, trying to downplay the president’s barbs. “I just feel like he can say whatever he wants.”

The late Friday pardon of Arpaio, the former sheriff who led a crackdown on undocumented immigrants, deepened tensions with Arizona’s two Republican senators — one of whom is up for reelection in 2018. Arpaio was convicted of ignoring a court order to stop detaining people whom he merely suspected were in the country illegally, and his pardon ignited protests from the Hispanic community.

“Symbolically it’s a bad idea,” said one RNC member who requested anonymity to avoid angering the president. The member said the move was “tone deaf” and “probably not the smartest political idea in this climate.”

Courtney Pedroza/The New York Times

Former Maricopa County Sheriff Joe Arpaio.

Senators Jeff Flake and John McCain each denounced the pardon, with McCain saying Trump’s move “undermines his claim for the respect of rule of law.”

The Arizona senators are already on edge after Trump, in Phoenix for a rally earlier in the week, privately met with a group of candidates considering challenging Flake in next year’s primary. The meeting, first reported by Politico, prompted worry that a sitting president would undermine GOP senators up for election.

It’s a difficult concept for party elites — once they’ve got a seat ticked off in their column, they typically don’t like to do anything to disrupt that.

RNC members pledged they will stay out of the fray — and scratched their heads at Trump’s moves.

“I’m not as sure of what I think than I used to be,” admitted John Ryder, former general counsel to the Republican National Committee, when asked about Trump’s intraparty bickering. “We’re still in unchartered waters in all of this. This is all new territory. We have not had a president who is as unconventional as Donald Trump.”

Still, Trump’s popularity with rank-and-file Republicans is helping them raise gobs of money; committee members frequently cited $86.5 million in donations so far. And he gave his base more reasons to be excited. On Friday, he followed through on his pledge to ban transgender service members with a memo that banned the military from accepting new transgender members and directing the Department of Defense to determine the fate of those already serving.

Bopp, who has ties with Trump’s top aides and is an adamant supporter, said Trump can be successful by just keeping his core supporters happy and energized, even if he can’t pass legislation in a Congress controlled by Republicans.

That’s a sea change from the old adage that winning elections is about increasing a candidate’s appeal, or as political junkies put it: Addition not subtraction.

“Trump can both survive and prosper if he keeps his base,” said Bopp. “They understand that Trump is operating in the swamp. And they are fighting him tooth and nail. Whether he can accomplish these things are not nearly as important as him fighting for these things.”

The view wasn’t unanimous. As the 168 Republican committee members who run the national party mingled in the hallways at the sprawling Gaylord Opryland Resort & Convention Center, many predicted Trump will have to rack up legislative wins to be seen as an effective president.

Andrew Harnik/Associated Press

President Donald Trump wave as he walked across the South Lawn of the White House last Tuesday.

“I see him maturing in the office and maybe continuing to mature,” offered Doyle Webb, the chairman of the Arkansas Republican Party.

“And getting more focused on specific areas rather than focusing on many areas at one time. I believe people will become more supportive as time goes on.”

Party leaders did try to steer the conversations in Nashville to areas of agreement, such as the economy. One of their featured speakers was Commerce Secretary Wilbur Ross, an envoy to the business community, which has been abandoning Trump in droves over his incoherent response to the racial violence in Charlottesville.

Ross’s comments, closed to reporters, focused on regulations the Trump administration is erasing and positive economic figures, according to several who attended.

And on Thursday night they heard from Eric Trump, the president’s son, who complimented the RNC on its network of activists who helped turn out the vote for his father, according to one member who was there.

They also soaked in a little campaign nostalgia, when the GOP had a political figure that everyone in the party could agree about: Hillary Clinton.

During a general session, RNC Chairwoman Ronna McDaniel played a tape of Clinton’s interview at a tech conference this year where the Democratic presidential nominee ripped into the Democratic National Committee for providing “nothing” to her campaign.

“It was bankrupt. It was on the verge of insolvency. Its data was mediocre to poor, nonexistent, wrong,” Clinton said.

The roomful of Republicans leaders in the audience cheered.
