She’d like a label, please

By Meredith Goldstein Globe Staff   July 26, 2017

Have a question for Meredith? Submit here.

Q. I’m a college student in Boston, currently in Los Angeles for a summer internship. I’ve been here for more than a month and was fortunate enough to meet a great guy. Last night, we discussed our relationship, and he told me he is not interested in seeing anyone else. However, he’s not comfortable with the terms “boyfriend/girlfriend” for two reasons: 1. I am leaving at the end of the summer, and he doesn’t like the idea of just being a “two-month boyfriend,” and 2. he is a recovering alcoholic and is very much focused on his health and well-being right now.

Advertisement

In the past, I’ve always lived by the rules that I am either casually dating someone or I am their girlfriend. I am glad he is being honest with me about what he needs, but I can’t help but feel that a guy who is truly interested would have no problem being my boyfriend, despite life’s inconveniences. I tend to live by the self-help book/dating guide “The Rules,” if you can’t tell.

Why do I want the label? Well, I care for him a great deal, and I’d like to be able to have a concise way to state that. We have a solid relationship, and “so-and-so, a guy I’m dating exclusively” sounds unnecessarily complex to me. Why not just call it what it is?

Also, maybe part of it is that I don’t think our relationship needs to end when I leave. I fully intend to be back in Los Angeles next May after graduation, and I’ve done long-distance for longer periods of time before. We haven’t discussed this as a possibility.

I should be ecstatic about our conversation about exclusivity, but instead, I am hung up on the label. What do you think?

Label-less in Los Angeles

Advertisement

A. Please do not live by the book “The Rules.” Please. The happiest, most genuine couples I know broke every one of those stupid self-help dating rules.

The person who screams “I’m your boyfriend!” from the top of the Hollywood sign two months into a summer relationship is not being real. You can’t expect him to have all the answers right now.

Your issue isn’t the label. I will not accept that you’re freaking out about how to explain this man to other people. (It’s pretty easy to introduce him by name.)

The real problem is at the end of your letter — the fact that you want to continue this relationship after you leave, and he might not. He’s already told you he needs to concentrate on himself for a very good reason. He might not be able to give you any guarantees.

Start preparing yourself to return to Boston without a big answer, and consider that leaving things open-ended — without a label — might be the best option for you, too. You’ve only known this guy for the summer. It’s way too early to be sure you want more.

Meredith

READERS RESPOND:

He has clearly stated to you that the relationship has an expiration date (whenever you leave his city and return to yours), he is not interested in long-distance after said date, and he doesn’t have the emotional capacity to put a lot of energy into your eight-week relationship even though he is not pursuing others. What else do you want? Concentrate on your internship instead of labeling some summer fling with a guy who’s doing his best to be honest and work on his own issues. URKIDDINME

“and I’ve done long-distance for longer periods of time before” . . . Apparently not successfully. LUCILLEVANPELT

I work in the recovery field, and if an alcoholic or an addict tells you that they need some space to take care of themselves or their sobriety, PLEASE LISTEN! Do not assume that your need to label this “relationship” is the most important thing here. Also, do people still read this “Rules” book!? I thought that got thrown out the window a long time ago. ONESMARTCOOKIE

If you continue to live by “The Rules,” exclusivity and labels will be the least of your problems. Hate to break it to you but this is a summer romance. You’re in LA for an internship, he’s trying to get his life together. Have fun with him, be supportive as he works through sobriety, and find another book to read. PATSFAN79

I think it’s ridiculous to try to have a long-distance relationship of that distance for a school year when you probably wouldn’t even be able to see each other until winter break, if then. It doesn’t mean that you can’t keep in touch, but relax. What if you can’t get a job in LA after graduation? It really puts too much pressure on both of you and pressure is the last thing this guy needs now. And adults don’t need “The Rules.” If you meet someone you are truly meant to be with, you don’t need to play games — things will just happen naturally without having to worry about arbitrary rules. LEGALLYLIZ

He sounds very mature and grounded. Respect what he’s saying and realize that in dating you can’t change what the other person wants or is ready for. It’s their life and they have to do what works for them. BKLYNMOM

Enjoy your time together, letter writer, and come home for your final year of school. Maybe when you go back to the West Coast after graduation, you’ll reconnect. You both have other priorities right now. ELLLEEM

Just call him the guy you are dating . . . because that is what he is. He can be your boyfriend later when he commits to a transcontinental phone call relationship. CAPECODDA

There are better ways to treat yourself and others with respect and intelligence than that dang book. JIVEDIVA
