More than 2,000 people simulate brain surgery at the same time

Rick Unis/The Congress of Neurological Surgeons

More than 2,000 neurosurgeons set a world record Monday by simultaneously navigating a neurological procedure within the brain.

By Andy Rosen Globe Staff  October 09, 2017

In a darkened room at the Boston Convention and Exhibition Center, rows of neurosurgeons raised their phones to inches from their faces and peered through specialized lenses to see a simulation of a brain suffering a cerebral hemorrhage.

Though many of the 2,057 people who participated in the exercise had seen the condition before, few had seen it like this — and none had shared such an experience with so many of their colleagues. Organizers of the event said it was the largest of any group virtual reality activity on record.

The Congress of Neurological Surgeons, said it had applied to Guinness World Records to certify the surgery demonstration as having the most simultaneous participants in a live VR experience. The standing record, set in March at an automotive simulation in Canada was 1,867.

Dr. Neil Martin, who led the simulation, said it would be a cultural achievement even without breaking the record.

“Certainly, we’ve set a record in getting more than six neurosurgeons to be instructed to do the same thing and actually do it at the same time,” said Martin, who is director of the Geisinger Neuroscience Institute in Danville, Pa.

The demonstration, using a mobile application from Ohio-based Surgical Theater, highlights ways in which neurosurgeons can map out plans to approach procedures.

There’s a reason that “brain surgery” is shorthand for any difficult task. As the simulation shows, any path through the tissue is lined with sensitive structures that could imperil a patient’s safety if damaged.

Brain surgeons have long worked to delicately plot their approaches. People at the conference this week said technologies like virtual reality and augmented reality — which are growing across the medical profession — will make preparation and practice easier.

Many also said such simulations can make it easier for patients to understand the complex procedures they’re about to undergo.

“I think it help to explain appropriately,” said Rupa Juthani a research fellow at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center who watched in the demonstration in VR. “It’s all about informed consent.”

Though spectators saw the simulation through a relatively simple setup in which they attached a set of special goggles to their phones, Dr. Martin used a more advanced setup, including a headset and handpieces.

The images used in preparation are built using the actual patients’ brain images from scans including CT scans and MRIs, and Surgical Theater said research shows its technology can help surgeons figure out their approaches with greater precision.

The company was founded by two former Israeli Air Force officers who sought to givesurgeons access to simulated training akin to the lessons that fighter pilots receive.

Martin, who has run demonstrations of Surgical Theater products in the past, said he expects technology to continue to improve the way neurosurgeons plot their surgeries.

He said he’s particularly excited about the prospect of glasses using augmented reality (think Google Glass for doctors) to overlay information on a surgeon’s real-life view of a brain.

“That will be the real superpower X-ray vision,” he said.
