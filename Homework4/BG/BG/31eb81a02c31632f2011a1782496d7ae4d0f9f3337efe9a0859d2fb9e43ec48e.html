HIGH SCHOOL FOOTBALL

Algonquin football team tackles the future: robots

Courtesy of Dartmouth News

The Mobile Virtual Player — a robotic tackling dummy — was developed by Dartmouth College.

By Nate Weitzer Globe Correspondent  October 04, 2017

NORTHBOURGH — The most valuable player at football practices may not be human at all in the near future.

A robotic tackle dummy, dubbed the Mobile Virtual Player or “MVP” for short, debuted at varsity football practice on Monday at Algonquin Regional High, the first time this cutting edge technology has ever been utilized at the high school level.

Originally developed by Dartmouth College grad students, the 150-pound MVP can travel up to 20 miles per hour, handle significant impact, and move in any direction via remote control.

At an average cost of $8,000 it is not yet widely affordable for high school teams, but this adaptable dummy is extremely valuable due to its ability to simulate live tackling without subjecting players to the dangers of concussions during practice.

“Whenever you can eliminate player-to-player impact that’s something we want to jump on,” said Algonquin first-year head coach Taylor Allen.

Nationwide enrollment in youth football programs has decreased over the past few years and that can in turn effect high school programs, so coaches are hopeful that utilizing this new technology can make football a safer sport to play.

“The more attention concussions get, the more reluctant parents will be to let their kids play [football],” said Algonquin assistant head coach Mark Allen.

Advertisement

“But every year there’s better helmet technology, new tackling techniques and now [the MVP], which can allow you to get the work in that you need without as much contact.”

Incorporated by collegiate and professional football teams over the past year, the MVP came to Algonquin thanks to a Northborough parent, who worked on the development of its’ engine and suggested his daughter’s school as grounds for beta testing.

Right after dismissal on a sunny afternoon, smiling Algonquin players took turns tackling, tagging and evading the dummy while the coaching staff remotely controlled their new MVP.

“It moves pretty fast and is surprisingly shifty so it’s actually tough to tackle,” said Algonquin senior captain running back John Polymeros.

“As an offensive player it’s great to limit the hits I’d be taking in practice and let the defense hit [the MVP] so I can stay fresh for games.”

Despite growing health concerns surrounding the game, football remains the most popular sport in America with approximately 1.1 million athletes currently playing according to data collected by the National Federation of State High Schools Association.

The same studies show that the rate of concussions is in decline and the company behind MVP hopes to support that positive trend.

“This is the first step in the direction to get the robot out into the high school market,” said Daniel Ryan, owner of the DR Marketing firm that represents MVP.

“It’s already popular in the NFL and college ranks and now Algonquin is officially the starting point for high schools, so it’s very exciting to see the MVP in action.”
