An icon for a love that has blossomed over 55 years

Suzanne Kreiter/Globe staff

Doug Coldwell, with his wife, Pat, created a massive heart out of wildflowers on a hill in Berlin, symbolizing his love for her.

By Thomas Farragher Globe Columnist  July 30, 2017

BERLIN, Mass. — The ageless symbol of love sits on a verdant hillside here, a meticulously tended garden fashioned from mustard seed, Shasta daisies, goldenrod, and Queen Anne’s lace, a nature’s heart as if carved by Cupid himself.

The lovely image has misted the eyes of strangers, attracted nervous boyfriends with diamond rings secretly tucked into pockets, and once served as the wedding venue for a poor groom who asked permission to marry his bride here in a broad blanket of green. It was, he said, the only place he could afford.

These seeds were sown long ago.

A lifetime ago, actually, when Doug Coldwell and Pat Condon were just school kids, sharing a high school locker, dancing at their senior prom where Pat was crowned queen, and slowly falling in love.

It was 1962. He had a crew cut, a pocket protector, and a contagious smile.

“He was very, very cute,’’ Pat said. “But he was very, very goofy. That’s how it started.’’

Doug, sitting next to her on their porch here, smiled and sweetly recalled the girl who was then a sophomore at nearby Tahanto Regional High School.

“Patricia was so pretty,’’ Doug said. “I couldn’t believe Boylston had someone that pretty. She just knocked me off my feet. I couldn’t believe she would go out with me if I asked her.’’

But she said yes.

Their first date was at a movie theater in Marlborough, where Doug was heckled by friends in the seats a row behind them as he fumbled to find a suave way to place his arm around his date.

Eventually, on warm summer nights, they found themselves in a parked car above a hillside in this tiny town of apple orchards and rolling hills, a secluded spot that became their lover’s lane.

With breathtaking vistas, that lovely emerald slope sweeps up and away from Route 62 not far from the Wachusett Reservoir and the local lumber store Doug’s grandfather opened a century ago.

And as they sat there — two teenagers headed toward commitment and marriage — they made a promise to each other: If this land ever becomes available, let’s make it our own

One day, they assured each other, this will be ours. One day.

But a war raged half a world away and Doug, in and out of college and trying to find his academic footing, had run out of his chances for a student draft deferral. He found himself instead at Fort Jackson, S.C., for Army basic training.

By then he’d already asked for Pat’s hand in marriage, the first time as they floated on a rubber raft in the middle of Bare Hill Pond in Harvard.

Her answer? Not yet. “We were still so young,’’ Pat explained.

Just months before a military transport plane would carry him to the jungles of Southeast Asia, Doug asked again and on Dec. 21, 1968, Pat Condon became Pat Coldwell.

The groom was late to the church. “It was tense,’’ he said. “I was getting gas for the getaway car.’’

“I was waiting,’’ Pat recalled. “I was so nervous. I would just as soon have eloped. We wanted to be married. We didn’t care.’’

The newlyweds walked out of St. Mary of the Hills Church in Boylston and celebrated with 135 guests. They danced and drank at a packed Sterling Inn. Doug’s uncle had a place on the water in Falmouth heated only by a brick fireplace. The honeymooners could not have cared less.

Doug soon had other things on his mind. The Army had deposited him in Xuan Loc, 38 miles east of Saigon, where his job was to predict what the enemy was going to do and to assess how much firepower the US could muster in an attempt to defeat an advancing guerrilla force.

They wrote each other every day.

Doug would lie on his bunk and, before carefully slicing open the envelope, he’d let the paper rest on his face, drinking in his new wife’s perfume – Estee Lauder Youth-Dew.

“I know it sounds goofy,” Doug said. “But we were both scared. We didn’t know what the next day was going to bring.’’

He kept a homemade cardboard calendar near his bunk, checking off each of 365 squares, a retrograde countdown clock marking the time that separated him from his wife.

With 45 days remaining in his tour of duty, Doug and Pat met in Hawaii for a reunion so memorable that it still prompts mutual blushes. “We spent a lot of time in the room,’’ Doug said, smiling slyly. “One member of the wait staff would come down and bring us sandwiches.’’

The young soldier had lost so much weight, his still-gleaming wedding band slipped off his finger, forever lost in the pounding Hawaiian surf.

“Boy,’’ said Pat. “That was hard to leave each other.’’

In the years that would follow, Doug and Pat Coldwell created a family — a son and a daughter — and tended to the family hardware and lumber business before it was sold to a local chain.

There have been momentous mileposts — anniversaries, weddings, the births of four grandchildren.

There have been life-changing challenges. Nineteen years ago, Doug was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease, an affliction he controls with medication.

Pat suffered a stroke just five weeks ago, which has left her with weakness in her left hand.

Across the years, they’ve never forgotten those moments on that secluded hillside, like the time the beam from a police officer’s flashlight interrupted their dashboard romance alone at night overlooking Route 62.

And they never forgot that pledge they’d made to each other way back when: If this land ever becomes available, let’s make it our own.

So when that 16-acre plot of land came up for sale a dozen years ago, they quickly scooped it up.

And then Doug had an idea.

What about a heart on that hill?

“I made it 500 feet around,’’ he said. “I wanted something to represent my total commitment and my feelings for her.’’

Pat sometimes rode the tractor with Doug as he carefully shaped their field.

People instantly noticed.

Messages, testifying to the hillside’s emotional payload, have been left in their mailbox. Poems are penned. Testimonials to long-lost loves are chronicled. Some 20 proposals of marriage from men on one knee have taken place here.

Pat’s therapist grew emotional during a recent post-stroke therapy session. “She said, ‘Every time I go by that heart, I get knotted up,’ ‘’ Pat recalled.

A local guy told Doug he had a bone to pick with him. His message? “You have just ruined it for every man in town. You’ve raised the bar too high, Doug!’’

As Doug and Pat, each now 70 years old, sat on rocking chairs the other day on the porch of the home they’ve built above that sloping field with a big heart — blossoming anew after a mid-summer’s rainstorm — they smiled and patted each other’s arms, recall-ing this half-century-old love story.

Then they retired to the field at the foot of their hill and settled into a pair of Adirondack chairs, where, on some splendid summer evenings they sip cool drinks and remember where the seeds of it all came from — the time they began a love affair that’s never withered.

There was a scent of flowers in the air. And some discreetly applied Youth-Dew.
