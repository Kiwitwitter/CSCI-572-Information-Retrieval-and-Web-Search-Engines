Chess notes

By Chris Chase   October 08, 2017

Alexei Shirov is 45 and well past his prime. He can still play entertaining chess but just not with the same consistency as before. He was once No. 4 in the world and was going to play Garry Kasparov for the world championship but sadly that match fell through. He has long been considered a tactical genius, very much in the image of Latvia’s famous son Mikhail Tal. His book of games was aptly titled “Fire on Board” He has come back home to Latvia, the prodigal son returning to perhaps serve as a role model for young players.

Alexandra Kosteniuk is a former women’s world champion (2008-10) who still pursues an active tournament schedule but seems to self-destruct in the women’s championship cycles, as witnessed by her implosion vs. Anna Muzychuk in the 2017 cycle.

Today’s game illustrates Shirov’s attacking abilities when he converted a seemingly dull position into a raging assault against White’s king.

2017 Chess.com Isle of Man Open – Douglas, Isle of Man

Alexandra Kosteniuk (2552) — Alexei Shirov (2630)

1.e4 e5 2.Nf3 Nc6 3.Bb5 a6 4.Ba4 Nf6 5.0–0 b5 6.Bb3 Bc5 A rather popular way for the Black to play this line these days. It’s certainly more aggressive than putting the bishop on e7 7.a4 Rb8 8.c3 We saw 8.Nxe5 in the game Sutovsky v Yoo last week. 8...d6 9.d4 Bb6 10.a5 Ba7 10...Nxa5 is interesting when after 11.Rxa5 Bxa5 12.dxe5 Ng4 13.Bg5 f6 14.exf6 gxf6 15.Bf4 Qe7 16.Nd4 Bd7 it’s unclear; Not 10...Bxa5 11.d5 wins 11.h3 0–0 12.Be3 Ra8 13.Nbd2 Bb7 14.Re1 Re8 All theory to this point 15.Ng5 Re7 16.Nf1!? 16.Ndf3 strikes me as better when after 16…exd4 17.Nxf7 Rxf7 18.cxd4 Nxe4 19.Bxf7+ Kxf7 20.d5 Bxe3 21.Rxe3 wins material 16...h6 17.d5 Pretty much forced as retreating the knight just loses the e-pawn 17...hxg5 18.dxc6 Bxc6 19.Bxg5 Nxe4!? At first I thought this was an enterprising exchange sac but then I looked for alternatives and found no good ones: 9...Re8 20.Ng3 with no good defense to Nh5. At least for the exchange, Black gets two good bishops and unbridled activity against White’s king: 120.Bxe7? They say the best way refute a sacrifice is by accepting it but not here. After 20.Bh4! Qd7 21.Bd5 Nf6 22.Bxf6 gxf6 23.Bxc6 Qxc6 24.Ng3  and White is virtually winning 20...Qxe7 21.Ne3 Qf6 Shirov is slow playing the position but this may be wrong. Equality can be found after 21...Nxf2 22.Kxf2 Qg5 23.Bd5 Qf4+ 24.Kg1 Bxd5 25.Qxd5 Bxe3+ 26.Kh1 Rb8 27.Qc6 Qg3 28.Rxe3 Qxe3 29.Qxc7 Ra8 22.Qe2 g6 This is really, really slow playing this position. He is planning on bringing the rook to the h-file which works in the game but probably shouldn't have. 23.Rf1 Kg7 24.Nd5? Necessary was 24.Bd5 exchanging off that dangerous white squared bishop:…24…Bxd5 25.Nxd5 Qh4 26.Nxc7 Rh8 27.Rad1 Ng3 28.Qd3 Nxf1 29.Rxf1 Qe7 30.Nxa6 with rough equality 24...Qh4 25.Nxc7 25.Nb4 is the last call for salvation when after 25...Bb7 26.Bd5 White can hold for a while 25...Rh8 26.Qf3 f5 Preparing Nxf2. Which right now simply fails to 27.Qxf7+ 27.Kh2 There’s no defense here; for example: 27.Nd5 Ng5 28.Qd3 e4 29.Qd1 Nxh3+ 30.gxh3 Qg3+ 31.Kh1 Rxh3# 27...Nxf2 28.Ne6+ 28.Qxc6 Qf4+ 29.g3 (29.Kg1 Nxh3+ 30.Kh1 Nf2+ 31.Kg1 Rh1#)29...Rxh3+ 30.Kg1 Qxg3+ 31.Qg2 Rh1#; 28.Qg3 Qxg3+ 29.Kxg3 f4+ 30.Kh2 Ng4+ 31.Kh1 Rxh3# 28...Kf6 29.Rxf2 29.Bd5 Bxd5 30.Qxd5 Ng4+ 31.Kh1 Qg3 29...Bxf2 30.Qd3 30.Qxc6 Qg3+ 31.Kh1 Rxh3+ 32.gxh3 Qxh3# 30...Be4 With no good way of keeping her queen and guarding g3, White gave up; 0–1
