KEVIN CULLEN

A police officer’s simple salute recognizes an honorable life

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Kevin Cullen Globe Staff  September 14, 2017

Anne Ciaburri knew her father was dead.

She was 15, a sophomore at Malden High, and she knew, just from the way her mother was crying when she came home from school that bleak winter’s day in 1968.

Her father, Raymond Gignac, was serving with the Army in Vietnam. She didn’t need to read the telegram. She didn’t need to talk to the honor guard at their house. Her mother’s crying told her everything she needed to know.

What made it harder is that Ray Gignac didn’t have to be there. He volunteered. He was a decorated combat veteran of World War II and the Korean War. He had served his country twice over already. But, at 45, he could not sit idly by while American boys were dying.

So he joined them and died 13 days after arriving in Vietnam.

Ray Gignac was 20 when he enlisted in the Army in 1943. A year later, he ran up the beach at Normandy and chased the Nazis across Europe. In Korea, he served with the 25th Infantry Division and earned three Bronze Stars in battle.

In 1967, retired from the Army for two years, Ray Gignac raised his hand again and swore an oath that brought him to Vietnam as an intelligence officer with the 9th Infantry Division. He was just getting his bearings when he suffered a fatal heart attack.

As the war in Vietnam dragged on, and as this country was torn apart, Anne tried to reconcile it all. She was an only child, and losing her father was hard enough. Watching Vietnam veterans return to open scorn left her hurt and confused.

“It was hard to watch the news,” she said, “hard to watch the protests.”

She wrote about her father in a college essay and was stunned when the professor was dismissive. Her father was an honorable man, and his service was dishonored by those who didn’t know him.

She honored her father by becoming a nurse and working with veterans. She spent nearly 30 years at the Soldiers Home in Chelsea, where she and others became families to veterans who had none.

Not long ago, Anne was in a line of traffic on Maplewood Street in Malden, slowed by construction. As her car approached the police officer working the construction detail, the officer noticed her Gold Star Family license plate.

“His whole posture changed,” she said. “His shoulders went back. His head went still, and he raised his right hand in a salute.”

Anne was overwhelmed. When she got home, she couldn’t stop crying.

“It’s hard to explain,” she said. “For a 15-year-old whose world was turned upside down, that officer turned it right side up. To me, it felt like the respect my father’s been waiting for all these years. That officer represented something decent, respecting decent men who died in a war they didn’t start.”

Anne Ciaburri went to the police station and told the desk officer the story. She didn’t know the officer’s name.

His name is Ayrton Borges. He is 23 years old, a Marine Corps veteran, who came to the United States from Brazil when he was 5 and moved to Malden when he was 10.

Borges has been on the job for just two months.

When Malden police Chief Kevin Molis asked to speak with Borges, the rookie cop wondered if, in his inexperience, he had unwittingly done something wrong. Molis shook his hand and told him how proud he was of him.

“I felt like it was a small gesture, that it was no big deal, just the least I could do for a Gold Star family,” Borges said. “But the chief told me it’s the smallest things we do as police officers, respecting the people we serve, that can have the biggest impact.”

It was a simple salute. But it was profound for a whole generation, especially for a woman who always longed not just for the father she lost but the respect he deserved.

Kevin Cullen is a Globe columnist. He can be reached at cullen@globe.com.
