25 years after original Dream Team, the rest of the world has arrived

John Gaps III/Associated Press/File 1992

By Bob Ryan Globe Correspondent  June 17, 2017

There were 113 international players representing 41 countries and territories on Opening Day rosters for the just-concluded NBA season.

I’m sure Boris Stankovic smiled when he was informed of that news tidbit. After all, the Dream Team was his idea.

That’s right, Boris Stankovic. Not David Stern. Not anyone connected with American professional basketball. Not anyone connected with the USOC. The Dream Team incursion into the 1992 Summer Olympics, or as I like to call it, “Beat Me, Whip Me, Take My Picture,” came about because the head man of the organization governing all basketball outside the USA felt the only way for the rest of the world to play at the same level as the best Americans was to, well, play against the best Americans. Let them feel the fury of the fiery dragon.

That was 25 years ago. We have reached the silver anniversary of the event that shook up the basketball world, setting in motion the forces that have created the NBA that annually features more than 100 participants lacking American passports. Only six years before the Dream Team was hatched it was quite the stir when Phoenix employed a solitary 6-foot-8-inch Bulgarian named Georgi Glouchkov, distinguished by the fact that he came to the league directly from European ball, not from an American college.

Among the awed televiewers as the Dream Team won the gold, from the preliminary Tournament of the Americas and the Olympics themselves, by an average score of 122-72 were a 14-year-old Dirk Nowitzki in Wurzburg, West Germany, and a 15-year-old Manu Ginobili in Bahia Blanca, Argentina. From that moment on, each lad had a goal and a game plan. In case they had harbored any illusions, they now knew how high the bar actually was.

Which was?

Which was almost unimaginable. The passing alone was at a level almost beyond description. “People may say it’s overkill and all that,” said assistant coach Mike Krzyzewski, “but there is no way anyone who professes to like basketball couldn’t appreciate that. Anyone who doesn’t isn’t a true basketball fan. You must understand what this team is doing, and its impact on the game. This is history — and we’ll be seeing the benefit of it for the next decade.”

His prescient remarks were illustrated as early as the 2000 Olympics, when Team USA went life and death with Lithuania among some disturbingly close calls. The prediction came true in full force exactly a decade later when Team USA finished sixth in the 2002 World Championships in Indianapolis, losing to Argentina (featuring, yup, Manu Ginobili), Yugoslavia, and Spain.

But I know many Americans still don’t get it. They think we muscled our way into the Barcelona Olympics because we had lost the gold to the Soviet Union four years earlier in Seoul. The fact is that when FIBA, the governing body headed by Mr. Stankovic, took its vote about allowing true open competition, the USA voted against it. Boris Stankovic’s concerns were not our concerns.

The original Dream Team was never about the competition. The entire world knew that and accepted that. “You no win gold?” joked famed Soviet coach Alexander Gomelski to Dream Team coach Chuck Daly. “I give you ticket to Siberia.”

You knew this would be a different type of competition when prior to the first game at the qualifying Tournament of the Americas in Portland, Ore., the Cuban squad requested a joint picture. Then they went out and took their 136-57 beating like men.

Cuban coach Miguel Calderon Gomez explained what it was like for his team to come up against so many extraordinary players at one time. “In Cuba we have a saying. One finger cannot cover the whole sun.”

“I like that,” said Daly. “That’s something I’ll take home with me this week.”

And so it began. There would be daily pregame photos. There would be daily beatdowns. There would be occurrences not normally part of the American basketball experience, such as Argentine guard Marco Milanesi, seeing himself being guarded by Magic Johnson on a switch, frantically signaling to a teammate wielding a movie camera to get the shot.

Back home, many Americans were disgusted. They wanted the college kids. They wanted some, you know, competition. But that was never the point.

“They want us to dunk on them,” Magic declared. And that was the truth.

Brazilian great Oscar Schmidt, who retired having scored a record 49,737 points in national and international competition, had this to say after participating in a 44-point loss in which five of his shots had been blocked: “I loved it. These are my idols. I will remember this game for the rest of my life.”

For the record, the best Dream Teamer on the court was Charles Barkley. He also won gold, silver, and bronze off the court. No one got more out of his Olympic experience than Sir Charles, whose post-midnight perambulations down Barcelona’s La Rambla were adventures in themselves. On the court, his patented coast-to-coast excursions, culminating in thunderous dunks, put fear in the hearts of just about every opponent.

Michael Jordan? There were some flashes of Jordan greatness, those were on days when he limited himself to 36 holes. As for Our Larry, he was dealing with back issues in what he knew would be his final competition ever. But he did have some Bird-like moments, including one memorable performance against Germany.

Their best basketball was never seen by the public. It came in a pair of scrimmages during a pre-Olympic week in Monte Carlo. The first was a 2-pointer and the second was declared a tie by Daly, who said he was fearful “these guys might kill each other.”

“The greatest basketball I’ve ever seen,” insisted Magic. I mean, wow. Think about that.

That sound we heard when they placed the gold medals around the necks of the One and Only Dream Team was the toothpaste exiting the tube, never to return. The World had watched. The World had taken notes. The World was preparing to work on its game.

Twenty-five years later, the World is here, just as Boris Stankovic envisioned.
