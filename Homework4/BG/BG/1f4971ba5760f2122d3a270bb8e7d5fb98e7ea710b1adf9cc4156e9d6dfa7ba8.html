Andris Nelsons is thinking big, kicking off BSO season with Bernstein

By Zoë Madonna Globe Staff  September 15, 2017

LENOX — When he first heard the music of Leonard Bernstein, Boston Symphony Orchestra music director Andris Nelsons was taken by surprise.

“I knew he was a conductor,” the Latvian maestro explained in an interview at Tanglewood in late August. Growing up behind the Iron Curtain, the young Nelsons had had access to recordings of Bernstein conducting the works of composers such as Mahler and Sibelius, but not his own imaginative music.

“We expect that probably he’s writing the same music . . . and then we heard the mambos,” Nelsons recalled with wonder, singing a fragment from “West Side Story,” the first work of Bernstein he remembered hearing. “This man is so witty, so modern, so open to different styles.”

As Nelsons enters his fourth season as music director, he’s making like Bernstein and thinking big. The season begins on Friday with Nelsons conducting an all-Bernstein program hosted by mezzo-soprano Frederica von Stade, featuring BSO principal flutist Elizabeth Rowe and soprano Julia Bullock. It will be the first all-Bernstein evening Nelsons conducts, kicking off a BSO centennial celebration of the famed conductor-composer.

It’s a natural fit for the BSO and Boston’s “native son” composer, notes BSO managing director Mark Volpe. Bernstein, who was born in Lawrence and educated at Harvard University, was a member of the first class of fellows of the Tanglewood Music Center and a protege of BSO music director Serge Koussevitzky; he conducted at Tanglewood and Symphony Hall many times.

“Our most intense connection with Bernstein was at Tanglewood, where his musical life sort of took on momentum,” said Volpe, who hinted at a Bernstein focus for the upcoming summer season. The Bernstein centennial events at Symphony Hall include Nelsons conducting Symphony No. 2, “The Age Of Anxiety,” which was commissioned by Serge Koussevitzky shortly before the end of his 25-year tenure at the helm of the BSO. Jean-Yves Thibaudet, who holds the title of BSO artist-in-residence, will perform the piano solo.

“There’s a real relationship with Bernstein,” said Volpe. “I don’t think he went onstage without Koussevitsky’s cufflinks.” Nelsons does not have any such signature performance rituals, but is fond of a spray of fragrance before going onstage, which he said he chooses based on the mood of the music he’s conducting.

In addition to the Bernstein centennial, this season also marks the first in which Nelsons will serve as both BSO music director and Kapellmeister of the Leipzig Gewandhaus Orchestra. Conductors frequently take contracts with wimultiple orchestras, but rarely do they collaborate and connect like these two orchestras intend to, with activities including this February’s “Leipzig Week in Boston” and a musician exchange program.

“Andris wanted to do this differently,” said Volpe, bringing up the long history and connection between Boston and Leipzig. “I started talking about wouldn’t it be interesting to not have it just be occasionally sharing commissions. . . . What if we put together the two institutions, and shared our histories and composer relationships?”

Volpe surmised that the degree to which the two ensembles will work together is unique among major orchestras that share a music director, and said that “a good 15 players” have expressed interest in temporarily working in Leipzig.

Nelsons said he was happy to be focusing his attention on those two cities, which he calls his “musical homes,” and keeping other engagements to a select few. “There is no more feeling of one week there, then one week there. . . ” he said with visible relief.

That number of engagements reduced by one when earlier this year, he and his wife, soprano Kristine Opolais, dropped out of the Metropolitan Opera’s new production of “Tosca” after tenor Jonas Kaufmann announced his own departure. Nelsons explained that without the original team of principals in place, he did not want to participate.

“I feel very sorry, but . . . I don’t understand the vision of that opera house,” he said. “My whole concentration in America is Boston, and so we can do opera concertante as well.”

Opera in concert has always been a priority for Nelsons at the BSO; past seasons have brought a trio of Strauss operas to the Symphony Hall stage, and this year he conducts the orchestra in Act II of Wagner’s “Tristan and Isolde,” with Kaufmann making his only American operatic appearance this season in his role debut as Tristan, and Camilla Nylund as Isolde. Nelsons’s operatic ambitions continue with a slew of gigantic German language titles: his wish list includes Alban Berg’s modernist monoliths “Wozzeck” and “Lulu.” He said he’d also someday like to conduct Schoenberg’s cantata “Gurre-Lieder” and Messiaen’s “Turangalîla-Symphonie,” another Koussevitsky commission for the BSO. Each is a piece of gargantuan scale, clocking in at over 75 minutes.

‘Our most intense connection with Bernstein was at Tanglewood, where his musical life sort of took on momentum.’

What other ambitions does he have? Earlier this year, he played the trumpet in public for the first time in 15 years with a performance on WGBH’s “Greater Boston,” and brought his horn again to perform with the Tanglewood Music Center Orchestra in their July kickoff concert. Now, he’s seeking to pick up another old hobby: tae kwon do, which he gave up at age 20 after an injury. He said he has been doing exercises on his own and is now looking for a teacher of the school in which he trained. Whether or not he’ll be showing off that skill onstage is unconfirmed.

BOSTON SYMPHONY ORCHESTRA

Season starts Friday at Symphony Hall, 6 p.m. 888-266-1200, www.bso.org
