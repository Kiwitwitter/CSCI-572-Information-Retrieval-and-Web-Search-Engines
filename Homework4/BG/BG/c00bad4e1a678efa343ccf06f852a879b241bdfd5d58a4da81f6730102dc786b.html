How to take better Instagram food photos

By Jaclyn Reiss Globe Staff  August 08, 2017

It’s the ultimate social media share: A beautifully arranged plate of cookies. A colorful basket of fruits. A smoothie that has reached that perfect berry color.

It seems that many people can’t take a bite out of that home-made fritatta or perfectly browned crème brûlée without first snapping a photo for all to salivate over on Instagram.

Advertisement

But if you’re one of the many who has yet to reach Instagram pro status when it comes to food sharing, here are some tips from Globe photographers on how to make that caprese salad worthy of photographing for all to see.

LIGHTING

The most important element by far in photographing a beautiful meal? Lighting. Kim Chapin, the Globe’s deputy director of photography, suggests trying to take a photo near a window so that natural light can stream onto your masterpiece. In a restaurant? Be bold and ask for that window seat — your followers will thank you later.

SUBJECT MATTER

There’s no easy way to put this: If your stew looks sludgey and monotone, it probably won’t make for an enticing photo.

Advertisement

Foods that feature a riot of colors will start you off with an interesting subject matter, and some additional prepping can make it all the better.

“Place contrasting colors of food next to each other — greens next to yellows, etc.,” said Lloyd Young, picture editor for the Globe.

Simplicity is also key, he said: “Don’t try to shoot too much in the image.”

FRAMING

The way you frame your shot, and where you shoot it from, can tell a drastically different story of the same meal, according to Lane Turner, assistant chief photographer at the Globe.

“Sometimes shooting straight down at something is a really cool,” Turner said. “If it’s a graphically simple piece of food, shooting straight across at plate level can be really dramatic. You really have to evaluate what you’re shooting.”

Young had a bit of different advice: “Try shooting from the same visual perspective that you eat your food from.”

Turner also repeated a mantra many photographers know: “If your pictures aren’t good enough, get closer.”

BACKGROUND

Depending on what kind of food you have, a light-colored plate or background can make your meal really pop: “Your food will stand out, especially if it’s colorful,” Chapin says.

However, Turner says the main rule is to make sure your background is simple.

“A white plate or background can be distraction it depends on what the food is and where the light’s coming from,” he said. “A really nice dark earth tone ceramic bowl can be a wonderful thing to shoot food in.”

And sometimes, of course, the background itself is worth sharing.

STYLING

Former Globe food editor Sheryl Julian recommends museum wax — “the greatest stuff in the world — for true diehard food stylists to affix props, such as a fork to a plate. She also recommends using something small to prop up the back of the plate.

To manipulate light, Julian recommends using foam boards or reflective discs to add more light.

Leaning your elbows on a table can also help stabilize shake iPhone shots, she said, and always make sure to crop out table edges before posting your final product.

And when it comes to Instagram, don’t be afraid to go #nofilter.

“I don’t like all the filters,” Julian said. “They just make food look terrible.”

SATURATION

Saturation — a fancy photography term that refers to the intensity of a hue — is one way to pick up some major color points in a photo.

“Saturation can make food look more appealing,” Turner said. “If there’s a way to increase saturation during your original capture or in the post, that can help.”

SHORT VIDEOS

Who said Instagram is only about still images? The app also supports videos and short clips, which can be conducive to capturing the way a particular food may move. Swirling a soup? Shaking some jello? Dressing a salad? Try making a short video clip with an app like Boomerang, which takes short few-second clips and integrates seamlessly with Instagram.

PRACTICE MAKES PERFECT

No one became a photography pro overnight. Keep practicing with different angles, light, subject matter, backgrounds, and filers to find a method that works for you.
