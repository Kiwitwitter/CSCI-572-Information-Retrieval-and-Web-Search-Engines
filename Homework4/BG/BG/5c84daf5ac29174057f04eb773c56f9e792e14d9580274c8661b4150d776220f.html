Mueller was in no rush to get to Broadway; now she seems destined to stay

The Boston Globe Tweet Share

By Christopher Wallenberg Globe Correspondent  August 31, 2017

Six years ago, Jessie Mueller wasn’t sure if she was ready to make a leap of faith.

Before she became a major Broadway star, she had started to carve out a career in the musical theater scene in her hometown of Chicago, getting to spread her wings and hone her craft while playing plum parts such as Amalia in “She Loves Me” and Carrie Pipperidge in “Carousel.”

Advertisement

Still, Mueller began to feel restless and ready for a change, but was initially hesitant about making the jump to New York — even after she landed a part in the 2011 Broadway revival of “On a Clear Day You Can See Forever,” headlined by Harry Connick Jr. But her mother urged her to just focus on the work and “do the gig.”

Several years later, she became the toast of Broadway for playing beloved pop singer-songwriter Carole King in the bio-musical “Beautiful,” for which she won a Tony. In 2016, she nabbed another Tony nomination, her third, for creating the part of the plucky pie baker with an abusive husband in the Sara Bareilles musical “Waitress,” which premiered at the American Repertory Theater in Cambridge. By that point, Mueller’s star was fully ascendant. She’ll be back on Broadway next spring as Julie Jordan in a hotly anticipated revival of Rodgers and Hammerstein’s “Carousel.” She’ll also be making her film debut in 2018 in the Steven Spielberg drama “The Post,” alongside Tom Hanks, Meryl Streep, and Sarah Paulson.

This weekend, fans can catch Mueller at Town Hall in Provincetown on Sunday as part of a Broadway concert series, accompanied by pianist and host Seth Rudetsky. Mueller, 34, chatted by phone from her home in New York.

Q. Can you tease any of the songs you’ll be singing in Provincetown?

A. I think we’re going to do some stuff from “Beautiful,” probably some stuff from “Waitress.” Any time I’m asked to sing something from “Beautiful,” I feel grateful that I can say, “Oh sure, I’d love to sing that Carole King song!” It’s music that you don’t get sick of. And I always find myself gravitating toward standards. I don’t know if they’re in my wheelhouse or it’s just my comfort zone. I’ve always loved the song “It Might as Well Be Spring” [from the 1945 Rodgers and Hammerstein movie musical “State Fair”]. And some of the songs I got to sing in “Nice Work If You Can Get It,” like “Someone to Watch Over Me.”

Q. You grew up in a family of theater professionals in Chicago. Was it inevitable that you were going to become a performer?

A. I don’t know, I assume it was kind of both nature and nurture. I was certainly exposed to it at an early age, which I’m so grateful for because I was a shy kid, and I wonder if I ever would have found it. It was a world that I had a tangible grasp on, so I was always drawn to it. But then as I got older, I struggled with whether or not it was something I really wanted to pursue as a career because I had somewhat of a realistic view of what a life in the arts might look like, and I knew it wasn’t always rosy [laughs].

Q. After graduating from Syracuse University, you moved back to Chicago and started working in the theater scene there, and you got to play some really meaty roles.

A. I did. For me, it was the best thing I could have done because I think people let me stretch myself there and were imaginative in their casting. I remember my first gig, just being very quiet and doing a lot of watching [laughs]. Because there were all these people who I had seen as a kid going to shows that were like the ultimate actors in my mind, and I couldn’t believe I was in a rehearsal room with them. So I felt like I was getting the insider story watching them work and then getting to learn from them.

Q. The journey you took probably really prepared you for the pressures and competitiveness of working on Broadway.

A. I think I would have been absolutely destroyed if I had come here any sooner than I did. It gave me time to find my own footing and learn about myself as an artist, and find what works for me and what doesn’t work for me.

Q. You got cast in “On a Clear Day You Can See Forever” on Broadway in 2011 and scored a Tony nomination for your performance. How did you finally decide to make that leap to New York?

A. Looking back now, I was having one of those moments of looking at my life and thinking, you know: Well, where am I? Where do I want to be? But I didn’t plan it. Then all of a sudden I was moving to New York to start rehearsals, and I couldn’t wrap my head around that. I was freaking out a little bit, and my mom was like, “Take it one step at a time. You got a gig, and it’s a great gig. So go do the gig.” After the show closed, I stayed and kept auditioning, and lo and behold, six years later, I’m still here.

Q. What do you remember most fondly about your time trying out “Waitress” at the ART?

A. I loved being in Cambridge. I think about it often. It was this great little capsule of time to me. It was creative and hard, but that’s what happens when you’re trying to build a new musical. You’re trying things out and trying to find out what works for a character. The Cambridge run was absolutely essential to me in informing who I created Jenna to be in my mind and my heart.

Q. Next spring, you’ll be playing Julie Jordan in “Carousel” on Broadway. What do you love about getting to work on that show again?

A. It has one of the most gorgeous, lush scores in the musical theater canon, and I think it’s a show that’s underrated. Some people kind of pooh-pooh it, and I’m not sure why. So I hope we can convert some people with this production. I’m excited to explore it and break it open and allow a new generation to discover it. The show has such heart, and it’s a piece about forgiveness and how we choose to spend the time we have on this earth and how we choose to treat people while we’re here. So we couldn’t be exploring a better message right now.

Q. Do you have any advice for aspiring young actors that you want to share?

A. The thing I try to tell people who are just starting in the business is to listen to yourself, trust yourself, and be kind to yourself. And do the work to cultivate who you are and what your point of view is. Don’t try to be like anybody else. That’s what will make you an interesting artist. This business is
crazy-pants. We flit in and out of these fantasy worlds and these intense periods of work and camaraderie, and then it goes away and you do it all over again. So you have to continue that conversation and ask yourself: What do I want? What satisfies me? Because the work will come and go, but you’re always going to be left with yourself. So you have to continue to cultivate that sense of who you are without the work.

JESSIE MUELLER: Broadway@Town Hall

With Seth Rudetsky. At Provincetown Town Hall, Sept. 3 at 6:30 p.m. Tickets $50-$150, www.brownpapertickets.com
