SJC considers appeal to raise state charter school cap

By James Vaznis Globe Staff  October 02, 2017

The Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court is wading into the thorny debate about whether the state’s limits on the number of charter schools that can operate is unconstitutional, giving charter-school advocates some hope for renewed momentum in a movement that suffered a crushing defeat at last year’s ballot box.

Attorneys representing five students who failed to gain admission to charter schools and are now in low-performing schools in Boston attempted to sway the seven-member panel during a Monday hearing to move forward with the case. They argued the state limits on the number of charter schools that can operate are arbitrary and prevent too many students from getting a better education.

“This is a policy allowing thousands of students to languish in failing schools,” said Kevin Martin, an attorney with Goodwin Proctor, who is representing the students.

The attorneys filed an appeal with the state’s highest court after Suffolk Court Associate Justice, Heidi E. Brieger, dismissed the lawsuit last October, ruling that the state has a right to protect the financial interests of traditional school systems.

A month after her ruling, voters overwhelmingly rejected a state ballot measure that would have accelerated the growth of charter schools statewide and would have effectively rendered the state’s cap on growth irrelevant.

The state has several different caps on charter school growth. But the one that is most often in play is tied to how much of a community’s overall spending on education can go toward funding charter schools, which are independent public institutions approved by the state even if local officials object.

In most cases, no more than 9 percent of a community’s overall spending on education can be diverted to charter schools. However, under state law, that amount can be as much as 18 percent in Boston and other communities where MCAS scores rank in the bottom 10 percent statewide.

Advertisement

Opponents of charter schools have long argued that the institutions drain money from local school systems. But charter school advocates counter that school systems don’t need that money because they are no longer educating those students.

During Monday’s hearing, one justice referenced the unsuccessful ballot measure — calling it “the elephant in the room” — suggesting that voters believe the caps are rational and not arbitrary.

Melissa Allison, an attorney with the NAACP and the firm Anderson & Kreiger, contended that the elimination of the caps would come at the detriment of the state’s most disadvantaged students who largely attend traditional schools, which could ultimately be forced to make budget cuts if more money is pumped into an expanded pool of charter schools.

“The cap on the number and funding [of charter schools] is well suited to ensuring traditional public schools are adequately funded,” she said.

The lawsuit names state Education Secretary James Peyser and education board chairman Paul Sagan as defendants. That has created a politically awkward situation for them as well as Governor Charlie Baker, all of whom support the opening of more charter schools, but have been forced to defend the charter-school caps.

In fact, Sagan has been under fire recently from teacher unions and other groups for donating more than a half million dollars last year to two organizations pushing for passage of the ballot question.

The attorney general’s office nevertheless has defended the caps and noted during Monday’s hearing that the state Legislature has repeatedly raised the caps since charter schools were created under the 1993 Education Reform Act.

The justices will decide in the comings weeks or months whether to uphold the lower court’s decision or reverse it and send it back to the lower court for pre-trial discovery.
